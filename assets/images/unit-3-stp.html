Overview

The $150 Million Misread

In 1996, McDonald's bet roughly $150 million on a burger called the Arch Deluxe. The marketing was a deliberate repositioning play: McDonald's wanted to be seen as a place adults went for grown-up taste, not just somewhere parents brought kids for Happy Meals. The campaign featured children making sour faces at the new burger while sophisticated adults enjoyed it. The tagline, in effect: this one's not for the kids.

The Arch Deluxe was dead inside two years. Adults who wanted a grown-up burger were not going to McDonald's to get it. Families who did go to McDonald's were not interested in being told the new burger wasn't for their kids. The brand had two targets — the one it had, and the one it wanted — and the campaign managed to alienate the first without converting the second.

The Arch Deluxe is the most famous example of a failure that is more common than the case studies suggest. Every dollar of media spend behind a campaign rests on three earlier decisions: which groups of customers exist in this market, which one of them we are going to chase, and what we want to mean to that group relative to everyone else competing for the same attention. Get the targeting wrong and the creative does not save you. Get the positioning wrong and the media plan does not save you. Get the segmentation wrong and you are not even in the right argument.

Tropicana Redesign
2009 · Lost ~$30M in 2 months
Replaced the iconic orange-with-a-straw carton with a glass of juice on a flat field. Loyalists could no longer find the brand on the shelf. Sales dropped 20% in eight weeks. Tropicana reverted the design.
Crystal Pepsi
1992 · Pulled in 18 months
Pepsi launched a clear cola for health-conscious '90s consumers. The problem: people who wanted cola wanted it to taste and look like cola. People who wanted "clear and pure" weren't buying soda in the first place.
Coors Rocky Mountain Sparkling Water
1990 · Withdrawn within 2 years
Coors put a beer brand on bottled water. The targeting was sound — premium water was a growing category — but the brand's beer DNA made the water feel wrong to water buyers and irrelevant to Coors drinkers.

Each of these brands had access to the same data the rest of their industries had. None of them was short on creative talent or media budget. The failure point in every case was upstream: the marketers picked the wrong target, or they picked the right target but tried to occupy a position the brand had no permission to hold. Segmentation, targeting, and positioning — STP — is the discipline that exists to stop this from happening.

The operating warning
The most common failure in marketing isn't picking the wrong target. It's skipping segmentation entirely, picking a target out of intuition, and discovering — usually after the launch budget is spent — that the "target" was three different groups in a trench coat, each wanting something the others didn't.

The Three Letters, in One Breath

Before we go deeper, the working definitions:

  • Segmentation is the act of dividing a market into distinct groups whose members share needs, behaviors, or characteristics that make them likely to respond similarly to a marketing program.
  • Targeting is the choice of which of those groups the firm will actually pursue — and, implicitly, which it will not.
  • Positioning is the deliberate construction of a brand's meaning in the chosen target's mind, in comparison to what they already think about competing brands.

The order matters. You cannot position to a target you have not chosen. You cannot choose a target without first seeing the segments. The discipline runs in one direction, even though in practice the data flows in loops.

The Four-Card Roadmap

The marketing literature, most cleanly in Belch's Advertising and Promotion, frames STP as a four-step process. We'll use this as the spine of the unit:

Step 1
Identify Markets With Unfulfilled Needs
Spot the opportunity. Where are buyers underserved? Where is demand growing? What gaps do competitors leave open?
Step 2
Determine Market Segmentation
Divide the market into groups using demographic, geographic, psychographic, or behavioral characteristics. Build profiles.
Step 3
Select a Market to Target
Evaluate segments. Choose how many to enter and which ones offer the best fit between the firm's capabilities and the segment's potential.
Step 4
Position Through Marketing Strategies
Decide what the brand will mean to the chosen target. Coordinate every element of the marketing mix to deliver and reinforce that meaning.

Steps 1 and 2 collapse in practice — you identify markets by segmenting them — so the rest of this unit covers them together. Each remaining card gets its own section.

01
Segmentation

How Marketers Cut the Market

Markets, in their natural state, are not groups. They are crowds. A "market for athletic shoes" is not a thing you can market to — it's millions of people who happen to share the loose attribute of buying athletic footwear at some frequency, and who otherwise want, value, and respond to different things. The first job of segmentation is to find the seams in that crowd: the natural fault lines where one group's needs diverge enough from another's that a single message stops working for both.

The reason this matters operationally is that every promotional dollar has a denominator. If your campaign reaches a hundred people and ninety of them have no use for your product, you have spent ninety percent of the budget on irrelevant impressions. Segmentation is what lets you raise the hit rate.

A campaign built on age and ZIP code is cheap and quick to assemble; it is also working with the equivalent of a customer's outline. A campaign built on what the customer has actually bought, browsed, and abandoned over the last six months is closer to a portrait — but getting that data ethically, storing it responsibly, and using it without creeping the customer out is the harder discipline.

1.2 Demographic Segmentation

Demographics are the most common base of segmentation, the easiest to defend in a meeting, and the most likely to mislead. The reason they mislead is that demographic categories are correlated with consumer behavior but rarely the cause of it. Two women in their thirties with the same household income can have completely different brand preferences. Demographics tell you who the customer is on paper; they rarely tell you what they want. Netflix has publicly stated that demographic data is "almost useless" to them compared to viewing behavior — knowing a household contains a 34-year-old woman in Atlanta tells them almost nothing; knowing she binged the first season of Wednesday in three nights tells them everything.

That said, demographics are not worthless. They remain useful when (a) the product is genuinely demographic-bound — diapers, retirement planning, AARP — or (b) they are combined with other bases to sharpen a profile. The mistake is to treat demographics as sufficient.

One demographic dimension worth pausing on is generation. Millennials (born roughly 1982–1996) and Gen Z (born roughly 1997–2012) are routinely treated as a single "young" segment, and they are not. Gen Z is more skeptical of brand promises, more influenced by friends and creators than by celebrities, less brand-loyal, and more likely to expect brands to take positions on social issues. A campaign that lumps them in with older millennials and uses the same channels and tone is targeting two segments with one budget — the Arch Deluxe error in miniature.

1.3 Geographic and Geodemographic Segmentation

Where people live shapes what they buy. The obvious cases are physical: snowblowers in Minnesota, sunscreen in Florida, four-wheel drive in mountain regions. The less obvious cases are cultural: certain soft-drink brands have built durable regional cult followings — Cheerwine in the Carolinas, Vernors in Michigan, Moxie in New England, Big Red across the South — that don't translate when the brand tries to go national. The Cheerwine slogan is literally "The South's Unique Cherry Soft Drink." Geography is the segmentation; the rest of the brand is built on top of it.

Geodemographic segmentation extends this idea by bundling geographic location with demographic and lifestyle characteristics. The premise: people who live in similar neighborhoods tend to share similar buying patterns. Several companies sell pre-built versions of this analysis. Esri's Tapestry Segmentation divides U.S. neighborhoods into more than 60 distinct lifestyle clusters with names like Soccer Moms, Laptops and Lattes, Salt of the Earth, and Heartland Communities. Nielsen's PRIZM does the same with 66 clusters bearing names like Bohemian Mix, Suburban Pioneers, and Country Casuals.

1.4 Psychographic Segmentation

Psychographics are an attempt to segment by why rather than who. Instead of grouping people by age or income, psychographic systems group them by what they value, what they do with their time, and how they see themselves. The technical term for the underlying data is AIO — activities, interests, opinions.

The best-known commercial psychographic system is VALS, developed by Strategic Business Insights. VALS sorts U.S. adults into eight segments based on two underlying dimensions: their primary motivation (whether they're driven by ideals, by achievement, or by self-expression) and the resources they bring to acting on that motivation (high vs. low). Segments include Innovators, Thinkers, Achievers, Experiencers, and four others. Marketers use VALS not to identify customers one at a time but to design messages, creative, and media plans that resonate with the motivational profile of the chosen target.

The point of psychographics is that two people who look identical demographically — same age, same income, same ZIP code — can have completely different orientations to brands. One sees a luxury car as a reward for achievement; the other sees the same car as a statement of identity. The advertising that lands for one won't necessarily land for the other, even though the demographic profile is the same.

1.5 Behavioral Segmentation: The Highest-Resolution Lens

Behavioral segmentation groups customers by what they have actually done — what they've bought, how often, how much, whether they're loyal, and which benefits they appear to be seeking. It is the most predictive of the four bases because past behavior is the single best predictor of future behavior in almost every consumer category.

Four behavioral cuts are worth knowing.

Usage rate. Customers in any category split into heavy, medium, and light users — and the split is rarely even. The pattern is so consistent across industries that it has its own name: the 80/20 rule, sometimes called the Pareto principle. Roughly 20% of a brand's customers tend to drive roughly 80% of its volume. In some categories the skew is steeper. The implication for marketing is that a campaign aimed at "everyone in the category" will mostly miss the people who actually matter. A targeted effort at heavy users — keeping them, deepening their relationship, preventing defection — is often the highest-leverage move a brand can make.

Loyalty status. Within any product category, customers fall into four loose loyalty bands. Hard-core loyals buy one brand all the time. Split loyals rotate between two or three brands they consider acceptable substitutes. Shifting loyals move from one brand to another over time. Switchers have no loyalty at all and buy whatever's on sale. Each group requires a different message. Hard-core loyals need reinforcement and retention. Split loyals can be moved up to hard-core with the right relationship investment. Shifting loyals respond to product innovation and competitive comparisons. Switchers respond mostly to price — and chasing them with brand-building messaging is usually a waste.

User status. Closely related but distinct: customers in a category can be non-users, ex-users, potential users, first-time users, or regular users. The messaging required to convert a non-user is completely different from the messaging required to win back an ex-user, which is different again from what a regular user needs in order to stay regular.

Benefit segmentation. A behavioral cut that focuses on why customers bought. Two people who buy the same toothpaste may want different things from it — one wants whitening, one wants cavity protection, one wants fresh breath, one is buying for sensitive teeth. The product is identical; the segment is not. Crest, Colgate, and Sensodyne each carry multiple SKUs aimed at different benefit segments within what looks from the outside like one category.

Behavioral data is the closest thing marketing has to a portrait of the actual customer. It is also the data customers are least comfortable knowing you have collected.

1.6 Behavioral Targeting Online — and What's Changing

The internet made behavioral segmentation operational at scale. Cookies, pixels, retargeting tags, and the entire programmatic-advertising stack exist to identify a customer by what they have just done — visited this page, abandoned this cart, watched this video — and serve them an ad calibrated to that behavior within seconds. This is why digital became the most efficient direct-response channel ever built, and it is also what triggered the regulatory and platform-led backlash now reshaping the field.

Apple's App Tracking Transparency, Google's deprecation of third-party cookies, EU and California privacy regulations, and growing consumer discomfort with feeling watched have all chipped away at the cleanest implementations. Marketers in 2026 are spending real money rebuilding measurement and targeting systems that used to be free. The strategic implication: behavioral targeting remains the most predictive base of segmentation, but obtaining the behavior is no longer frictionless. First-party data — what customers do on a brand's own properties — has become more valuable than ever. Third-party behavioral data has become less reliable.

1.7 Combining Bases

In practice, useful segments are almost always built from more than one base. A ski-equipment brand isn't targeting "people who ski" — that's a category, not a segment. The segment might be: 25–45 years old (demographic), household income above $90,000 (demographic), located within 200 miles of a major mountain (geographic), active outdoor lifestyle and identifies with peak performance (psychographic), and has purchased technical equipment within the last three seasons (behavioral). Each base sharpens the picture. None of them alone would do.

The temptation is to keep adding layers until the segment is so specific it contains six people. The discipline is to stop layering at the point where the segment is sharp enough to design for but still large enough to be worth designing for. Which brings us to the next card.

02
Targeting

Choosing Whom to Chase

Segmentation produces a map. Targeting is the choice of which territory to enter. The two are linked but distinct, and one of the most common mistakes new marketers make is to collapse them — to skip the segmentation work entirely and "target" a vague group like "younger consumers" or "the premium segment" without ever doing the analysis that would have revealed what those phrases actually contain. You cannot target what you have not first segmented. Or, more precisely: you can, but you'll be guessing, and the cost of being wrong will be visible only after the launch.

2.1 Three Coverage Strategies

Once a market has been segmented, a firm faces a structural choice about how many segments to enter and with what kind of marketing mix. The literature settles on three options.

Undifferentiated
One mix for everyone
Ignore segment differences. Offer one product with one message to the entire market. Cheap to execute. Effective only when the market is genuinely homogeneous, which it almost never is.
Example: Early Coca-Cola — one bottle, one taste, one ad, the whole world. Largely abandoned as a strategy in most consumer categories.
Differentiated
Different mix per segment
Enter multiple segments with a separate marketing mix tailored to each. Higher cost, higher coverage, requires the organization to keep multiple positioning lines straight without contaminating each other.
Example: Marriott runs Ritz-Carlton, JW Marriott, Courtyard, Residence Inn, Fairfield, and Moxy as separate brands, each pitched at a different traveler segment and price point.
Concentrated
Go all-in on one
Pick a single segment and dominate it. Lower revenue ceiling, but the strategy lets a small firm punch above its weight by being the obvious choice for its chosen group.
Example: Tesla in its first decade — luxury electric vehicles for early-adopter affluent buyers, no economy model, no hybrid hedge. The Roadster and Model S were not for everyone.

2.2 The Five Tests

Not every segment a marketer can identify is one worth pursuing. The standard checklist from the marketing literature is five tests. A segment that fails any of them is probably not a viable target, regardless of how interesting it looks on the slide.

  1. Measurable. Can the segment's size, purchasing power, and key characteristics actually be quantified? If you cannot count the segment, you cannot plan for it, budget for it, or report on it.
  2. Substantial. Is the segment large enough — or profitable enough per customer — to justify a separate marketing effort? Niche is fine. Tiny is not.
  3. Accessible. Can the firm reach the segment through available media, channels, and distribution? A perfectly defined segment that lives outside your media plan and your retail footprint is a segment for someone else.
  4. Differentiable. Does the segment respond differently from other segments to the marketing mix? If two "segments" want the same product, at the same price, through the same channel, with the same message — they are one segment.
  5. Actionable. Does the firm actually have the resources, capabilities, and organizational fit to design and execute a program for this segment? A target you cannot serve well is worse than no target at all.

2.3 From Segment to Persona

The five tests give you a target. They do not yet give you a target that feels like a person. And it turns out that the more concrete the target feels in the heads of the people designing the work — the strategists, the creative team, the media buyers — the better the work tends to be.

This is where personas come in. A persona is a semi-fictional representation of a target customer, constructed from the segmentation data but rendered as an individual. Instead of describing the target as "women 30–45, household income $75K+, suburban, two children, college-educated, value-conscious but brand-aware" — which is a bullet list — the persona has a name, a face, a job, a daily routine, a set of frustrations, and a relationship to the product category. Sarah Chen, 38, dental hygienist in suburban Denver, married with two kids in elementary school, listens to true-crime podcasts on her commute, shops at Target because it's faster than Whole Foods, frustrated that her current toothpaste hasn't helped her sensitivity.

Personas are fashionable, and like all fashionable tools they get over-applied. Used badly, they become an exercise in creative writing that distracts from the underlying segmentation. Used well, they do something the bullet list cannot: they give the team a coherent person to design for. It is easier to imagine what would make Sarah Chen's day better than to imagine what would make "women 30–45, HHI $75K+" feel anything at all.

A persona is not a replacement for segmentation. It is the segmentation made human enough to design against. The segment is the math. The persona is the muse.

The format is flexible — you'll see them called marketing personas, buyer personas, donor personas, candidate personas, shopper personas, lead personas, and a dozen other names depending on the context — but the underlying move is the same: take a segment, make it specific enough to imagine, and design for the imagined person.

What you're looking at. Some of this data appears to have been collected quantitatively — numbers, scales, rankings of motivations or technology proficiency. Some is collected qualitatively — quotes, interviews, descriptions of frustrations and goals. The best personas combine both, and the final panel (a cartoonist's gentle skewering) is a reminder that personas done badly become caricatures that get filed away rather than used.

The most instructive real-world case of persona-driven targeting comes from Harley-Davidson. Most large consumer brands rent their segmentation from systems like Claritas or Esri Tapestry — buy the cluster file, map the customer base, target accordingly. Harley-Davidson does not. The company has, for roughly four decades, built its marketing around seven internally developed customer personas that it has refined through its own ethnographic research, owner-group meetings, and rider events. The personas don't map cleanly onto any commercial segmentation system, and Harley believes that's the point.

85%
The myth-buster. Most people picture a Harley rider as either a wealthy "Rich Urban Biker" (RUB) or a leather-clad outlaw. Combined, those two segments are only 15% of actual Harley owners. The other 85% are the riders almost no outside observer would think to put on the bike.

What this list reveals — and what no commercial cluster file would have given Harley — is that the brand's two most visible rider types account for only 15% of its actual buyers. A marketing program built around the cultural stereotype would have spoken loudly to the smallest segments while ignoring the Pragmatists, Campers, and Loners who together make up 70% of the customer base. The off-the-shelf segmentation would have produced ads for the Harley nobody actually rides.

The lesson for smaller brands is not that everyone should refuse commercial segmentation systems. It's that commercial systems should be treated as starting points, not destinations. A pre-built cluster will tell you what an average resident of a neighborhood looks like. Only your own data, your own customers, and your own attention will tell you what your customer actually is.

03
Positioning

Owning a Place in the Customer's Mind

Once a target has been chosen, the firm faces its final and most consequential strategic decision: what does the brand mean to that target, in comparison to every other brand competing for the same attention? This is positioning. It is the one element of marketing strategy that lives entirely in the customer's head, which makes it both the hardest to control and the one that decides whether everything else works.

3.1 The Definition Worth Memorizing

The cleanest definition comes from Jack Trout and Al Ries, who introduced the term to American marketing in the 1970s:

Positioning is not what you do to a product. Positioning is what you do to the mind of the prospect. It is how you differentiate yourself in the mind of your prospect.
— Al Ries and Jack Trout, Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind

Two things in that quote are doing the work. The first is the location: positioning happens in the prospect's mind, not in the product, the ad, or the brand book. The second is the structural requirement: positioning is comparative. A brand cannot be positioned in isolation; it can only be positioned relative to what the customer already thinks about other brands in the category. Volvo is positioned as safe only because the customer's mental category for cars already contains brands the customer associates with performance, luxury, or fuel economy. Strip away the comparison and "safe" stops meaning anything.

Two implications follow. First, positioning is harder to change than to establish, because changing it requires altering what the customer already believes — and people are bad at updating beliefs about brands they've held for years. Second, every brand is positioned somewhere whether the firm intends it or not. The choice is whether to direct the positioning or to inherit whatever the customer assembles on their own.

3.2 Seven Approaches to Positioning

The marketing literature, most thoroughly in Aaker, Shansby, and Myers, identifies seven distinct approaches a brand can take to establish its position. Most successful brands use more than one, but one tends to dominate.

1
By Attribute or Benefit Set the brand apart on a specific feature or benefit that competitors don't credibly own. Volvo (safety) · Apple (design simplicity) · 5-hour ENERGY (sugar-free quick energy)
2
By Price / Quality Position at the top of the price ladder as a quality signal, or at a favorable price for the quality offered. Rolex (premium quality at premium price) · Kohl's (good value at competitive price)
3
By Use or Application Associate the brand with a specific occasion or use case, sometimes inventing one to expand the category. Arm & Hammer (baking → fridge deodorizer → carpet refresh → toothpaste) · Gatorade (during athletic performance)
4
By Product Class Position against a different category entirely to redefine what the brand competes with. Amtrak (vs. airlines, not other trains) · Liquid Death (canned water positioned against soda and energy drinks, not bottled water)
5
By Product User Define the brand by the kind of person who uses it. The user imagery does the positioning. Ralph Lauren (aspirational lifestyle) · Patagonia (the environmentally serious outdoorsperson) · Stanley tumblers (post-2020, the Gen Z hydration-as-identity buyer)
6
By Competitor Position explicitly against a named or unnamed competitor. Risky, but powerful when the comparison is favorable and credible. Avis ("We try harder" — vs. Hertz) · Apple "Get a Mac" (vs. PC) · Samsung "It doesn't take a genius" (vs. Apple)
7
By Cultural Symbol Anchor the brand in a memorable character or icon that carries the meaning across decades. Tony the Tiger (60+ years) · The Geico Gecko · The Pillsbury Doughboy · Duolingo's owl (the modern reinvention)

3.3 A Second Lens: Functional, Symbolic, Experiential

The seven approaches describe how to position. A complementary framework, from Park, Jaworski, and MacInnis, describes what kind of need the position appeals to. Brands satisfy three types of needs:

  • Functional needs — practical problems to be solved. Crocs are lightweight, comfortable, and easy to clean. Tide gets stains out. The positioning answers "does this work?"
  • Symbolic needs — identity, belonging, status, self-expression. Rolex is not primarily about telling time; it tells the world (and the wearer) something about the wearer. The positioning answers "what does owning this say about me?"
  • Experiential needs — sensory pleasure, variety, stimulation. Dove chocolate is "a chocolate experience like no other." A theme park sells the day, not the rides. The positioning answers "how does this feel?"

Most brands carry traces of all three, but successful positioning emphasizes one. Trying to be functional, symbolic, and experiential at once produces messaging the customer cannot summarize back to you — which is a working definition of weak positioning.

3.4 Repositioning: The Old Spice Case

Repositioning is what a brand does when its current position has stopped working — when the target has aged out, when the category has shifted, when a competitor has annexed the ground the brand used to own, or when the brand has simply become irrelevant to the people the firm needs to sell to. It is among the hardest things a brand can do, because it requires altering customer perceptions that have been forming for decades.

The textbook case from the last twenty years is Old Spice. Before 2010, Old Spice was a 75-year-old men's grooming brand that consumers reliably associated with grandfathers. The product was real, distribution was strong, and the brand was dying. Younger men were not buying it, younger women weren't recommending it for their husbands and boyfriends, and the category was being eaten by Axe, Dove Men+Care, and newer entrants.

In February 2010, Old Spice launched "The Man Your Man Could Smell Like" with a 30-second Super Bowl spot. The actor was Isaiah Mustafa, a former NFL practice-squad player with no particular fame. The agency was Wieden+Kennedy. The strategic insight was that women influence men's grooming purchases — so the ad spoke directly to women, in a tone of absurd hyper-masculine confidence, about what their man could smell like.

The execution worked on every level a campaign can. The spot went viral within days. Wieden+Kennedy followed it with a real-time social-media response campaign that summer, in which Mustafa recorded short personalized video replies to tweets and Facebook comments — nearly 200 of them in two days — which became one of the most studied case studies in digital advertising. Old Spice body wash sales reportedly grew 107% in the first three months of the campaign. The brand's average buyer age dropped, category share rebounded, and the cultural meaning of "Old Spice" shifted from your grandfather's drugstore aftershave to the brand that did the funniest commercial of the year.

What made Old Spice work, and what makes most repositioning attempts fail, is that the campaign did not ask consumers to update a single belief. It gave them a new memory to attach to the brand entirely. The old position wasn't argued against — it was made irrelevant by the volume and freshness of the new one.

Sidebar: Buick's Quieter Repositioning

When you don't have a Super Bowl moment, you grind

Not every repositioning gets a viral campaign. Buick spent most of the 2010s trying to shed the same "old person's car" perception that haunted Oldsmobile before it went out of business in 2004. The average age of a Buick owner in 2008 was 63. By 2018 it had dropped to 58 — five years of progress that took five years to make.

The effort included new product (the Enclave SUV, the Encore crossover, the Cascada convertible — Buick's first convertible in 25 years), a redesigned dealer experience, and a long-running ad campaign called "That's a Buick?" that explicitly mocked the brand's own outdated image. Younger and older actors saw new Buicks and refused to believe they were Buicks. The campaign ran for more than five years without changing its premise.

The Buick case is worth knowing because most repositioning looks more like Buick than Old Spice. There's no single ad that turned the brand. There's a decade of small, expensive, mostly invisible work that bent the perception five degrees a year. Old Spice is the case study because it photographs well. Buick is the case study because it's the one most brands will actually have to run.

Postscript, 2026: The repositioning moved the needle in the short term — average buyer age dropped from 63 to 58 and sales rose — but Buick has continued to contract in the U.S. market. The brand now sells only crossovers and SUVs (Encore GX, Envista, Envision, Enclave), most of GM's Buick volume now comes from China rather than North America, and GM has announced the brand will transition entirely to electric by 2030. Even successful repositioning may not save a brand from category-level decline.

Repositioning is usually grind, not viral.

3.5 The Positioning Statement

Most brand teams eventually write down a single sentence that captures what the brand stands for in the target's mind. The standard form, originally attributed to Geoffrey Moore, is some variant of:

For [target customer], [brand name] is the [category] that [single most important benefit/point of differentiation], because [the reason to believe].

The statement is internal. It is not a tagline. It is not customer-facing copy. Its job is to give every person making a decision about the brand — the agency, the product team, the PR team, the social team, the retail team — a single sentence to test their work against. A choice that supports the positioning statement is on-strategy. A choice that contradicts it is off-strategy, regardless of how clever the choice is on its own merits.

04
Operationalizing

Making STP Actually Work

STP is not a deliverable. It is an operating system. The segmentation document, the targeting brief, and the positioning statement are useful only to the degree that they cascade into the rest of the marketing decisions a firm makes. A brilliant positioning statement in a drawer is worth less than a mediocre positioning statement that every team across the organization can recite.

4.1 STP Into the Four Ps

The link between STP and the rest of marketing runs through the four Ps. Each one must be checked against the positioning, and any P that contradicts the position fights the brand from the inside.

Strategy
STP
→
Product
Features, design, quality, packaging — must deliver what the position promises.
Price
Premium positions require premium prices. Value positions require credible affordability.
Place
Where the product sells signals what it is. Tiffany at a kiosk would break the brand. Walmart at premium prices would too.
Promotion
The element that shouts the position — but only credibly if the other three Ps back it up.

The four Ps fail when one of them gets out of alignment. A luxury brand that discounts heavily during slow quarters trains its customer to wait for the sale and undermines the positioning that justified the premium price in the first place. A budget brand that introduces an upmarket product line confuses the original target without convincing the new one. A premium brand that lets its product end up at off-price retail loses control of its place dimension and dilutes the position.

4.2 Push, Pull, and the Targeting of Channels

One distinction worth pulling out of the place-and-promotion crossover is the difference between push and pull strategies, because each implies a different target.

  • Push aims promotional effort at the channel — wholesalers, distributors, retailers — to convince them to stock, display, and sell the product. The "target" of push promotion is not the end consumer but the trade. Trade shows, sales incentives, retailer co-op programs, and slotting fees are all push tactics.
  • Pull aims promotional effort at the end consumer, to generate demand the consumer will then bring to retailers. The retailer stocks the product because customers are asking for it. Most consumer advertising is pull. Direct-to-consumer brands are pure-pull plays — they bypass the channel entirely.

Most established brands run both simultaneously. A new brand often has to start with one. Newer DTC brands typically start pull-heavy because they have no distribution to push through. Industrial and B2B brands often start push-heavy because the consumer demand they need to generate is mediated through purchasing departments and procurement processes that respond more to sales reps than to advertising.

4.3 Five Takeaways

If a single page of notes had to capture what this unit argued, it would be these five claims.

  1. Segmentation is observation; targeting is choice. The market gives you the groups. You decide which ones to chase. Confusing the two is how brands end up "targeting everyone."
  2. The easiest data is the least predictive. Demographics are convenient and visible. Behavior is the truth. The strongest segmentations combine bases; the weakest stop at demographics.
  3. A segment is not a target until it passes five tests. Measurable, substantial, accessible, differentiable, actionable. A segment that fails any of them is a slide, not a strategy.
  4. Positioning is what your customer thinks, not what you say. Your job is to influence the thinking, not to declare it done. Every brand is positioned somewhere — the only question is whether you directed it.
  5. STP fails when it's a slogan instead of an operating system. If your product, price, and place don't reinforce your position, your promotion is a check the brand can't cash.
Marketing's hardest question isn't "what should we say?" It's "to whom, and why us?" Everything else in this course is downstream of that answer.

Glossary

Curated terms used in this unit. Definitions are one sentence each.

Segmentation
The act of dividing a market into distinct groups whose members share characteristics that make them likely to respond similarly to a marketing program.
Targeting
The choice of which segment or segments a firm will pursue, and the strategic allocation of resources to those segments.
Positioning
The deliberate construction of a brand's meaning in the target customer's mind, relative to competing brands in the same category.
Demographic Segmentation
Dividing a market by measurable population characteristics: age, gender, income, education, ethnicity, household composition.
Geographic Segmentation
Dividing a market by location — nation, region, state, city, neighborhood, climate zone.
Geodemographic Segmentation
The combination of geographic and demographic data into pre-built neighborhood-level clusters, sold commercially by firms like Esri (Tapestry) and Nielsen (PRIZM).
Psychographic Segmentation
Dividing a market by activities, interests, opinions, values, and lifestyles — the AIO variables.
VALS
A commercial psychographic framework from Strategic Business Insights that classifies U.S. adults into eight segments based on primary motivation (ideals, achievement, self-expression) and resources.
Behavioral Segmentation
Dividing a market by what customers actually do — purchase history, usage rate, loyalty status, benefits sought, and online behavior.
80/20 Rule (Pareto Principle)
The empirical observation that roughly 20% of a brand's customers tend to account for roughly 80% of its sales volume.
Loyalty Status
A behavioral classification of customers into hard-core loyals, split loyals, shifting loyals, and switchers, based on their pattern of brand purchases.
Benefit Segmentation
Grouping customers by the specific benefits they are seeking from a product, rather than by who they are or where they live.
Undifferentiated Marketing
A coverage strategy that ignores segment differences and offers one product with one marketing mix to the entire market.
Differentiated Marketing
A coverage strategy that enters multiple segments with a separate marketing mix tailored to each.
Concentrated Marketing
A coverage strategy that selects one segment and focuses all marketing effort on dominating it.
The Five Tests
The standard checklist for evaluating segment attractiveness: measurable, substantial, accessible, differentiable, actionable.
Persona
A semi-fictional individual representation of a target customer, built from segmentation data but rendered as a named, photographed person with a backstory. Also called a buyer persona, marketing persona, donor persona, or candidate persona depending on context.
Repositioning
The deliberate alteration of a brand's existing position in the customer's mind, usually in response to declining relevance, target aging out, or competitive displacement.
Positioning Statement
An internal sentence that captures who the brand is for, what category it competes in, the central benefit it offers, and the reason the customer should believe it.
Push Strategy
A promotional strategy aimed at convincing channel members — wholesalers, distributors, retailers — to stock and merchandise a product.
Pull Strategy
A promotional strategy aimed at end consumers, intended to generate demand that consumers then bring to retailers.
