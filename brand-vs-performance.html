Brand vs. Performance, and the 60/40 Principle

The brand-versus-performance debate is the single most consequential strategic argument in modern marketing, and it shapes almost every budget decision a marketing organization makes.

Two Camps Both Half Right The Research Deviations More Views

7.1 The Two Camps

The two camps, drawn at their cleanest, look like this.

Brand marketing, sometimes called awareness marketing, and informally and somewhat dismissively called squishy marketing, is the activity of building long-term equity in the brand: emotional connection, recognition, preference, and the kind of cultural presence that makes a consumer think of you before they're shopping. Brand marketing works slowly. It plants seeds for future purchase decisions that haven't been triggered yet. It's measured in awareness scores, brand health surveys, share of voice, and qualitative impressions, none of which a CFO finds entirely satisfying. The Ally "Finally" campaign is brand marketing. The Apple "Think Different" campaign was brand marketing. The Nike swoosh on a billboard with no call to action is brand marketing.

Performance marketing is the activity of producing immediate, measurable response: clicks, conversions, sign-ups, purchases. Performance marketing works fast. Every dollar can be traced, at least in theory, to a specific outcome. Search ads, retargeting, paid social with conversion objectives, affiliate links, abandoned-cart emails, promotional offers. The Google Ads dashboard is the natural habitat of performance marketing. The metrics are clean. The attribution is plausible. The CFO is happy.

7.2 Both Camps Are Half Right

Both camps are correct about something the other camp misses, and both camps overplay their hand when they're left in charge.

The performance side is correct that measurement matters and that brand marketers have, for decades, hidden behind unfalsifiable claims about "awareness" and "consideration" while spending real money on work that nobody could prove was working. The performance discipline has imposed a level of accountability on marketing that the discipline genuinely needed. Wanamaker's quote about not knowing which half of his advertising worked is less embarrassing to modern marketers than it was, because we now have at least some honest answer to the question, at least for some channels.

The brand side is correct that performance marketing systematically undervalues anything that doesn't convert within a measurement window, which means it systematically undervalues the work that creates future customers as opposed to harvesting existing demand. A brand with no equity has no pricing power, no defense against competitors who decide to outspend it on the same performance channels, and no pool of warm prospects for the performance ads to convert. Performance marketing without brand investment upstream is, in a memorable phrase, fishing in a shrinking pond.

7.3 What the Research Actually Says

The research evidence on this debate is unusually clear, for marketing research. Les Binet and Peter Field, working from the IPA Effectiveness Databank in the United Kingdom, have spent two decades analyzing what actually drives long-run marketing effectiveness across thousands of campaigns. Their headline finding, replicated in multiple studies and across multiple categories, is that established brands optimize long-run growth at roughly a 60/40 split: about 60% of marketing budget in brand-building activity and about 40% in performance activation.

The 60/40 principle is the closest thing modern marketing has to a default. It is not, however, a universal law. Several classes of business have good reasons to deviate from it, and understanding the deviations is more useful than memorizing the headline number.

7.4 The Deviations That Matter

A few of these deviations deserve a closer look, because they reveal something about how the principle actually operates.

Luxury brands run brand-dominant for a structural reason, not a stylistic one. A click-to-purchase retargeting ad for a $12,000 Rolex isn't just inefficient. It's actively destructive, because the entire commercial proposition of a luxury good rests on the appearance of scarcity, exclusivity, and the implicit suggestion that you don't need customers, they need you. Performance marketing in luxury isn't a different choice from brand marketing. It's an admission that the brand has failed.

Startups run performance-dominant out of necessity. When you have eighteen months of runway and need to prove unit economics before the next funding round, you cannot afford the long lag time of brand investment. Performance marketing buys you measurable feedback in days, which is what the early stage requires. The trap, and it is a trap that has killed many otherwise promising companies, is staying at the startup ratio permanently. The DTC supplement and apparel brands of 2020 to 2023 are the textbook cases: heavily performance-dependent for years, eventually unable to grow past the ceiling that paid-channel CPAs imposed, because they never built the brand recognition that would let them acquire customers more cheaply. They were efficient until they weren't, and by the time the efficiency broke, the brand asset they would have needed to fall back on had never been built.

Seasonal businesses flip the ratio during their active window. H&R Block runs brand-light campaigns through most of the year and floods the channel with performance activity from January through April. Halloween costume retailers do the same thing in October. The seasonal pattern is the inverse of the steady-state model: brand investment in the off-season enables performance efficiency during the window, because the brand is in the consumer's mind when the deadline triggers the buying decision.

The deeper point in all of these deviations is that the 60/40 split is a long-run average across an established brand's normal operations. It is not a recipe to be applied to every business. The right ratio for any specific company depends on its category, its maturity, its competitive position, and what part of the customer relationship it currently needs to invest in. What stays constant across all the deviations is the underlying mechanic: brand investment fills the pool, performance investment harvests the pool, and businesses that abandon the first activity eventually run out of fish.

We'll see this principle in action when we look at Ally Financial and Solo Stove later in the course as extended cases. For now, here's the working framing to carry forward: when someone proposes a marketing plan that's all performance, ask them what they're doing about the top of the funnel. A plan that's all brand deserves the inverse question: how are you harvesting the demand you're creating? The right answer is almost never only one. The right answer is almost always both, in proportions that depend on what kind of business this is and where it currently sits in its lifecycle.

Takeaway
  • Brand marketing builds long-term equity, works slowly, hard to measure.
  • Performance marketing drives short-term conversion, works fast, easy to measure.
  • The 60/40 principle (60% brand, 40% performance) is the default for established brands.
  • Deviations are real: luxury runs 85/15, startups run 20/80, seasonal businesses flip during their window.
  • The right question isn't "brand or performance?" It's "what does this business currently need most?"

More Views

The 60/40 principle has been the most influential single number in modern marketing for over a decade — and the most contested. Binet and Field published it in 2013 based on nearly 1,000 IPA Effectiveness Awards case studies covering 700 brands across 83 sectors from 1980 onward. The research is unusually robust for the field. But the headline number obscures real complexity: the optimal split varies by category, brand maturity, lifecycle stage, and competitive context, and the original research itself acknowledged these deviations. Meanwhile, between 2014 and 2024 the broader market moved decisively in the opposite direction. WARC data shows the budget allocation inverted to roughly 69% performance and 31% brand by 2024 — exactly the inversion Binet and Field warned against, with predictable long-term effectiveness declines. The recent DTC collapses (paid customer-acquisition costs up 40-60% from 2023 to 2025; the average DTC brand now losing money on the first order) are the cleanest natural experiment for what happens when performance dominates for too long. The five pieces below sample these positions: the primary source from IPA, Mark Ritson's ten-year anniversary commentary, a comprehensive 2026 reanalysis, a vertical-specific application, and a reality check on the DTC failures.
