Project Context · 01

The Category Changes the Marketing

A bag of chips, a mattress, and a Tesla are bought in three completely different ways. Before you can judge a brand's marketing, you have to know what its category demands of it.

01
The Argument

The Category Sets the Rules. The Brand Decides How to Play.

There is a default reasoning pattern that turns up constantly in marketing pitches and student case studies. It starts with the brand. The brand has a personality. The brand has a value proposition. The brand needs to be more like this competitor or lean into that trend. From there, the recommendation flows outward to channels, creative, and budget.

The reasoning is backwards. The brand does not get to decide how its category is bought. The category was being bought a certain way long before the brand showed up. A bag of chips and a mattress and a luxury handbag and a B2B software contract are not bought the same way, advertised the same way, or won the same way, and the brand that ignores this loses to one that does not.

Before you can judge a brand's IMC choices, you have to know what its category demands of it. Five questions will get you most of the way there.

02
The Diagnostic

Five Questions That Reset Everything Downstream

1. How often is the category bought?

Toothpaste runs out every few weeks. Mattresses are replaced roughly every eight years, according to Better Sleep Council research that tracks the figure year over year (Better Sleep Council, 2023). Wedding dresses, often once in a lifetime. Funeral services, once per family member. Frequency determines whether you are maintaining mental availability (Sharp, 2010) across thousands of small purchase moments, or intercepting a buyer during a single, deliberate search window that opens and closes inside a few weeks.

Tip High-frequency categories generally lean heavier on ongoing brand-building, because mental availability is the everyday lever. Low-frequency categories often need a smaller, persistent brand presence plus concentrated performance media inside the listening window. More frequency does not automatically mean more total ads. It means a different shape of media, weighted toward consistent reach over time.

2. What occasion or trigger creates demand?

Some categories are routine. Detergent runs low, you replace it. Some are event-driven. A new baby, a move, a divorce, a death, a wedding, an illness. Some are aspirational and seasonal. Gym memberships in January, fragrances in December, lawn equipment in April. The trigger tells you when the listening window opens, and therefore when paid media is not being wasted on a buyer who is not yet in market.

Tip Event-driven categories often benefit from pulsing media around the trigger rather than steady always-on spending. Seasonal categories typically pre-load awareness several weeks before the season and shift to conversion-oriented work inside it. If your brand sits in a clustered, occasion-driven category, the calendar matters as much as the budget.

3. How much risk, research, or explanation precedes purchase?

Marketing economists since Phillip Nelson distinguished search goods, whose qualities are evaluable before purchase (a shirt's fit and color), from experience goods, whose qualities are revealed only by using them (a restaurant, a hotel stay) (Nelson, 1970). Darby and Karni later added a third category, credence goods, whose qualities the buyer cannot really verify even after consumption: a financial advisor's competence, a vitamin's effect, a contractor's structural work (Darby & Karni, 1973). The category's place on that spectrum determines how much middle-funnel work matters. A $3 yogurt requires almost no consideration. A $3,000 mattress requires a stretch of weeks. A $300,000 enterprise software contract can take a year.

Tip High-research categories generally need substantial middle-funnel investment: reviews, comparison content, demos, retargeting, and longer-form video. Low-risk impulse categories can run almost entirely on shelf presence, distinctive assets, and brand recognition. If buyers are doing weeks of homework, your awareness work has to be carrying their memory of the brand the whole way through.

4. Are the buyer and user the same person?

Baby food is bought by a parent and used by a baby. Pet food is bought by an owner and consumed by an animal. B2B software is bought by procurement and used by the employees who file tickets when it breaks. Engagement rings are bought by one person and worn by another. The communication target is whoever holds the wallet, but the experience target is the user, and most failures here come from optimizing only for the one who pays (Andrews & Shimp, 2018).

Tip When buyer and user differ, the brand often has to talk to both. The buyer needs reassurance about price, safety, or fit. The user needs to actually enjoy the product. Audit whether the brand's communications are quietly speaking to only one of the two, because that is the most common audience-strategy failure in categories with a split.

5. Is success driven by habit, comparison, trial, or social meaning?

Laundry detergent and gas stations are largely habitual. You bought what you bought last time. Car insurance and mattresses are comparison-driven. You actively shop. Craft beer and new restaurants are trial-driven. You sample, then settle. Handbags, sneakers, watches, and the brand of car you park in front of your house are won at least partly through social meaning, which is what owning the thing says about the person owning it. Most categories combine several of these. Identifying the dominant lever is the point, because the lever decides where the money should go.

Tip Habit categories typically require disruption to break in (sampling, distinctive packaging, deals). Comparison categories reward visibility at the moment of search. Trial categories reward demos, samples, and first-purchase incentives. Social-meaning categories reward owned media, community, and cultural association. The lever changes the PESO mix.
03
In Practice

Two Brands, Two Categories, Two Different Games

Same five questions, two very different answers. Notice what the diagnosis implies for funnel coverage and budget allocation in each case.

Pringles
High Frequency, Low Risk, Habitual
  • Bought weekly or every few weeks
  • Zero research, near-zero consideration
  • Won on shelf presence, distinctive assets, and the next purchase moment
  • Mental availability is the whole game
  • Many small impressions compound across categories of media
Tempur-Pedic
Low Frequency, High Risk, Comparison
  • Bought roughly once a decade
  • Reviews, in-store trials, financing decisions
  • Won during a four-to-six-week active shopping window
  • Search ads, retargeting, and store presence carry disproportionate weight
  • Brand-building has to maintain a name worth remembering when the window opens

Both brands are in mature categories. Both have strong awareness. Both run national advertising. But the budgets, the funnel coverage, the role of search, and the relationship between paid and earned media look almost nothing alike. Treating them as if they have the same job to do is the kind of mistake that gets confidently delivered in pitch decks.

In Your Project

The category's profile reshapes most of the IMC concepts you will be graded on. Use this diagnostic before you write any judgments about your brand's mix.

  • Funnel coverage. Habitual, high-frequency categories live mostly at the top of the funnel (mental availability and distinctive assets). Comparison-driven, infrequent categories require heavy middle-funnel investment (reviews, comparisons, retargeting).
  • Repetition needs. Repetition compounds best when buyers are in-market often. Infrequent categories need a different model: long-running brand presence to be remembered when the window opens, plus concentrated bottom-funnel work inside the window.
  • The 60/40 split. The default skews toward more performance in high-consideration categories with long search windows. It skews toward more brand in habitual, low-consideration categories where mental availability is the real lever.
  • PESO emphasis. Trial-driven categories lean on earned media. Habit-driven categories lean on paid. Social-meaning categories lean on owned and shared. None of these is a default for "good marketing." They are responses to category conditions.

Research Prompts

Use the Find the Facts queries to source hard data about your category. Use the Think It Through prompts only after you have the data. AI is helpful for interpretation and unreliable for invention.

Find the Facts

  • Google: "[your category]" purchase frequency and "[your category]" consumer journey
  • Search NielsenIQ, IRI, Mintel, Statista, or Kantar for category-level purchase data
  • Trade publications often publish purchase-cycle data for their category (Beverage Digest, BedTimes, Drug Channels, etc.)
  • Annual reports and investor decks of large players sometimes disclose buyer demographics and frequency benchmarks
  • Industry associations (Better Sleep Council, ISPA, IAB, IFA) publish category-level research

Think It Through

  • "Given that [category] is bought every [frequency] with [low/medium/high] research effort, what should that imply about funnel coverage and brand-versus-performance split for a brand competing in it?"
  • "Who is the buyer and who is the user for [brand]'s products? Are they the same? If different, how should that show up in the creative brief and the media plan?"
  • "What share of [category] purchases are habitual, comparison-driven, trial-driven, or driven by social meaning? Where are the sources for that estimate?"
  • "What occasions or triggers create demand in [category]? When do most purchases cluster on the calendar, and what does that imply for media timing?"

Vocabulary

Glossary

Search good
A product whose qualities can be evaluated before purchase: color, size, price, specifications. Easy to compare, low informational risk.
Experience good
A product whose qualities are revealed only after use: a restaurant meal, a hotel stay, a streaming service. Reviews and trial periods matter disproportionately.
Credence good
A product whose qualities the buyer cannot fully verify even after consumption: legal advice, vitamins, structural repairs, a financial planner's competence. Trust signals carry the weight.
Habitual purchase
Repeat buying with little deliberation. The brand chosen last time is usually the brand chosen this time. Mental availability and distribution carry the win.
Replenishment purchase
Bought when the previous unit runs out. The listening window opens quickly and closes quickly.
Mental availability
The probability that a brand comes to mind in a purchase moment. The Ehrenberg-Bass formulation popularized by Byron Sharp. Distinct from awareness, which only requires the buyer to recognize the brand when prompted.
Listening window
The period during which a buyer is actively in-market and receptive to category messages. Long in mattresses and cars. Short in chips and gas.
