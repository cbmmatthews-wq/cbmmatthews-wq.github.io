Project Context · 02

Launch, Scale, Defend, or Revive?

Most growth problems are misdiagnosed because the brand and the category get analyzed as if they were the same thing. Three questions, asked in order, separate them, and the answer usually picks the communications objective for you.

01
The Argument

Stop Asking Whether the Brand Is Mature

Students often reach for the undergraduate-textbook lifecycle and apply it to brands as if the four stages were properties of the brand alone.

The Classic Textbook Approach

The four-stage product lifecycle, popularized in marketing textbooks since Theodore Levitt's 1965 Harvard Business Review article, defines four stages a product or brand passes through (Levitt, 1965): Introduction (low sales, high marketing cost per buyer, awareness and trial work), Growth (rising sales, competitors entering, differentiation and distribution work), Maturity (sales peak and plateau, intense competition, retention and modest extension work), and Decline (sales fall, harvest or exit).

That framing is not exactly wrong. It just collapses two different conditions into one fuzzy label and implies that mature brands should all do roughly the same thing. The diagnostic that actually informs marketing decisions is three layered questions, asked in order: what is happening to the category, what is happening to the brand inside that category, and what growth lever is available to the brand right now. A new brand can enter a mature category. An old brand can operate inside a rapidly growing one. A market leader can be quietly declining while a small challenger inside the same category is doubling. Treating those as the same situation produces the same generic plan, which is the situation we are trying to avoid.

02
The Diagnostic

Three Questions, In Order

1. What is happening to the category?

Emerging means the category is small but growing fast, awareness is low, and rules of use are still being defined. Growing means real demand is expanding and competitors are arriving. Mature means demand is saturated and brands are fighting for share more than they are creating new buyers. Declining or disrupted means the category is shrinking, or an adjacent category is taking buyers (think landline phones, casual dining, traditional cable).

Tip Category growth fills the brand's sails. In growing categories, even average brands often grow because the tide is rising. In mature categories, growth has to be won from other brands or from non-buyers entering the category. Diagnose the category first, because it sets a ceiling on what the brand's plan can plausibly achieve.

2. What is happening to the brand?

Launching means new, building first awareness and first trial. Scaling means a proven product expanding buyers, geography, or distribution. Defending means an established position under competitive or category pressure. Entering means an existing brand stepping into a new segment, geography, or use case. Reviving means a faded brand attempting to come back. Harvesting means managing decline for cash, not for growth.

Tip A brand and its category can be in completely different states. The Apple Vision Pro is launching inside an emerging category. Liquid Death is scaling inside a mature one. Stanley revived inside a mature one. Diagnose the brand's state independently of the category's, because the IMC plan depends on the intersection of the two.

3. What growth lever is available?

The classical levers are seven: more buyers (penetration), more purchase occasions or use cases, higher purchase frequency among existing buyers, bigger basket per transaction, higher price or richer mix, new geography, or a new product or extension. Most brands have one or two genuinely available to them at any moment. The others sound plausible in a pitch deck and fail in the field.

Concrete examples make the abstract list easier to apply. McDonald's pushing All Day Breakfast and adding a coffee daypart is an occasion lever. Liquid Death expanding from convenience stores into grocery, foodservice, and club is a geography and channel lever. A streaming service moving from monthly to weekly episode releases is a frequency lever. Apple extending from iPhone to AirPods to Watch to Vision Pro is an extension lever. Same framework, different lever in each case, different campaign.

Tip Different levers point to different IMC plans. Penetration favors broad reach and brand-building. Frequency among existing buyers leans into retention email, loyalty programs, and reminder media. Occasion expansion leans into time-of-day or use-case content. Geography means starting awareness from zero in the new place. Identify the dominant lever, because the budget should weight to it.
03
In Practice

Four Brands, Four Different Problems

Same diagnostic, four very different answers. Each scenario sits at a different intersection of category condition and brand condition, and each implies a different shape for the IMC plan.

Launch · Apple Vision Pro
Big Brand, Emerging Category
  • Category not yet defined: spatial computing
  • Rules of use, language, distribution still forming
  • Marketing's first job is category education, not brand differentiation
  • Awareness has to convert to category interest before it can convert to trial
  • Lever: category-buyer creation, not share
Scale · Liquid Death
Challenger, Mature Category
  • $333M revenue in 2024, $1.4B valuation, in 133,000-plus US doors (Sacra, 2026)
  • Bottled water is mature and dominated by Coke and Pepsi
  • Lever: penetration. New category buyers and new occasions
  • IMC skews brand-heavy because category is well-understood
  • Brand's job is to install itself in memory, not explain the product
Defend · Coca-Cola
Leader, Mature Category
  • Dominant share, decades of distinctive assets
  • Lever: share defense plus modest occasion expansion
  • Distinctive brand assets carry most of the load
  • IMC emphasizes consistency, presence, and freshness inside the same essential codes
  • The mistake to avoid: chasing the challenger's playbook and abandoning your own assets
Revive · Stanley (Quencher)
Faded Brand, Mature Category
  • A 100-plus-year-old work-utility brand
  • Cultural moment around the Quencher tumbler turned it into a fashion item
  • Lever: convert a one-off cultural surge into a durable position
  • IMC has to extend the moment without becoming dependent on it
  • The risk: peak too high, then visibly fall, which is itself a brand signal
Specific brand examples are stickier than generic categories, but they date faster than the framework does. I opted for the sticky approach. If a brand here has shifted significantly since you read this, the diagnostic still applies. Update the example, not the method.
04
Theory Behind the Frame

Penetration, and Why It Usually Wins the Argument

The Sharp and Ehrenberg-Bass research (introduced in Unit 2A) gets pulled forward here because it is the empirical scaffolding behind question three. Across hundreds of categories, sustained brand growth has been shown to come more from adding new buyers than from extracting more from existing buyers (Sharp, 2010). The finding is more robust than its critics allow, and it explains why so many loyalty-focused campaigns produce small returns relative to the spend behind them.

That said, it is not a universal commandment. Retention, frequency, and customer expansion matter especially in subscription, B2B, and other relationship-based models, where the value of a buyer is realized only over time (Sharp & Romaniuk, 2016). The way to hold both ideas at once is that they are sequential, not competing. Most growth requires acquiring more buyers. How valuable those buyers become depends on retention. A subscription brand with terrible retention has a broken model. A subscription brand with excellent retention but no new acquisition eventually stops growing.

05
In Execution

From Penetration to Targeting: Wide or Narrow?

The penetration argument has a direct execution implication students often miss. Wider targeting and narrower targeting are not the same media plan, and choosing between them shifts the budget toward awareness or toward conversion.

Wide targeting reaches all category buyers regardless of intent. Expensive per conversion, cheap per impression. It is the only kind of media buy that grows mental availability among buyers who are not yet in-market. Narrow targeting reaches a defined audience with known intent or behavior. Efficient per conversion, limited in available impressions, best for capturing demand the brand has already created.

This is roughly what the 60/40 brand-versus-performance split is naming. The 60% brand portion is wide targeting at category buyers. The 40% performance portion is narrow targeting at in-market or warm audiences. 60/40 is not a budget rule. It is a width-and-temperature decision dressed up as a percentage.

The common student instinct is "more targeted is better." Sharp's penetration argument is the opposite at the brand tier. A brand that over-targets to a narrow in-market audience appears to be performing efficiently while quietly shrinking its addressable market. Conversion goes up. Growth stops.

In ad-buying terms, audiences are typically grouped by temperature: cold (no prior interaction with the brand, requires awareness work), warm (some prior engagement with the brand or its content), and hot (past purchasers, cart abandoners, email subscribers). The cold-warm-hot vocabulary roughly maps to brand-versus-performance, with cold audiences carrying the brand-building work and hot audiences carrying the conversion work.

Tip When the project pivots to a new segment, the width question becomes explicit. Narrow the media buy to the new segment only, or widen to all category buyers and let positioning sort the segment out? Narrowing makes the pivot look efficient. Widening gives the brand room to grow into the new segment. Smaller or cash-constrained brands usually narrow. Mature, well-funded brands usually widen.
06
The Bridge

From Growth Problem to Communications Objective

The growth diagnosis is the bridge from segment choice to the rest of the IMC plan. Once you know what the brand's growth problem actually is, the communications objective often picks itself. A challenger trying to scale in a mature category usually needs awareness and trial in a defined segment. A leader defending share needs consistent presence and the maintenance of its distinctive assets. A reviving brand needs to convert a moment into a position.

Three levels of objective are worth keeping straight, because they are routinely confused.

Level What it names Example
Business objective The financial or share outcome the company actually cares about Grow revenue from buyers aged 35 to 50 by 12% in 12 months
Communications objective The change in awareness, perception, or behavior the IMC plan can plausibly produce Increase consideration of the brand among buyers aged 35 to 50
Media metric A measure of activity, not outcome Reach 60% of the segment at adequate frequency

For a segment pivot like the second half of your project, the right communications objective will almost always be one of these:

  • Build awareness in a new segment
  • Drive trial in a new segment
  • Shift consideration or perception
  • Increase purchase frequency among existing buyers
  • Add purchase occasions or use cases
  • Defend share against a challenger
  • Reposition on a specific attribute
  • Shift balance from acquisition to retention

Pick one as primary. A campaign that tries to do all eight does none of them. The full treatment of objectives, evaluation, and attribution will arrive on a later page in the term; for this project, one well-defended communications objective is enough.

In Your Project

The growth diagnosis is the single most consequential analytical move in the second half of your work. Get it right and the rest of the plan flows logically. Get it wrong and every downstream choice has to be defended on instinct.

  • Reach versus frequency mix. A penetration problem points to reach. A frequency or retention problem points to frequency.
  • The 60/40 direction. Brand-heavy is the right default when the lever is penetration. Performance-heavy is defensible when the lever is conversion of an already-aware audience.
  • The communications objective. Use the growth diagnosis to choose one of the eight objectives above. Defend why that one and not another.
  • What counts as success. Tie the objective to a measure that is a communications outcome, not a media activity. Reach is an input. Consideration shift is an outcome.

Research Prompts

Use the Find the Facts queries to source hard data about the brand and the category. Use the Think It Through prompts only after you have the data. AI is helpful for interpretation and unreliable for invention.

Find the Facts

  • SEC EDGAR for 10-Ks and 10-Qs of public companies (often discloses category growth, channel mix, marketing spend)
  • Google: "[brand]" CMO interview, "[brand]" year ahead, "[brand]" strategy 2025
  • Trade publications: AdAge, Marketing Brew, Modern Retail, Business of Fashion, Beverage Digest
  • Category growth rate from NielsenIQ, IRI, Statista, Euromonitor; compare to brand growth rate
  • Earnings call transcripts (Motley Fool, Seeking Alpha) often state the growth problem in management's own words

Think It Through

  • "Given that [brand]'s category is [emerging / growing / mature / declining], and the brand appears to be [launching / scaling / defending / entering / reviving / harvesting], what is the primary growth problem?"
  • "Which growth lever is most plausible for [brand] right now: more buyers, more occasions, more frequency, bigger basket, higher price, or new geography? What is the evidence for that lever versus the others?"
  • "Translate that growth diagnosis into one primary communications objective. Defend why that is the objective rather than a media metric like reach, impressions, or video views."
  • "Is [brand]'s most public recent campaign aimed at the right growth lever, given the diagnosis? If not, what would a better-aimed campaign look like?"

Vocabulary

Glossary

Penetration
The share of category buyers who buy your brand in a given period. The central growth metric in Sharp's framework. Bigger brands almost always win on penetration.
Loyalty
The rate at which buyers of your brand keep buying it across repeat purchases. In most categories, loyalty differences between brands are smaller than people assume.
Double Jeopardy
The empirical pattern that small brands have fewer buyers and those buyers are slightly less loyal. Bigger brands win on both sides. Named because the small brand is punished twice.
Growth lever
The mechanism by which a brand can grow: more buyers, more occasions, more frequency, bigger basket, higher price, new geography, or new product. Most brands have one or two genuinely available at any moment.
Business objective
The financial or share outcome the company is trying to produce: revenue, profit, share. The thing the CFO cares about.
Communications objective
A stated change in awareness, perception, or behavior that an IMC plan is designed to produce: build awareness, drive trial, shift consideration, increase frequency, defend share.
Media metric
A measure of media output (reach, impressions, clicks, video views), not an outcome. Confusing media metrics with objectives is one of the most common mistakes in undergraduate and early-career marketing plans.
Targeting width
The breadth of the audience a media buy is aimed at. Wide targeting reaches all category buyers regardless of intent; expensive per conversion but the only way to grow mental availability. Narrow targeting reaches a defined audience with known intent or behavior; efficient per conversion but limited in scale.
Audience temperature (cold, warm, hot)
A media-buying convention that groups audiences by prior interaction with the brand. Cold audiences have no prior interaction and require awareness work. Warm audiences have had some engagement with the brand or its content. Hot audiences are past purchasers, cart abandoners, or email subscribers. Cold audiences roughly carry the brand-building work, hot audiences roughly carry the conversion work.
