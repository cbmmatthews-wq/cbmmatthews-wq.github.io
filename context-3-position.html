Project Context · 03

Where Does the Brand Sit, and What Does Its Price Signal?

A label like "premium" or "niche" carries more weight in a marketing plan than it should. Three separate questions sit underneath it, and they sometimes disagree.

01
The Argument

One Label, Doing Too Much Work

In student case studies and pitch decks the brand often gets sorted into one of three buckets: mass, premium, or niche. Occasionally luxury gets added as a fourth. The labels are not exactly wrong, but they are doing too much work because they conflate three separate dimensions that frequently disagree.

A product can be mass-produced but aimed at a narrow niche. Handmade and sold to a broad market. Premium but widely distributed. Expensive without being meaningfully premium. Scarce by manufacturing necessity or scarce by deliberate strategy. Each of those combinations implies a different IMC plan. To diagnose where a brand actually sits, ask three independent questions, then read the combination.

02
The Diagnostic

Three Axes, Not One

1. Market scope

How big a tent is the brand pitching? The classical sequence runs from mass market (broad demand, designed for everyone) through broad differentiated (a clear segment focus) to specialist or niche (a narrowly defined buyer with specific needs) and on to prestige (a deliberately small slice that pays disproportionately).

Tip Market scope is the segmentation question wearing different clothes. Most brands have a defensible position on this axis, but extending it (a mass brand creating a premium line, a niche brand chasing mass) is one of the most common sources of growth-strategy failure.

2. Availability

How easy is the brand to find and buy? Ubiquitous at one end (drugstores, supermarkets, gas stations). Selective in the middle (limited retailer list, brand-controlled environments). Exclusive beyond that (boutique-only, by appointment, private client). Intentionally scarce at the far end (the Hermès Birkin, certain Rolex models, limited-release sneakers).

Tip Availability and scope are not the same axis. A premium brand can be widely available (Tide is in every supermarket). A niche brand can be hard to find even when its positioning is for a small audience. Availability is also one of the few axes a brand controls directly through distribution choices.

3. Price tier

Where does the brand's price sit relative to category alternatives? This is the most consequential of the three for IMC, because price tier interacts with discount strategy, channel mix, distribution, and how aggressively the brand can compete on performance media. It is also the one most often labeled in isolation, which is the source of the confusion. The next section covers it in more detail.

03
Price Tiers

The Five Tiers (and Why They Are Comparative)

Most brands sit on a five-tier price spectrum. The tier label is meaningful only inside a category. A $75 notebook is prestige in stationery. A $75 mattress is discount in bedding.

  • Discount. Priced clearly below the category norm. The brand wins on price. Distribution often skews to mass retailers, value channels, and warehouse clubs.
  • Value. Priced at or slightly below the norm with a clear benefit story. Not "cheapest available," but "defensible deal." Costco's Kirkland is the textbook case.
  • Mainstream or competitive. Priced inside the broad acceptable range. Most established brands live here. The work is done by differentiation, distinctive assets, and execution rather than by the price itself.
  • Premium. Priced clearly above the category norm with a defended difference in performance, materials, service, or experience. The buyer is paying for tangible better.
  • Prestige or luxury. Priced significantly above the norm with a difference that includes symbolism, scarcity, status, and cultural meaning, not only performance. The category is large: the Bain–Altagamma 2025 study estimated global personal luxury goods sales at €358 billion (Bain & Altagamma, 2025).

The longer treatment of price elasticity, the reference-price boundary, and the modifier that widens or narrows the buyer's zone of tolerance (the price range inside which a buyer will not switch) lives on the dedicated Zone of Indifference page. For project purposes, you mainly need to identify which tier the brand occupies in its category, and whether its current pricing is consistent with that position.

Tip When in doubt about which tier a brand occupies, look at where and how it discounts. Discount brands discount constantly. Value brands discount strategically. Mainstream brands discount in cycles. Premium brands discount carefully. Prestige brands rarely discount at all, because the value depends on the price holding.
04
The Key Distinction

Premium versus Prestige

The premium and prestige labels get used interchangeably, but they describe different brands and require different IMC plans (Kapferer & Bastien, 2012).

Premium brands promise tangible better: superior performance, materials, service, or experience. Tide Pods over store-brand detergent. Hoka over a generic running shoe. The Westin over a Holiday Inn. The premium brand argues, "pay more, get more, and here is what more means."

Prestige brands promise that plus something else: symbolism, scarcity, status, cultural meaning, controlled availability. The Hermès Birkin is not just a better bag. It is a signal that travels in social space when carried. Patek Philippe is not just a better watch. It is a generational object that is consciously designed to be inherited.

The most common student confusion at this distinction is the assumption that a prestige brand should restrict everything, including communications reach. That is wrong, and it matters. A prestige brand often wants enormous communications reach because broad fame is part of what makes the brand prestigious. Millions of non-buyers need to recognize, desire, and culturally understand the brand for the small number of actual buyers to feel the social value of owning it.

What prestige does need to restrict is product availability, presentation, distribution, discounting, and access to certain lines. A luxury brand can want mass awareness without mass ownership. The Bain–Altagamma research has tracked this dynamic explicitly: the global luxury customer base contracted by roughly 50 million buyers between 2022 and 2024, even as the surrounding cultural visibility of luxury brands remained high (Bain & Altagamma, 2025).

Tip Premium brands face a softer version of the same set of risks. Repeated discounting can erode the reference price, weaken quality inferences, and train buyers to wait for sales. Prestige brands are simply more sensitive to that damage, not uniquely exposed to it. If you are not sure whether the brand is premium or prestige, ask whether its value depends on what owning it says about the owner. If yes, you are closer to prestige.
05
In Practice

What Each Tier Changes for IMC

A discount brand and a prestige brand can both run national campaigns. Those campaigns should look almost nothing alike.

Tier Where the IMC plan usually weights
Discount Performance media, price-led messages, retailer co-op support, frequent sales promotion
Value Benefit and comparison content, light strategic promotion, balanced brand and performance
Mainstream Distinctive assets, broad reach, periodic promotion, default 60/40 brand-versus-performance
Premium Brand-building, proof points, controlled presentation, careful discount strategy
Prestige Owned and earned media, cultural placement, broad fame paired with controlled availability, discounting rare or avoided

The mistake to avoid in each direction: a discount brand spending like a prestige brand will run out of money before it runs out of competitors, and a prestige brand running discount tactics will erode the position the price was meant to defend.

In Your Project

Diagnose the brand on all three axes before you write any judgments about its IMC plan. The combination decides several things you will be graded on.

  • Discount strategy. Whether the brand can promote, and how often, depends on its tier. Audit whether current promotional behavior is consistent with the stated positioning.
  • Channel and media mix. Performance media carries more weight at the discount and value end. Owned and earned media carry more weight at the prestige end. Mid-tier brands have the most freedom and the most ways to misallocate.
  • Tone and creative. Tier sets the visual, verbal, and emotional register. A prestige brand making jokes is making a careful choice. A discount brand making jokes is competing with other discount brands.
  • The premium / prestige call. If you classify the brand as prestige, the project recommendation has to handle the reach-without-availability problem. If premium, the recommendation has to defend the price signal across all channels and promotions.

Research Prompts

Use the Find the Facts queries to source hard data about the brand and the category. Use the Think It Through prompts only after you have the data. AI is helpful for interpretation and unreliable for invention.

Find the Facts

  • Compare the brand's prices to category benchmarks using Statista, NielsenIQ, retailer aggregators, or direct retailer site walks
  • Map the brand's distribution: how many doors, which retailers, which channels (DTC, marketplace, wholesale, boutique)
  • Pull the brand's promotion frequency over the last 12 months from email archives, Honey, RetailMeNot, or social ad libraries (Meta Ad Library)
  • Bain & Company publishes the annual Luxury Goods Worldwide Market Study with Altagamma; current data on the personal luxury goods market is available free
  • Kapferer & Bastien's The Luxury Strategy is the canonical reference for the premium-versus-prestige distinction

Think It Through

  • "What is [brand]'s position on each of the three axes: market scope, availability, and price tier? Are the three answers consistent, or do they conflict?"
  • "Is the brand premium or prestige? What evidence supports the answer? If prestige, what is the brand's strategy for broad communications reach without broad product availability?"
  • "How often does [brand] discount, and through what channels? Is the pattern consistent with the stated tier, or is it slowly eroding the price signal?"
  • "If the project pivot moves [brand] into a different segment, would that pivot pull the brand toward a different tier? What would have to change about price, distribution, and discount strategy to make the pivot internally consistent?"

Vocabulary

Glossary

Market scope
How broadly the brand targets buyers. Mass, broad differentiated, specialist or niche, prestige.
Availability
How easy the brand is to find and buy. Ubiquitous, selective, exclusive, intentionally scarce. Controlled directly through distribution.
Price tier
Where the brand's price sits relative to category alternatives. Discount, value, mainstream, premium, prestige. Comparative, not absolute.
Premium
Priced above the category norm with a defended tangible difference: performance, materials, service, or experience. The brand argues for better.
Prestige (or Luxury)
Priced significantly above the norm with a difference that includes symbolism, scarcity, status, and cultural meaning, not only performance. Highly sensitive to uncontrolled availability and discounting.
Reference price
The price a buyer expects to pay for a category, formed through experience and shaped by the brand's own past pricing. Eroded by repeated discounting.
Zone of Indifference
The price band inside which a brand can move without losing buyers or signaling something new. Bounded above by perceived overpricing, bounded below by perceived quality risk. Covered in depth on the linked Zone of Indifference page.
