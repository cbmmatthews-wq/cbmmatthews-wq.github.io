Project Context · 04

DTC, Retail, or Both?

Direct-to-consumer was sold for a decade as the future of retail. The data does not show that the future arrived. It shows that the model has costs the original pitch hid, and that e-commerce and DTC are not the same thing.

01
The Argument

Two Confident Claims That Need Correcting

There are two confident claims that come up in nearly every pitch-deck conversation about modern retail. The first is some version of "e-commerce is taking over." The second is some version of "DTC is the future." Both contain a grain of truth wrapped around a much larger error.

On the first claim: the US Census Bureau's most recent quarterly release put US retail e-commerce sales at $326.7 billion against $1.93 trillion in total retail, or 16.9% of retail spending in the first quarter of 2026 (Census Bureau, 2026). E-commerce did grow faster than total retail year over year (9.8% versus 3.9%), so the share is still rising. But the level matters: roughly 83 cents of every retail dollar in the United States is still spent in a physical store or other non-e-commerce channel.

The second confusion is more consequential. E-commerce is not DTC. Most of that 16.9% online slice happens on Amazon, Walmart.com, Target.com, and other retailer sites. When a footwear brand sells through Zappos, or a CPG brand sells through Walmart's website, that is wholesale or marketplace e-commerce, not brand-owned DTC. True DTC is a substantially narrower slice that excludes both physical retail and most online retail.

Tip The 16.9% e-commerce share gets repeated as if it were the DTC share. It is not. Brand-owned DTC sits somewhere inside that number, often a small fraction of it. When students write that "DTC is taking over retail," they almost always mean e-commerce, which is a different and much larger thing.
02
The Taxonomy

Five Distribution Models

Most brands operate through some combination of the following. The mix matters more than any single channel.

  • Brand-owned DTC. The brand's own website and, where relevant, its own stores. Maximum margin per unit. Maximum customer data. Maximum control of the brand experience. Maximum operating cost, as the next sections explain.
  • Wholesale to retailers. Selling to brick-and-mortar and online retailers (Target, Sephora, REI, Best Buy). The retailer pays the brand a wholesale price, marks it up, and owns the customer relationship at point of sale.
  • Marketplace. Listing on Amazon, Walmart Marketplace, eBay, Etsy. The marketplace operates discovery, payment, and (often) fulfillment. The brand operates the listing, the ad spend, and the product.
  • Distributor or wholesaler. Especially common in beverages, foods, hardware, and B2B. The distributor handles logistics and retailer relationships in exchange for a substantial cut.
  • Franchising or licensing. Restaurants, fitness brands, and some apparel brands extend through operator partners who pay royalties on revenue.

Most established brands run several models at once. Liquid Death sells through grocery, mass, club, foodservice, Amazon, and its own website. Tide is in supermarkets, drug stores, mass retailers, and Amazon. Hoka is in specialty running stores, REI, Dick's Sporting Goods, its own website, and via international distributors.

Tip Channel mix is a strategic choice, not a neutral description. A brand that leans heavily into one channel is making a deliberate bet that the channel's economics will hold. It is also accepting that channel's specific risks: retailer power in wholesale, customer-acquisition cost in DTC, platform dependence in marketplace.

Where the Margins Sit

The Value Chain on One Product

Before working out what DTC saves and what it costs, it helps to see where the money goes in a traditional retail chain. The numbers below are illustrative but consistent with industry norms: a brand sells to a wholesaler at roughly double its cost of goods, the wholesaler marks up by 20 to 30%, and the retailer applies keystone pricing (doubling the wholesale price) to set the consumer sticker price.

The retailer carries the markdown risk. A 20% discount on the $24 sticker drops their margin from $11 to about $6. A 50% clearance takes them to roughly break-even. A buy-one-get-one promotion gives away the second unit's margin entirely. That risk is the price the brand and wholesaler pay to push inventory through someone else's footprint.

04
The Economics

What DTC Actually Costs

The original DTC pitch was that removing the retailer would let the brand keep the retailer's margin. The pitch was incomplete in one important way. Removing the retailer does not remove the work the retailer was doing. The brand inherits all of it.

Wholesale
What the Retailer Does
  • Generates store traffic and online discovery
  • Operates payment, fraud, and chargebacks
  • Warehouses, picks, and packs
  • Ships and handles returns
  • Provides customer service
  • Merchandises the shelf or the page
  • Manages customer data and reactivation
DTC
What the Brand Inherits
  • All demand generation, paid and earned
  • Website operations, payment processing, fraud
  • Warehousing and inventory exposure
  • Picking, packing, freight, returns logistics
  • Customer service infrastructure
  • Product discovery without a shelf
  • Data, attribution, and lifecycle marketing

The funnel has no free top

The most often-missed DTC cost is at the top of the funnel. A wholesale brand at Target gets discovery essentially for free: foot traffic the retailer paid to generate, shelf placement next to category alternatives, cross-merchandising, and adjacency. Someone walks in for shampoo and notices a brand of conditioner that the retailer placed next to it. Someone shopping a blouse on Nordstrom.com gets "frequently bought together" suggestions for shoes. None of that exists for a DTC brand. A DTC brand has to pay to generate awareness, pay to drive consideration, and pay to convert the click. Top, middle, and bottom of funnel. All three lines are the brand's. The retailer's footprint is gone, and so is the retailer's gravitational pull on shoppers who were never going to seek the brand out.

CAC and LTV: the test the DTC model has to pass

Two acronyms make this concrete. CAC is customer acquisition cost, what the brand pays in advertising, content, and discovery to convert one buyer. LTV is customer lifetime value, the total margin one buyer is expected to produce over the years they keep buying. A common rule of thumb is that a healthy business needs an LTV-to-CAC ratio of at least 3 to 1: every dollar spent acquiring a customer should earn three dollars of margin back over time. DTC raises the CAC line directly because the brand pays for all funnel stages. The model only works if LTV justifies it through repeat purchase, subscription, premium price, or a strong basket lift over time. Many DTC brands have failed because they assumed LTV would justify CAC and discovered, slowly, that it would not.

Tip When you read a brand's financials, look at the cost lines wholesale brands largely do not have: fulfillment, freight, returns, customer service, and paid marketing as a percentage of revenue. If digital marketing is consuming 25 to 35% of revenue and gross margin isn't extraordinary, the model is straining.
05
A Cautionary Case

The Allbirds Reality Check

Allbirds was the DTC darling of the late 2010s. Sustainability story, distinctive product, clean brand, IPO in November 2021 at roughly a $4 billion valuation. By early 2026, the picture had inverted. The company announced it would close all remaining US full-price retail stores by the end of February 2026, leaving only two outlet locations (Allbirds, 2025). Net revenue had fallen 23.3% year over year in the third quarter of 2025, to $33.0 million for the quarter. In April 2026, Allbirds agreed to be acquired by American Exchange Group for $39 million, paired with a planned wind-down of the company.

The lesson is not that DTC failed. Plenty of DTC brands continue to grow. The lesson is that DTC was sold as a structural advantage and turned out to be a channel choice with its own costs and risks. Allbirds' particular mistakes (over-expansion of physical stores, an unclear product strategy after the original wool runner, repeated promotional behavior that quietly eroded the premium price signal) are specific to Allbirds. But the general lesson is general. Removing the wholesaler does not remove the work. Customer-acquisition costs in pure DTC can rise faster than the margin gain. The model is not a moat.

06
In Practice

What Each Model Changes for IMC

A heavily wholesale brand and a heavily DTC brand can both run national campaigns. The composition of those campaigns should look almost nothing alike.

Model Where the IMC plan usually weights
Brand-owned DTC Performance media (paid search, paid social), retargeting, retention email, owned media, customer data investment
Wholesale Shopper marketing, retail media (Walmart Connect, Amazon Ads, Target Roundel), trade promotions, packaging-as-advertising
Marketplace Marketplace search ads, listing and SEO optimization, review management, fulfillment by marketplace
Distributor Trade marketing, sales materials, distributor incentives, in-store support, less direct consumer spending
Hybrid / omnichannel Channel coordination, conflict management, integrated planning across paid, owned, trade, and retail media

In Your Project

Diagnose the brand's channel mix before you write any judgments about its IMC plan. The mix decides several things you will be graded on.

  • Channel mix inside the IMC plan. Wholesale brands should be heavy in shopper and retail media. DTC brands should be heavy in performance and retention. Hybrid brands need a coordination story.
  • Data ownership. DTC brands own the customer relationship and the data. Wholesale brands often do not. That asymmetry affects measurement, attribution, and what the brand can plausibly know about its buyers.
  • CAC as a budget line. In DTC, customer acquisition cost is a first-order line item that competes with brand-building spend. In wholesale, much of the equivalent cost sits inside the retailer's margin and shopper-marketing spend instead.
  • Pricing consistency across channels. Heavy promotion on one channel without coordination on others erodes the price signal everywhere. This is one of the most common defects you will find when auditing a brand's PESO mix.

Research Prompts

Use the Find the Facts queries to source hard data about the brand's distribution. Use the Think It Through prompts only after you have the data. AI is helpful for interpretation and unreliable for invention.

Find the Facts

  • SEC EDGAR for public companies: 10-K and 10-Q filings disclose channel mix, gross margin, marketing spend as a share of revenue, store counts, and segment revenue
  • The brand's own website: store locator, retailer list, and "where to buy" pages reveal the wholesale footprint
  • Trade publications: Modern Retail, Retail Brew, Marketing Brew, RetailWire publish regular channel-mix analyses
  • Census Bureau: census.gov/retail/ecommerce.html for current quarterly e-commerce share data
  • eMarketer, Statista, and Marketplace Pulse publish marketplace and retailer share estimates

Think It Through

  • "What share of [brand]'s revenue comes from each channel (DTC, wholesale, marketplace, distributor)? How has the mix changed over the last three to five years?"
  • "If [brand] is heavily DTC, what is its CAC, what is the estimated LTV, and what is the ratio? Where does that put the model relative to a 3-to-1 benchmark?"
  • "If [brand] is heavily wholesale, how much of the IMC budget should be going into shopper marketing and retail media versus brand-building advertising?"
  • "If the project pivot would shift [brand]'s channel mix (adding DTC, expanding wholesale, exiting one), what new costs would the brand absorb, and what existing costs would it avoid?"

Vocabulary

Glossary

DTC (Direct-to-Consumer)
Selling directly to the end buyer through brand-owned channels (own website, own stores). Removes the wholesaler or retailer from the transaction, but not the work that party was doing.
E-commerce
Any retail transaction completed online, regardless of seller. Includes Amazon, Walmart.com, Target.com, retailer sites, marketplaces, and brand DTC. Roughly 16.9% of US retail in early 2026.
Marketplace
A platform that hosts third-party sellers (Amazon, eBay, Walmart Marketplace, Etsy). The platform handles discovery, payment, and often fulfillment. The brand is a tenant, not the landlord.
Wholesale
Selling product to retailers who resell it. The retailer pays a wholesale price below the consumer price and captures the difference, along with the customer relationship at point of sale.
Distributor
An intermediary between brand and retailers, common in beverages, foods, and B2B. Handles logistics and retailer relationships in exchange for a substantial cut of revenue.
Keystone pricing
A retail markup convention in which the consumer price is set at double the wholesale price (a 100% markup). Common in apparel, accessories, and softer-goods categories; less common in grocery, where margins are far thinner.
Omnichannel
A coordinated strategy where multiple channels (DTC, wholesale, marketplace, retail) work together rather than competing. Requires deliberate planning and conflict management.
Channel conflict
The tension that arises when a brand's channels compete with each other on price, exclusives, or audience. Most acute when wholesale partners feel undercut by the brand's own DTC pricing.
Shopper marketing
Marketing activity aimed at the point of purchase: in-store displays, packaging, in-aisle media, retailer-specific promotions, co-op advertising. Predominantly a wholesale-brand discipline.
Retail media
Paid advertising sold by retailers on their own properties (Walmart Connect, Amazon Ads, Target Roundel, Kroger Precision Marketing). One of the fastest-growing categories of digital ad spend.
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
The total marketing and sales cost to win one new buyer. In DTC, a first-order line on the income statement. In wholesale, much of the equivalent work sits inside the retailer's margin.
LTV (Customer Lifetime Value)
The total margin a customer is expected to produce over the time they keep buying from the brand. A common health benchmark is an LTV-to-CAC ratio of at least 3 to 1.
