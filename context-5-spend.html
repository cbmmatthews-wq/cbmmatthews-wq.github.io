The 5 to 10 Percent Rule, and Why It Needs an Asterisk

A line in nearly every intro marketing textbook says brands should spend roughly 5 to 10% of revenue on marketing. The rule is useful as a starting point. It is also wrong in most specific cases.

01
The Rule

Where the 5 to 10 Percent Comes From

The closest thing to an authoritative anchor for the rule is Gartner's annual CMO Spend Survey, which tracks marketing budgets at large companies. The 2025 edition found the average marketing budget was 7.7% of revenue, unchanged from 2024 (Gartner, 2025). That number sits comfortably inside the textbook range.

The 7.7% figure hides as much as it reveals. Half of the CMOs surveyed reported budgets of 6% or less. "Big spenders" exceed 10.5% of revenue. "Budget-conscious" CMOs sit at 4% or less. They operate in completely different financial worlds. The Gartner sample is also heavily weighted toward companies with over $1 billion in annual revenue, which is to say the rule, as confirmed, is a large-company rule. Apply it to a venture-funded startup or a small regional brand and the answer is unlikely to fit.

02
Definitions Matter

What "Marketing Spend" Actually Means

Before applying any benchmark, you have to know what is being counted. "Marketing spend" can mean any of the following, and the answer changes by an order of magnitude depending on which one:

  • Total marketing budget. Paid media, agencies, martech, internal labor, creative production, research.
  • Advertising or paid-media budget alone. What's spent placing ads.
  • Sales promotion and trade allowances. Funding for retailer discounts, displays, and co-op advertising (covered on the Promotion Funding page).
  • Sponsorships and partnerships. Events, athletes, properties, influencers under contract.
  • Marketing staff and overhead. Salaries, benefits, and the cost of running the marketing department itself.

Gartner's 7.7% figure covers the broad total. Within that total, paid media is only 30.6%, or 2.4% of company revenue. So when a textbook says "spend 5 to 10% on advertising," it usually means the broad total including agencies and tech. When a CMO says "we run a 2% media budget," they mean paid media specifically. The two numbers can disagree by a factor of three or four.

Tip When evaluating a brand's marketing investment, specify what you're measuring. "[Brand] spends 8% on marketing" is meaningless until you know whether that's paid media, total marketing including labor, or total selling expense. If you cannot find a clean disclosure, say so explicitly. (See Tool B: Evidence Ladder.)

The table understates the spread at smaller scales. Venture-funded startups and pure-play DTC companies routinely exceed 20% of revenue on marketing in their early years. Luxury brands often spend in the 10 to 15% range, supported by gross margins the table's sample doesn't fully capture. Mass retailers with thin margins run on lower marketing ratios than the table shows, because their absolute spend is large but their revenue base is much larger still. The Gartner data is a useful benchmark for large incumbents. It is a misleading anchor for almost everyone else.

04
The Underlying Mechanic

Margin Sets the Ceiling

The single biggest reason the 7.7% average fails to predict any given brand's marketing budget is gross margin. A brand's gross margin is what's left after the cost of producing the product is deducted from revenue. Everything else (marketing, sales, overhead, profit) comes out of that.

LVMH operates at roughly 66% gross margin across its 75 Maisons. Each €100 of revenue leaves €66 to fund everything that isn't manufacturing. The group spent approximately €9.5 billion on advertising and promotion in 2024, around 11.5% of revenue (Robin Report, 2025). The model handles that level of spend because the margin makes room.

A grocery retailer operates at roughly 25 to 30% gross margin and 2 to 4% net margin. The math literally does not allow it to spend 11.5% of revenue on marketing without going out of business. Different gross-margin structures impose different ceilings on what marketing can plausibly cost. This is the link from Page 3 (where the brand sits on price tier) to Page 5 (what it can sustainably spend). A premium or prestige brand's margin advantage is what unlocks higher marketing investment in the first place. Move a brand down-market and the budget that supported its old positioning collapses with the margin.

Four economics terms you need

Four terms shape the math behind any marketing budget claim. You do not need to do the calculations. You do need to know what the words mean.

  • Gross margin. Revenue minus the cost of producing the product, expressed as a percentage. The first and hardest ceiling on what marketing can cost.
  • Contribution margin. Gross margin minus variable costs (shipping, transaction fees, returns). What each sale actually contributes toward fixed costs and profit. Often lower than gross margin, sometimes dramatically.
  • CAC (customer acquisition cost) and LTV (customer lifetime value). Defined and illustrated on the Distribution page. The DTC stress test on whatever margin is left after acquisition.
Tip Before judging a brand's marketing spend, look up or estimate its gross margin. If the brand's marketing-to-revenue ratio looks high, check whether the margin makes it sustainable. If it looks low, check whether the margin permits more. Most "the brand should spend more on marketing" recommendations from students collapse the moment the margin numbers come out.
05
In Practice

Three Real Deviations from the Rule

Three concrete cases where the 5-to-10-percent rule does not hold, and why.

Startup or DTC turnaround: 25 to 40%

Allbirds reported marketing expense of $32.3 million on $105 million of revenue for the first nine months of 2025, or 30.8% of revenue (Allbirds, 2025). That is roughly four times the Gartner large-company average. This is not a sustainable benchmark. It is the financial signature of a brand in distress, trying to spend its way back to growth, with limited time before the cash runs out. Several DTC startups have run similar ratios over the last decade. Most have not made the math work. (See the Allbirds case on the Distribution page for what happened next.)

Luxury and high-margin: 10 to 15%

LVMH's roughly 11.5% of revenue on advertising and promotion is well above the Gartner industry averages because gross margin permits it. Premium and prestige brands routinely spend in the 10-to-15% range. The output isn't a bigger sales pop per dollar. It is the construction and defense of a brand that justifies the price in the first place. Cut the marketing and the price premium starts to erode. The marketing spend is, in part, what the buyer is paying for when they pay the luxury markup.

Low-margin retail and mass CPG: 2 to 4%

Walmart, Costco, and Kroger run marketing budgets in the low single digits as a percentage of revenue. Their scale and gross-margin structures do not permit more. They make up for it with foot traffic, distribution power, scan data, and private-label leverage. A startup looking at Walmart's marketing-to-revenue ratio and concluding "we should spend like Walmart" is reading the wrong benchmark. Walmart's absolute marketing spend is enormous; its ratio to revenue is small because its revenue is hundreds of billions of dollars. Ratios compare brands of similar margin structure. Across very different margin structures, they mislead.

In Your Project

The 7.7% benchmark is a useful sanity check and a poor blueprint. Before defending any recommended marketing budget in your project, work through these:

  • State what you mean by "marketing spend." Total budget, paid media only, sales promotion, or some mix. If you cannot define it, you cannot defend the number.
  • Anchor against the right benchmark. If the brand is a startup or DTC, use 15-30%, not 7.7%. If it's a mass retailer, use 2-4%. The Gartner average is a large-incumbent number.
  • Pressure-test against gross margin. A recommended marketing budget that consumes a quarter of the brand's gross margin is questionable. A recommended budget that exceeds gross margin is wrong.
  • Defend the deviation explicitly. If your recommendation puts the brand above or below benchmark, name why. New segment entry, defending a price premium, recovering from share loss, and absorbing CAC from a channel shift are all defensible. "Because it's a big brand" is not.

Research Prompts

Use the Find the Facts queries to source hard data about the brand's financials. Use the Think It Through prompts only after you have the data.

Find the Facts

  • SEC EDGAR for public companies: 10-K and 10-Q filings disclose total marketing or selling expense as a line item, and increasingly disclose "marketing expense" specifically
  • Earnings call transcripts (Motley Fool, Seeking Alpha) often discuss marketing investment as a percentage of revenue
  • Gartner CMO Spend Survey: published annually in May, free summary at gartner.com under marketing research
  • Deloitte CMO Survey: alternative biannual benchmark, with US sample skew different from Gartner's
  • Industry-trade analyses (AdAge, Marketing Brew, Modern Retail) often report category-specific marketing ratios
  • For private brands: estimate using gross margin and known media activity rather than guessing a percentage

Think It Through

  • "What is [brand]'s reported marketing expense, and what does that include or exclude (paid media, agency fees, internal labor, trade allowances)?"
  • "What is [brand]'s gross margin, and what ceiling does that put on what it can sustainably spend on marketing?"
  • "Where does [brand]'s marketing-to-revenue ratio sit against the Gartner industry benchmark for its sector? If above or below, what explains the deviation?"
  • "If the project pivot would change [brand]'s segment, price tier, or channel mix, how would that change the sustainable marketing budget?"

Vocabulary

Glossary

Marketing budget
The total annual cost of running a brand's marketing function. Includes paid media, agencies, martech, internal labor, creative production, and research. Distinct from advertising or paid-media budget alone, which is a subset.
Advertising (or paid-media) budget
The portion of marketing spend that goes to placing paid ads in media. Around 30.6% of the total marketing budget on the Gartner 2025 average, or 2.4% of company revenue.
Gross margin
Revenue minus the cost of goods sold, as a percentage of revenue. The first ceiling on what marketing can sustainably cost. A 70% gross-margin brand has far more room to spend on marketing than a 25% gross-margin brand.
Contribution margin
Gross margin minus variable costs per unit (shipping, transaction fees, returns). What each sale actually contributes toward fixed costs and profit. Often meaningfully lower than gross margin, especially in DTC.
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
The marketing and sales cost to win one new customer. Defined in detail on the Distribution page.
LTV (Customer Lifetime Value)
The total margin a customer is expected to produce over the years they keep buying. A healthy business typically requires an LTV-to-CAC ratio of at least 3 to 1. See the Distribution page.
"Big spender" and "budget-conscious"
Gartner's working terms for CMOs whose marketing budgets exceed 10.5% of revenue versus those at or below 4%. They face very different strategic constraints despite running marketing for companies of similar size.
