Project Context · 06

The Sale Tag Has a Payer

When a $26 sweater goes on sale at 30% off, someone is paying for that $7.80 discount. Producer, retailer, wholesaler, or a negotiated mix. Knowing which one matters more than students usually realize, because it changes who has the leverage and what the discount is actually doing to the brand.

01
The Argument

The Discount Is Real Money. The Payer Decides What It Means.

Sales promotion is the second-largest line on most consumer-goods P&L statements, behind only the cost of producing the product itself. It is also the line marketers understand least well, partly because the funding can come from three different places and partly because the word "promotion" gets used as a catchall for very different mechanics.

The two distinctions that matter most for this project: consumer promotions versus trade promotions, and who actually pays. Pull those apart and a lot of the mystery around discounting disappears. So does the assumption that promotional intensity is automatically a sign of strength.

02
The Taxonomy

Two Types of Promotions

Promotions split cleanly into two families based on who they target. The mechanics, the funding, and the strategic effect of each are different.

Aimed at the Shopper
Consumer Promotions
  • Coupons (paper, digital, app-triggered)
  • Rebates (mail-in, instant)
  • Loyalty offers (points, tiered rewards)
  • Samples and trial sizes
  • Buy-one-get-one (BOGO) and bundle pricing
  • Temporary price reductions (sticker shelf discounts)
  • Sweepstakes, contests, gift-with-purchase
Aimed at the Retailer or Distributor
Trade Promotions
  • Off-invoice allowances (price cut at the wholesale invoice)
  • Bill-backs and scan-backs (rebates paid after sale)
  • Display and feature allowances (paid for shelf or endcap placement)
  • Co-op advertising (manufacturer funds retailer ads)
  • Slotting fees (paid for new-product shelf space)
  • Volume incentives (rebates for hitting purchase tiers)
  • Free goods and markdown support

The difference is more than where the money lands. Consumer promotions move shoppers. Trade promotions move retailers, which then either pass the value to shoppers or absorb it into their own margins. A manufacturer running a trade promotion has limited control over whether the discount ever reaches the shelf. That is one of the central frustrations of CPG marketing.

03
The Mechanics

Who Actually Funds the Discount?

The honest answer is: it depends on the mechanism, and it is often a negotiated combination. A few simplified cases:

  • The retailer marks down excess inventory. The retailer eats the markdown out of its own margin. This is the case when a season ends and the retailer has units left.
  • The manufacturer offers a temporary off-invoice allowance. The manufacturer funds the discount at the wholesale level. The retailer can pass all, some, or none of it to shoppers. Many do not pass it through fully, which the manufacturer cannot prevent.
  • A retailer runs a loyalty-card promotion with vendor support. The cost is typically shared. Manufacturer funds a portion through scan-backs or co-op dollars; retailer covers the rest.
  • A distributor administers the discount and charges it back. The producer ultimately funds it through a chargeback against future invoices. The distributor is the operator, not the payer.
  • A co-op advertising campaign. Jointly funded under a contractual formula, often 50/50 or 75/25 manufacturer-to-retailer.
Tip Most retail promotions involve several payers, not one. When you see a "30% off" sign in a store, the producer, the retailer, and possibly a distributor are usually all contributing something. Following the dollars is the only way to know whether the promotion is sustainable for the brand or quietly destructive.
04
The Reality Check

Trade Promotions Are Huge, and Most of Them Lose Money

The scale of trade promotion spending surprises most non-marketers. McKinsey's research found that CPG companies worldwide invest around 20% of revenue in trade promotions annually, with various sectors reporting 15-to-25% as typical and some categories running higher (McKinsey, 2019). For most CPG companies, trade spend is the second-largest line item on the income statement, behind only cost of goods sold.

Read that one more time. The second-largest line on the P&L, and the majority of it loses money on a per-event basis. The reasons are structural: events get repeated because they are familiar, not because the data supports them. Retailers demand promotional support that brands cannot easily refuse. Measurement of incrementality (how many of the units sold during a promotion would have sold anyway) is weak at most CPG companies. The dollars get spent and the analysis happens later, if at all.

The implication for the project is straightforward. If your brand operates in a category where trade spend is heavy, do not assume the spend is producing growth. Ask which promotions are running and whether the brand has any data on what they actually return.

05
A Cautionary Case

How a Discount Habit Quietly Damages a Brand

The story of J.Crew over the 2010s is the clean case study for why promotional intensity is not neutral. The brand was, in the early 2010s, a culturally dominant American specialty retailer with a recognizable preppy point of view. Then a leveraged buyout in 2011 added roughly $1.7 billion in debt, and the company began promoting more aggressively to drive sales. The pattern compounded over years.

Case · The J.Crew Discount Spiral

By the late 2010s, customers had been "trained to expect 70% off" on J.Crew merchandise (Marie Claire, 2020). The pattern, in summary form: "Frequent discounting trained customers to wait for a markdown. Sales tumbled."

The reference price had moved. The sweater that listed at $98 had been on sale at $39 too often. A shopper who waited a few weeks could expect 50% off. A shopper who waited a few months could expect 70%. The list price had become fiction.

J.Crew filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in May 2020 with $1.7 billion in debt. The pandemic was the trigger. The discount spiral was the underlying condition. As of early 2026, the brand is again at risk, with S&P downgrading the parent company in late 2025 after another quarter of declines.

The mechanism is what marketing economists call reference-price erosion. Each promotion teaches the buyer that this is what the product is actually worth, and the next "regular" price looks inflated. The brand is not selling at a discount. It is selling at the discount price, and pretending the list price still means something. Premium positioning unwinds first. Then quality inferences ("if it discounts this often, it must not be very good"). Then margin. Then the brand.

06
In Practice

What Promotions Actually Do (and Don't)

Promotions are not a neutral tool. They produce specific effects, some intended, some not. The serious ones to track:

  • Incremental sales vs. subsidized purchases. A promotion that moves units only because the buyer would have bought anyway is subsidizing existing demand, not creating new sales. The hardest measurement in promotional analytics.
  • Stockpiling and pantry loading. Heavy discounts encourage buyers to load up. Future demand gets pulled forward, not added. Next month's sales drop accordingly.
  • Cannibalization. A promoted SKU often steals sales from the brand's other SKUs rather than from competitors. Total brand revenue can stay flat while one product looks like a winner.
  • Trial. The legitimate strategic use of consumer promotion: get a first-time buyer to try a new product. Works best when paired with a product that earns repeat purchase on its own merits.
  • Retailer traffic. Promotions can drive shoppers into a store or onto a site. The brand effectively subsidizes the retailer's foot traffic.
  • Reference-price erosion. The J.Crew effect. Each promotion shifts what buyers think the product should cost.
  • Training the buyer to wait. Predictable promotion cycles teach shoppers when to expect the next markdown. Demand collapses in the gaps.
  • Conflict with premium positioning. A brand that sells on prestige or premium quality erodes its positioning every time it discounts visibly. Prestige brands rarely discount in public for this reason.
One Legal Boundary

Manufacturers cannot necessarily give whatever promotional support they want to one retailer while denying comparable support to competing retailers. Under US trade-discrimination rules, promotional allowances and services generally must be made available to competing customers on proportionally equal terms (FTC). The full rules are complex and not on the syllabus. The single sentence to retain: preferential promotional terms have legal limits.

In Your Project

Promotion is where IMC meets the operating P&L. Several judgments in your final deck depend on getting this right.

  • Audit the brand's current promotion behavior. Frequency, depth (10% off versus 50% off), whether it's predictable, and whether it's coordinated across channels. A brand that promotes constantly in one channel and never in another is creating channel conflict.
  • Diagnose consumer versus trade. If the brand sells through retailers, trade promotions are part of its P&L whether the project deck mentions them or not. Ask what share of revenue trade allowances consume.
  • Check for reference-price erosion. If list prices and selling prices have diverged systematically, the brand is in the J.Crew zone. Your pivot recommendation should address this, not assume the list price still functions.
  • Match promotion to position. A prestige brand running BOGO is contradicting its own positioning. A discount brand not promoting is leaving sales on the table. The promotion strategy should reinforce the price tier from Page 3, not undermine it.

Research Prompts

Use the Find the Facts queries to source hard data about the brand's promotion patterns. Use the Think It Through prompts only after you have the data.

Find the Facts

  • Sign up for the brand's email list and observe promotion frequency, depth, and timing over a month
  • Search the brand's website for "sale," "promo," or "clearance" landing pages and document what's typical
  • SEC EDGAR for public CPG companies: 10-K disclosure of "trade promotion" or "selling allowances" as a contra-revenue line
  • Trade publications: Modern Retail, Marketing Brew, and NielsenIQ often report on promotional intensity by category
  • Honey, RetailMeNot, and similar coupon aggregators show the brand's promotional history
  • FTC Guides on Promotional Allowances at ftc.gov for the legal boundary discussion

Think It Through

  • "How often does [brand] discount, and how deeply? Is the pattern predictable enough that buyers can wait for the next promotion?"
  • "Does [brand] participate in trade promotions through retailers (display allowances, co-op, scan-backs)? What share of its revenue is consumed by those programs?"
  • "Is [brand]'s list price still its selling price most of the time, or has reference-price erosion set in?"
  • "If the project pivot would change [brand]'s segment or tier, would the brand need to reduce promotional intensity to support the new position, or increase it?"

Vocabulary

Glossary

Consumer promotion
A promotional offer aimed at the end shopper: coupons, rebates, BOGO, loyalty offers, samples, sweepstakes. The shopper sees and responds to the offer directly.
Trade promotion
A promotional offer or allowance aimed at the retailer or distributor: off-invoice allowances, scan-backs, display and feature allowances, co-op advertising, slotting fees, volume incentives. The shopper may never see these directly, but they often fund the in-store discounts the shopper sees.
Off-invoice allowance
A trade promotion mechanic where the manufacturer reduces the wholesale invoice price for a defined period. The retailer pockets the difference unless they choose to pass it through to shoppers.
Scan-back
A trade promotion mechanic where the manufacturer pays the retailer a defined amount for each unit scanned at register during a promotional window. More controllable than off-invoice but more administratively complex.
Co-op advertising
An arrangement under which the manufacturer and retailer jointly fund advertising for the brand at the retailer's location. Typically governed by a contractual formula (e.g., 50/50, 75/25).
Slotting fee
A one-time payment from manufacturer to retailer for shelf space for a new product. Common in supermarket and mass-channel CPG. Effectively a price of entry to the shelf.
Reference price
The price a buyer expects to pay for a category or brand, formed through experience. Repeated discounting moves the reference price downward, after which the list price stops functioning as the brand's actual price signal.
Incrementality
The portion of promoted sales that would not have happened without the promotion. The opposite of subsidized demand. Notoriously difficult to measure cleanly, which is part of why so many trade promotions lose money.
Stockpiling (pantry loading)
Buyer behavior in which a shopper purchases more units than usual during a promotion, then buys nothing for an extended period. Future demand pulled forward rather than added.
Cannibalization
Sales of a promoted product that come from the same brand's other products rather than from competitors. Total brand revenue unchanged or down; individual SKU revenue up.
