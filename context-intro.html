Project Context · Intro

Background Before You Diagnose

A short series of six diagnostic pages and two project tools, written to give a group with mixed marketing backgrounds a common analytical baseline before they audit their assigned brand and propose a pivot. Read in any order. Apply to the project as you go.

01
Purpose

What These Pages Are For

The class has thirteen students with no formal marketing background and three with substantial industry experience. The project asks all of them to audit a brand against the IMC concepts already covered in the course (PESO, the funnel, brand-versus-performance, repetition, 60/40, creativity, social), then pivot the brand to a new segment with a new communications objective.

The diagnosis half of the project routinely surfaces gaps that are not really about IMC. They are about context: how the category is bought, how the brand is growing or shrinking, where it sits on price tier, how it gets to the buyer, what it sustainably spends, and how it discounts. Without that context, the IMC analysis floats. It might be technically right and strategically empty.

The series fills that gap. It is not new material on top of what the course already covers. It is the connective tissue that makes the existing material apply to a specific brand in a specific situation.

02
The Series

The Six Content Pages

Each page closes the same way: a dark box titled What This Changes in Your Project, listing the IMC concepts the page should make you reconsider. That box is the link from these readings back to your final deliverable. Reading times below are estimated at 220 words per minute for dense reference material.

01 · Category
The Category Changes the Marketing
How frequently the category is bought, what occasion triggers it, and how much research it requires. Why a mattress and a bag of chips need entirely different funnels.
1,587 words · 7 min read
02 · Growth Diagnosis
Launch, Scale, Defend, or Revive?
Category condition crossed with brand condition. Where most communications objectives actually come from. Plus the targeting-width and audience-temperature decision that translates penetration theory into a real media plan.
2,188 words · 10 min read
03 · Position & Price
Where Does the Brand Sit, and What Does Its Price Signal?
Mass, niche, premium, prestige. Three different questions that get confused for one. Includes a short version of the Zone of Indifference treatment.
1,630 words · 7.5 min read
04 · Distribution
DTC, Retail, or Both?
What direct-to-consumer actually costs. Why e-commerce and DTC are not the same thing. The value chain on one product, the CAC-LTV test, and what functions a brand absorbs when it removes the retailer.
2,648 words · 12 min read
05 · Marketing Spend
The 5 to 10 Percent Rule, and Why It Needs an Asterisk
Marketing budget as a share of revenue. Why the rule of thumb breaks for startups, DTC, and luxury. Four economics terms you need without the math.
1,710 words · 8 min read
06 · Promotion Funding
The Sale Tag Has a Payer
Who funds discounts: producer, retailer, wholesaler, or a negotiated mix. Consumer promotions versus trade promotions. Why a 20-percent-off coupon can quietly damage a brand.
1,796 words · 8 min read
03
Project Tools

Two Short Tools You Will Actually Use

The two tools sit alongside the content pages. They are not readings. They are checklists to apply while you write the second half of the project: the recommendation. Use them on every claim, not just the easy ones.

Tool A · Segment Feasibility
A New Target Is Not Automatically a Good Target
A seven-test scorecard for the segment you propose to pivot toward. Need, value, permission, product fit, price fit, access, and conflict with the existing base. Worked example: Hoka into formal footwear.
1,734 words · 8 min read
Tool B · Evidence Ladder
What You Know, What You Can Infer, What You Should Not Pretend to Know
A five-level evidence ladder for any claim about PESO mix, 60/40 split, ad spend, distribution, or competitor strategy. Forces honesty about what is a fact versus a guess in a tie.
1,715 words · 8 min read

Total series reading time, if read straight through: approximately 73 minutes. Most students will not read straight through. They will skim, return when stuck, and re-read the sections that apply to their specific brand.

04
How to Use Them

Patterns You Will See on Every Page

Each content page repeats the same closing pattern so you know what to expect.

  • What This Changes in Your Project. Dark box at the end of the body. Bullets that translate the page back into the rubric: which IMC concept it should make you reconsider, and how.
  • Research Prompts. Pale blue box at the foot. Two subsections: Find the Facts lists Google or research queries to find hard numbers about your brand. Think It Through lists prompts to give an AI assistant once you have the numbers. The distinction matters. AI is useful for interpreting data you sourced yourself, and unreliable when you ask it to invent the data.
  • Glossary. A small panel covering only that page's vocabulary. Per-page so it works whether you land on the intro or jump straight to a section.
  • References. APA citations, clickable. A bracketed author-year in the text jumps to the entry at the bottom. The arrow next to each reference returns you to the spot in the text.

Glossary and references live at the foot of each page. Previous and next buttons sit between the research box and the glossary, so you can move forward without scrolling past the reference material to find them.

05
Reading Order

Read in Any Order

The six content pages are independent. You can read them in the listed order or jump straight to the one most relevant to your assigned brand. The two tools should be applied throughout the second half of the project, not read once and set aside.

If you do want a default sequence, the order on the page above (Category → Growth → Position & Price → Distribution → Marketing Spend → Promotion Funding → Tool A → Tool B) moves roughly from the outside of the brand inward: from how the category behaves, to how the brand sits inside the category, to what the brand sustainably spends, to what it discounts, and finally to the two analytical disciplines that govern the recommendation half of the project.

Use whichever path gets you to a defensible deck. The series exists to make that easier.
