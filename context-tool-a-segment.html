Project Tool · A

A New Target Is Not Automatically a Good Target

A seven-test scorecard for the segment you propose to pivot toward. Apply it to every candidate segment, not just the obvious ones. This is the structure of the second half of your project deck.

01
Why This Tool Exists

The Problem the Scorecard Is Built to Catch

The second half of your project asks you to pick a new or adjacent segment, set an objective, and recommend a communications plan that wins there. The most common failure mode at this point is not bad creative work or weak media planning. It is assuming a communications change can fix a product, price, or distribution mismatch.

A pivot recommendation that reads "we should target [new segment] with [new creative angle] in [new channel]" is not a pivot recommendation. It is a creative brief written before the segment was diagnosed. The scorecard below is the diagnosis the brief needs to be built on.

Seven tests. Each gets a pass, a partial, or a fail. The recommendation that follows must either pass all seven or name explicitly which it does not pass and how it plans to bridge the gap. "It is a great opportunity" is not a bridge.

02
The Scorecard

The Seven Tests

Walk every candidate segment through these in order. Do not skip ahead.

Test 1Need

Does this segment have a meaningful problem or desire the brand could address?

PassReal, articulable friction in the segment's life that current options do not solve well.
FailYou can describe the segment in detail but cannot articulate what they are not getting today.
Prompt"If we built nothing new, what hour of their day is this segment poorly served in?"

Test 2Value

Is the segment large enough, growing enough, or strategically useful enough to justify the work?

PassSizeable now, growing, or strategically important even if small (a learning bet, a defensive bet, a beachhead).
FailSmall, shrinking, and offers no strategic learning value beyond itself.
Prompt"How many real people are in this segment, how much do they spend, and is that number growing?"

Test 3Permission

Would this segment believe this brand if it showed up there?

PassThe brand has known associations the segment would credit or value.
FailThe brand would feel like a non-sequitur, or worse, like a stretch the brand has not earned.
Prompt"If the brand launched in this segment tomorrow with no announcement, would anyone understand why?"

Test 4Product Fit

Can the existing product (or a modest variant) actually satisfy this segment?

PassThe current product addresses the need well, or with light modification.
FailThe product would have to fundamentally change to serve the segment. New tooling, new materials, new format.
Prompt"If a buyer in this segment bought the product as it is today, would they like it enough to buy again?"

Test 5Price Fit

Can this segment afford the brand's price, and does the price say the right thing to them?

PassPrice is within the segment's reach, and the price signal matches what the segment values.
FailPrice is too high (unreachable), too low (signals wrong tier), or misaligned with how the segment shops.
Prompt"Does this segment shop in the brand's tier (discount, value, mainstream, premium, prestige)? See Page 3."

Test 6Access

Can the brand reach and serve this segment through realistic channels?

PassExisting channels touch the segment, or new ones could be added without disrupting the model.
FailThe brand would have to fundamentally rebuild distribution, sales, or service to access them.
Prompt"Where does this segment actually shop, and is the brand there or able to be there? See Page 4."

Test 7Conflict

Would pursuing this segment damage the existing customer base or position?

PassThe new segment can be served without alienating current buyers or diluting the position.
FailGoing after the new segment signals something that makes current buyers feel different about the brand. This is the most easily missed test.
Prompt"Would the brand's most loyal current customer feel anything is different about the brand if they saw the new positioning?"
03
Worked Example

Hoka Into Men's Formal Footwear

Hoka has built a successful athletic-footwear brand around its maximum-cushion running shoes. Could it pivot into men's formal footwear? Walk all seven tests. The results below are illustrative, not a real strategy recommendation.

Test 1 · Need: Pass. Dress shoes are notoriously uncomfortable, and brands like Cole Haan have built businesses on putting athletic-shoe technology inside formal designs. There is a real, articulable problem Hoka could plausibly address.

Test 2 · Value: Pass. Formal footwear is a multi-billion-dollar global category, mature but not shrinking among professional segments. Sizeable and strategically meaningful.

Test 3 · Permission: Partial. Hoka has very strong comfort and performance permission. It has effectively no formal or dress permission. A buyer might credit Hoka for engineering comfort into a formal shoe but would not credit it for fashion or formality. The brand can stretch along the dimension of its core meaning (comfort) but not against it (athletic visual codes).

Test 4 · Product Fit: Fail. The existing product is athletic. A formal shoe requires different last shapes, leather construction, sole materials, and finishing methods. Hoka would need a substantially new product line, not a modification of the current one. The existing factories and SKU base do not cover this.

Test 5 · Price Fit: Partial. Hoka sits in the $140-200 premium athletic range. Entry-level premium dress shoes start around $300, real premium runs $500+, and luxury is $800+. Hoka could plausibly enter the lower premium dress end but would have to navigate price expectations carefully against incumbent premium dress brands.

Test 6 · Access: Partial fail. Hoka's wholesale relationships (specialty running stores, REI, Dick's Sporting Goods) and its DTC channel do not reach the formal-footwear buyer. New wholesale partners (Nordstrom, Saks, formal-wear retailers) would be required, along with new DTC merchandising and likely a different sub-site or product environment.

Test 7 · Conflict: Risk. Hoka's brand currently signals serious athletic performance. A formal extension risks diluting that signal. Loyal Hoka runners may feel the brand has lost its focus. The On comparison from the existing Hoka/On page is instructive: On has carefully navigated lifestyle extensions through tennis collabs and lifestyle silhouettes that still feel like running shoes, but even On has not crossed into formal footwear.

Diagnosis

Two clean passes (Need, Value). Two partials (Permission, Price Fit). One partial fail (Access). One outright fail (Product Fit). One risk (Conflict).

The pivot is not impossible. It cannot be executed through a communications change alone. New product development, new wholesale partners, careful pricing decisions, and disciplined brand stewardship would all be required. The most defensible path is probably a sub-brand or distinct product line rather than the core Hoka name. The communications work is the last 10% of this pivot, not the whole pivot.

The teaching point is the structure of the work. A pivot recommendation that names which tests it passes, which it does not, and how it plans to bridge the gaps is a serious recommendation. A pivot recommendation that asserts the new segment is "a great opportunity" without doing this work is what gets returned with edits.

04
How to Use It

What to Do with the Scorecard

The scorecard is meant to produce one of three outcomes for any candidate segment. Each outcome implies different next steps in your project work.

  • Seven clean passes. Rare in practice. If you genuinely got there, the pivot is well-aimed and the communications work in Part 2 can carry the recommendation. Defend the diagnosis briefly in your deck and move on to the brief.
  • One or two partial failures. The most common outcome, and the most useful. Name them. Explain how the recommendation bridges them (modest product variant, new pricing tier, sub-brand naming, new channel partner). The deck shows you saw the gaps and chose to cross them deliberately.
  • Three or more partial or full failures. The candidate segment is not the right pivot. Pick another. Forcing a segment that fails the scorecard produces a recommendation that has to defend itself against four problems instead of solving one.

The scorecard is for diagnosis, not justification. If you find yourself adjusting the criteria to make a favorite segment "pass," you are using the tool backwards.

In Your Project

The scorecard is the structure your pivot recommendation in Part 2 should be built on.

  • Run two or three candidate segments through the seven tests before committing. The first segment you think of is rarely the strongest candidate.
  • Show the diagnosis in the deck. Even a one-slide summary of which tests pass and which do not signals to the grader that the recommendation is built on analysis, not instinct.
  • Be honest about the fails. A pivot that admits one Product Fit failure and explains how to bridge it is stronger than a pivot that pretends seven passes when three are partials.
  • Use the prompts. The diagnostic prompts in each test are designed to be the questions you ask yourself (or an AI assistant) when stuck. Treat them as the work, not as decoration.

Research Prompts

For each test, the Find the Facts queries point you to data sources. The Think It Through prompts are the per-test diagnostic questions in a form you can paste into an AI assistant.

Find the Facts

  • Segment sizing: Statista, Mintel, NielsenIQ, IBISWorld, government census data, and industry-association reports
  • Segment behavior: trade publications and category-specific newsletters (Beverage Digest, BedTimes, Modern Retail, Business of Fashion, Footwear News)
  • Brand permission: customer reviews, social listening, brand-tracking studies (YouGov BrandIndex, Morning Consult, Kantar BrandZ)
  • Price benchmarks: direct retailer site walks, price-tracking services, category aggregators
  • Channel mapping: brand's "where to buy" page, retailer locator tools, distributor directories

Think It Through

  • "For [brand]'s proposed pivot into [segment], walk the seven feasibility tests and grade each as pass, partial, or fail with one sentence of reasoning."
  • "Which two tests are the binding constraints on this pivot, and what would have to change for them to flip from partial or fail to pass?"
  • "Generate two alternative candidate segments for [brand]. Run the same seven tests on each. Which of the three is the strongest pivot?"
  • "If the recommendation fails Conflict, what specifically does it signal to current loyal customers, and what could mitigate that?"

Vocabulary

Glossary

Segment pivot
A deliberate shift of the brand's targeting from its current customer base to a new or adjacent group of buyers. The second half of the M544 project asks for one of these.
Brand permission
The set of meanings and associations a brand carries that determine which extensions feel credible to buyers. A brand can stretch along the dimension of its core meaning, but stretching against it usually fails.
Brand stretch
The act of extending a brand into a new product category, segment, or use case. Stretches succeed when permission holds and product, price, and channel can follow. Failures are usually rooted in one of those four.
Sub-brand
A distinct brand name (or modifier) used when the core brand cannot credibly enter a new segment but the parent company still wants to. Toyota's Lexus, Gap's Old Navy, P&G's Olay are all examples.
Channel conflict
The tension that arises when a brand's existing distribution partners feel undercut by a new channel or segment strategy. Covered in the Distribution page.
Binding constraint
The single test or condition that decides whether a pivot can work, holding everything else equal. The most useful question to ask of a scorecard with multiple partial fails is "which one is the binding constraint?"
