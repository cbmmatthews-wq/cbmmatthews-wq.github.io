Project Tool · B

The Evidence Ladder

Five levels of evidence for every claim in your project deck. The point of the ladder is not to find perfect data. The point is to label honestly which rung your evidence actually sits on.

01
Why This Tool Exists

The Honest Number Beats the Confident One

There is a specific kind of sentence that turns up in nearly every student deck:

"The brand spends 70% on brand and 30% on performance."

The sentence is precise. The sentence is confident. The sentence is, in nine cases out of ten, completely made up. Real brands rarely disclose this split. Public companies disclose it in aggregated form at best. Private companies disclose nothing.

The problem is not that the student is lying. The problem is that the deck does not distinguish between things the student knows, things the student can infer, and things the student is guessing. A grader reading the deck cannot tell which is which. The result reads as overconfident at best and fabricated at worst.

The evidence ladder fixes this with a single discipline: every consequential claim in the deck gets labeled with the level of evidence behind it. Five levels. The label takes one word. The intellectual honesty it produces is the difference between a deck that gets returned with edits and a deck that gets graded.

02
The Five Levels

The Five Rungs of Evidence

Levels 1 through 4 are claims the deck can defend. Level 5 is the honest acknowledgment that the deck cannot. All five are acceptable in your project, as long as the label is correct.

1Strongest

Disclosed Facts

Statements made by the brand itself in materials a reader could verify directly.

ExamplesAnnual reports and 10-K filings (SEC EDGAR), 10-Q quarterly reports, investor presentations, press releases, official company statements, regulatory filings, retailer counts on the brand's site, store-locator data.

Label as"According to [Brand]'s 2024 10-K..." or "The company disclosed in its Q3 earnings call that..."

2Strong

Directly Observable Evidence

Things any reader could see for themselves with public tools, without needing the brand to confirm.

ExamplesMeta Ad Library (active and historical ads), TikTok Creative Center, Google Ads Transparency Center, the brand's social-media activity, retailer presence (in-store visits, retailer search), website promotions, email blasts (sign up for the list), packaging, sponsorships visible in public, physical distribution counts.

Label as"Observed in Meta Ad Library between January and March 2026..." or "A retailer audit of three major grocery chains in February 2026 found..."

3Moderate

Credible Outside Estimates

Numbers and analyses published by third parties with known methodology, used widely enough to be checkable.

ExamplesTrade publications (Modern Retail, Marketing Brew, AdAge, Business of Fashion), market-research firms (NielsenIQ, IRI, Statista, eMarketer, Gartner, Bain), agency case studies, media-monitoring services, well-sourced journalism, financial-analyst reports.

Label as"eMarketer estimated in May 2025 that..." or "Per a 2024 Bain & Altagamma study..."

4Weaker

Informed Inference

Reasoned conclusions drawn from Level 1, 2, or 3 evidence, clearly flagged as the reader's own interpretation.

Examples"Given that [brand]'s observable ad spend in Meta Ad Library skews heavily to brand-style creative..."; "Given the brand's wholesale-heavy channel mix, we can infer that customer-acquisition cost..."; "The pattern of seasonal pricing suggests..."

Label as"This appears to lean toward...", "Based on observable behavior, we estimate...", "The available evidence suggests..."

5Honest

Acknowledged Unknown

A specific claim the deck cannot defend at any of the four levels above. Naming the unknown is itself a contribution; pretending it is known is the failure.

ExamplesThe actual brand-versus-performance budget split at a private DTC company. The exact CAC at a brand with no disclosed financials. The actual incrementality of a competitor's trade promotion. Specific media-mix percentages for any private company.

Label as"The actual percentage split is not publicly disclosed. Any specific number would be speculation."

03
Worked Example

Walking a Liquid Death Claim Through All Five Levels

A student wants to write: "Liquid Death spends 75% of its budget on brand-building and 25% on performance." Specific, confident, and at least partly wrong. Walk the claim through the ladder.

Level 1 · Disclosed Facts: Mostly absent. Liquid Death is private and has not disclosed its media-mix percentages. The student cannot use this level to support the 75/25 claim. Some related facts ARE at Level 1: revenue figures from funding-round disclosures, store counts, the original Spotify launch story. None of those tell us the brand-versus-performance split.

Level 2 · Directly Observable Evidence: Substantial. The student can pull Liquid Death's ads from Meta Ad Library, observe its TikTok and YouTube activity, audit its retailer presence (Sacra reports 133,000-plus US doors as of 2024), document its celebrity activations (Tony Hawk, Martha Stewart, others), and catalogue its stunt marketing. This evidence shows what the brand does, not how much it spends.

Level 3 · Credible Outside Estimates: Available. Sacra has published revenue and valuation figures. AdAge, Marketing Brew, and other trade publications have covered Liquid Death's strategy in depth. These sources support claims like "Liquid Death is one of the most stunt-driven CPG brands of the last decade" because the trade press has documented it. They do not support a specific percentage split.

Level 4 · Informed Inference: Defensible. Combining Levels 2 and 3, the student can write: "Based on observable activity in paid social, the dominance of earned-media stunt marketing, and the prominence of celebrity partnerships, Liquid Death's communications appear to skew heavily toward brand-building." This is a defensible claim because the evidence and the reasoning are both visible to the reader.

Level 5 · Acknowledged Unknown: Required. The 75/25 number itself is not knowable from outside the company. The deck should say so: "The actual budget split between brand-building and performance media at Liquid Death is not publicly disclosed. Any specific percentage in this deck is a reasoned estimate, not a sourced figure."

The Rewrite

Before: "Liquid Death spends 75% on brand and 25% on performance."

After: "Liquid Death's communications appear to skew heavily toward brand-building based on observable activity in Meta Ad Library and the prominence of stunt and celebrity-led marketing covered in AdAge and Sacra. The actual budget split is not publicly disclosed. For the purposes of this deck, we estimate roughly 70-75% brand to 25-30% performance, with the caveat that this is an inference (Level 4) rather than a sourced figure (Level 1 or 3)."

The number is the same. The defense is now visible. The reader can tell exactly what is known and what is being estimated. This is what intellectual honesty in a deck looks like.

04
How to Use It

Applying the Ladder in Your Deck

Five claim types in this project almost always require the ladder. They are the places where students reach for confident numbers without the evidence to support them.

  • PESO mix. Specific percentages by quadrant are almost always Level 4 or 5. Observable activity in each quadrant is Level 2. Document what you see, then estimate.
  • Brand versus performance / 60/40 split. Same problem. Honest version: name the inputs you can see and the inference you are drawing.
  • Ad spend estimate. Public companies disclose this at Level 1. Private companies do not. If the brand is private, you are working at Level 4 plus Level 5 acknowledgment.
  • Distribution economics. Channel share is sometimes disclosed (Level 1), often estimated by trade press (Level 3), often inferred from observable retailer footprint (Level 4). Be clear about which.
  • Competitor strategy. What a competitor does is observable (Level 2). What a competitor intends is inference (Level 4) unless they have stated it publicly (Level 1).

A deck that confidently asserts unknowable numbers is weaker than a deck that estimates them honestly and says so.

In Your Project

The ladder is a labeling discipline. Apply it to every major numerical or strategic claim, not just the easy ones.

  • Label every consequential claim. "Level 1 disclosure" or "Level 4 inference" or "Level 5 unknown" can be a footnote, a slide-corner tag, or a short phrase in the body. The form does not matter; the discipline does.
  • Don't fake Level 1. Inventing a precise percentage and citing "industry sources" without naming them is the move the ladder is built to catch. If you cannot name the source, the claim is at Level 4 at best.
  • Use Level 5 deliberately. Naming what is genuinely unknown shows the grader you understand the limits of your evidence. It also points to where additional research would change the recommendation.
  • Be especially careful with competitor claims. Most "[competitor] spends X" claims in student decks are Level 4 dressed as Level 1. Audit them ruthlessly.

Research Prompts

Find the Facts maps to the five rungs by which sources sit at which level. Think It Through is a labeling exercise for your own deck.

Find the Facts (by Level)

  • Level 1: SEC EDGAR (sec.gov/edgar), Annual Reports, the brand's investor-relations page, press releases on the brand's own newsroom
  • Level 2: Meta Ad Library (facebook.com/ads/library), Google Ads Transparency Center, TikTok Creative Center, social media activity, the brand's email list (sign up), in-store audits, the brand's "where to buy" page
  • Level 3: Modern Retail, Marketing Brew, AdAge, Business of Fashion, NielsenIQ, IRI, Statista, eMarketer, Gartner, Bain, McKinsey, Sacra (for private companies)
  • Level 4: Your own reasoning, clearly flagged. AI assistants are useful here for stress-testing the inference, not for inventing the data the inference rests on
  • Level 5: The honest acknowledgment. No source needed

Think It Through

  • "For each major claim in our deck, name the level of evidence behind it. Which claims are at Level 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5?"
  • "Take the strongest claim in the deck. Could it be reframed at Level 4 with an explicit Level 5 acknowledgment, and would the deck be more honest as a result?"
  • "Identify any claim that is currently asserted at Level 1 (as fact) but would actually be Level 4 (inference) if labeled honestly. Rewrite it."
  • "For the two or three most consequential numbers in the deck, what would moving them from Level 4 inference to Level 1 disclosure require? Is that level of research feasible before the deadline?"

Vocabulary

Glossary

Disclosed fact
Information stated by the brand itself in a material a reader can verify directly: SEC filing, annual report, press release, earnings transcript. The strongest evidence available for any claim about a public company.
Observable evidence
Information any reader can confirm through public tools, without the brand's cooperation: ad libraries, social-media activity, retailer audits, packaging, email lists, sponsorship visibility.
Credible outside estimate
A figure or analysis published by a third party with known methodology and adequate transparency: trade press, market-research firms, agency case studies, financial analysts.
Informed inference
A reasoned conclusion drawn from Level 1, 2, or 3 evidence, with the reasoning visible to the reader. Acceptable if labeled; problematic if asserted as fact.
Acknowledged unknown
The deliberate naming of a claim the deck cannot defend at any of the four upper levels. The honest fifth-level move. Not a weakness in the deck; a sign that the writer understands the limits of the evidence.
Labeling discipline
The practice of tagging every consequential claim with its evidence level. The label is the contribution: it shows the reader what is known, what is inferred, and what is acknowledged as unknown.
