AI in Creative Work

Six questions about how AI is being used across the IMC stages, posed to ChatGPT in a fresh chat. Six unedited responses, pasted in as given — useful as a baseline for what the most popular tool will tell a marketer who asks.

The setup

Why a raw Q&A and not another essay

People in marketing are using AI throughout the IMC stages. We have a separate unit on AI later in the course, but for now, this is how AI is currently being used — sometimes by big brands with big spends, and sometimes by smaller brands with smaller budgets who feel overwhelmed at what it takes to be competitive in 2026.

This is a Q&A with ChatGPT. I asked the question; it gave me its response. I instructed it to answer each of the questions in 500 words or less, and I asked the questions in a fresh chat with no prior context so the answers would be the tool’s default voice and default thinking — the same voice and thinking a small-business owner, a junior strategist, or a non-marketing executive would get if they sat down and asked the same questions cold.

The responses below are unedited. No paraphrasing, no rewriting, no smoothing of the voice. The only formatting I’ve added is occasional bold and italics on phrases I think are worth slowing down for — useful or revealing, depending on the line. The questions themselves were mine.

Read this section as a baseline reading, not a verdict. The question worth asking at the end is not was ChatGPT right? but where is the answer useful, where is it safe, and where is it the kind of confident, well-organized response that §1 warned about — the polished version of an average idea?

Q1
The overall relationship

Describe overall the relationship between AI and marketing or advertising people. What are people doing to integrate or replace human work with AI?

AI is becoming part of almost every stage of marketing and advertising, but that does not mean it replaces the whole job. A better way to describe the relationship is that AI is taking over pieces of the work, especially the repetitive, first-draft, and variation-heavy parts. Marketers use it to summarize research, draft audience personas, create outlines, write social captions, generate ad copy options, build content calendars, adapt one message for different platforms, analyze reviews or comments, and produce quick versions of creative ideas. That can save time and help smaller teams do work that once required more people.

At the same time, AI is not equally strong at every task. It can produce a lot of polished language very quickly, but polished language is not the same thing as strategy. AI can make an average idea sound more complete than it really is. That creates a risk: people may accept the AI answer because it is fluent, not because it is strategically right.

So the strongest relationship is partnership, not full replacement. AI can help with speed, options, structure, and pressure-testing. Humans still need to define the problem, understand the brand, judge the audience insight, make ethical decisions, and choose what idea deserves to become the center of the campaign.

The replacement is most likely to happen with lower-level production tasks: basic captions, routine emails, simple product descriptions, first drafts, headline variations, and reporting summaries. The human value is stronger in areas that require judgment, taste, empathy, cultural awareness, brand understanding, and creative risk.

A useful way to summarize the relationship is this:

  • AI can generate more options faster than most people.
  • AI can improve weaker first drafts or help people who feel stuck.
  • AI still needs human judgment because it often gives safe, average, or overly familiar answers.

In marketing, AI is not really replacing creativity. It is replacing some of the labor around creativity. The danger is when people mistake that labor for the whole creative process.

Q2
Brainstorming and the Big Idea

Consider the usage of AI when brainstorming or coming up with a Big Idea to center a campaign on. Is AI best as a replacement or partner? Is AI better at thinking about the target, divergent ideas, reshaping ideas, or selecting ideas?

AI is best used as a partner in brainstorming, not as the replacement for the creative team. The best stage for AI is after the team has defined the problem but before the team has fallen in love with one solution. At that point, AI can widen the field. It can generate possible campaign territories, rewrite rough ideas, suggest different emotional angles, and help the team see what it may be missing.

AI is especially useful in the divergent stage of brainstorming. That is the stage where the goal is not to choose the answer yet, but to create more possibilities. AI can quickly produce dozens of ideas, headlines, tensions, metaphors, audience concerns, or platform executions. Some of those ideas will be weak, but even weak ideas can be useful because they give the team something to react against.

AI is also helpful in reshaping ideas. For example, a team can ask AI to make an idea more emotional, more direct, more unexpected, less corporate, more youth-oriented, more premium, or more consistent with a brand voice. It can also help identify missing ideas, such as an audience pain point the team ignored or a different way to frame the same benefit.

AI is less reliable when asked to deeply understand the target. It can imitate empathy, and it can summarize what a target audience may care about, but it does not actually live in that audience’s world. It needs human research, real observations, interviews, social listening, or cultural knowledge to keep it from relying on stereotypes.

AI is weakest as the final judge of the Big Idea. A Big Idea is not just a clever phrase. It is the strategic center of the campaign. It has to connect the target insight, brand truth, emotional tension, and desired response. AI can make an ordinary thought sound finished, which is dangerous. Humans still need to decide which idea has real weight, originality, brand fit, and enough flexibility to hold a campaign together.

In short, AI should widen the room, not replace the room. Let AI generate, stretch, challenge, and refine. Let people choose.

Q3
Which stage of the funnel

In your opinion, is AI more useful at the ToFU stage, MoFU stage, or BoFU stage?

In my opinion, AI is probably most useful in the middle and bottom of the funnel, and more risky at the top of the funnel.

At the top of the funnel, the goal is awareness, emotional connection, attention, and sometimes surprise. This is where brands need ideas that feel fresh, human, and culturally aware. AI can help at this stage by generating territories, emotional hooks, and campaign lines, but it should not be trusted too quickly. Top-of-funnel work often depends on originality and emotional judgment. AI tends to pull from patterns that already exist, so it may create ideas that sound like advertising but do not feel distinctive. It is helpful as a creative assistant here, but not as the final creative mind.

AI may be strongest in the middle of the funnel. This is where prospects are trying to understand the brand, compare options, answer questions, reduce doubt, and see whether the product fits their life. AI is good at organizing information. It can help create FAQs, blog posts, explainer videos, email sequences, buying guides, comparison charts, landing page copy, and educational content. It can also predict what questions a customer might have before taking action. That fits AI’s strengths because the task is less about producing one brilliant idea and more about clarity, usefulness, and relevance.

AI is also useful at the bottom of the funnel, especially for testing and variation. It can generate different calls to action, offer messages, subject lines, urgency appeals, retargeting copy, and landing page versions. This is where AI can work well with performance marketing because messages can be tested quickly. If one version performs better, marketers can adjust and keep improving.

The caution is that bottom-of-funnel AI can become too pushy. It may overuse urgency, scarcity, FOMO, or direct-response language. That can increase short-term clicks while weakening the brand if everything starts to sound desperate or transactional.

So my ranking would be: AI is strongest in MoFU, very useful in BoFU, and useful but most risky in ToFU. The higher the need for originality and emotional connection, the more human judgment matters.

Q4
Creating brand assets

How effective is AI at creating brand assets, color palettes, font pairings, and logos or icons? How does this work best with humans, and where might it replace humans?

AI can be effective at creating early-stage brand assets, but it is much stronger as a starting point than as a finished branding system. It can quickly generate mood boards, color palette directions, icon concepts, sample layouts, packaging mockups, and visual territories. That is useful because branding often begins with exploration. A team may not know yet whether the brand should feel bold, playful, premium, nostalgic, minimalist, technical, local, youthful, or serious. AI can make those directions visible quickly.

AI can also help non-designers see possibilities. A small business owner, student team, or early startup may use AI to create rough logo options, social templates, color schemes, and icon ideas before hiring a designer or building a more polished identity. In that sense, AI may replace some very basic entry-level design work, especially when the stakes are low and the brand does not need a highly distinctive visual system.

The limitation is that branding is not just decoration. A good brand identity has to be memorable, usable, legally safe, flexible, and tied to the brand’s positioning. A logo has to work small and large, in color and black-and-white, on a website, in a social profile, on packaging, on signage, and sometimes on merchandise. AI-generated logos can look impressive in a mockup but fail when they need to become real production files. AI can also produce designs that feel generic, derivative, or too close to existing brands.

This is why humans still matter. A designer or brand manager brings judgment: Does this look like us? Does it fit the target? Is it distinctive in the category? Can the colors meet accessibility standards? Are the typefaces appropriate and available for commercial use? Could the logo create trademark problems? Will this identity still work two years from now?

The best workflow is human-led and AI-assisted. Humans should define the brand strategy, audience, positioning, personality, and constraints. AI can then explore visual directions, generate rough options, and help compare possibilities. Humans should refine, simplify, test, and finalize the system. AI can replace some rough draft labor, but it should not replace the strategic and professional judgment behind a serious brand identity.

Q5
Monitoring brand voice and assets

How effective is AI at monitoring brand assets and keeping text in the same voice while also using the same brand assets?

AI may be more reliable at monitoring brand consistency than at inventing a brand from scratch. Once a company has a clear brand guide, AI can help check whether new copy sounds like the brand, whether the same terms are being used consistently, whether captions match the approved tone, and whether campaign messages drift away from the central idea. This is especially useful for organizations with many people creating content across social media, email, websites, sales decks, ads, and internal documents.

For text, AI can be very helpful as a brand voice editor. A company can give AI examples of approved writing, banned phrases, preferred language, reading level, tone, and formatting rules. Then AI can rewrite copy to make it sound more confident, more conversational, more premium, more playful, or less corporate. It can also flag language that feels off-brand, too promotional, too vague, or inconsistent with the brand’s usual point of view.

For visual assets, AI can help, but the process needs structure. AI can compare layouts, images, logos, colors, and templates against brand guidelines, but it works best when the rules are specific. For example: use this logo version only on light backgrounds; do not stretch the logo; use these colors; use these fonts; keep this amount of clear space; avoid unauthorized icons or stock imagery. AI can assist with quality control, but it should not be the only approval system for important public-facing work.

The biggest benefit is scale. A human brand manager may not have time to review every social post, flyer, email, slide, or landing page. AI can serve as a first-pass checker that catches obvious problems before they go live. That can reduce inconsistency and help maintain a more professional identity.

The risk is that consistency can turn into sameness. If AI keeps forcing every message into the same pattern, the brand may become stiff, repetitive, or boring. A good brand voice has consistency, but it also needs flexibility. A customer service apology, a TikTok caption, a product page, and a CEO statement should not all sound exactly the same. So AI is useful as a guardrail, not as the final brand police. It can flag problems and suggest improvements. Humans should decide when the brand needs to stretch, adapt, or break its own pattern for a good reason.

Q6
AI and social media content

Additional thoughts: AI and social media content

AI is very useful for social media content, but only after the brand has a clear strategy. It can draft captions, suggest hooks, create content calendars, turn one campaign idea into multiple posts, rewrite copy for different platforms, and adjust tone for Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn, email, or paid social. It is especially helpful for the unglamorous middle work: alternate openings, post sequences, comment prompts, short-form video ideas, and make-this-less-corporate rewrites.

The risk is that social media can become a volume game. Some people treat content as if the only goal is to post more often, sound casual, and chase engagement. AI makes that easier, but it can also create a lot of content-shaped noise. A brand may look active without saying anything meaningful. It may sound current without having a real point of view.

That is why AI should help execute the voice, not invent the soul of the brand. The brand still needs a target, a position, a reason to exist, and a creative idea worth repeating. Once those are clear, AI can be extremely helpful. Before that, it may simply produce more posts without making the marketing better.

Glossary

Terms used in this section

IMC (Integrated Marketing Communications)
The discipline of coordinating advertising, PR, sales promotion, digital, and direct channels so the brand sends one coherent message across every touchpoint. The frame the whole course is built on.
ToFU / MoFU / BoFU
Top, middle, and bottom of the funnel. Carried over from §7, §8, and §9. ToFU is awareness, MoFU is consideration and helpful content, BoFU is conversion. The three stages where the creative job changes shape entirely.
Big Idea
Carried over from §3. The strategic kernel that can hold a campaign together across years and channels — You’re Not You When You’re Hungry, Just Do It, Real Beauty. ChatGPT’s Q2 answer is essentially a long-form version of why AI can help build out a Big Idea but should not be trusted to pick one.
Distinctive brand assets
Carried over from §7. The non-name elements — colors, characters, voices, layouts — that trigger brand recognition on their own. The Q5 answer is essentially the brand-monitoring case for AI: once the assets exist, AI is a useful enforcement layer.
Divergent stage
The phase of brainstorming where the goal is to widen the possibility space, not narrow it. Carried over from §2. Q2 names this as AI’s clearest strength — volume of options, not selection among them.
Content-shaped noise
ChatGPT’s own phrase, in Q6. The thing a brand produces when it has access to easy generation but no point of view — posts that look like content but do not say anything. Worth keeping in your vocabulary.
