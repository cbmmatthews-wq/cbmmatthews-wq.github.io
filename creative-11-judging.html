Judging Creative When You’re Not the Creative

The creative team has left the room. You have notes due Friday. You did not write the script. Here is how not to ruin the work — and how not to bless work that should be ruined.

01
The empty room

The two failure modes of the non-creative in the room

The creative team has just walked out of the room. Three of them came in, presented for forty-five minutes, took your questions, and packed up their laptops. You are the brand director, or the marketing manager, or the small-business founder, or the VP whose only formal training in advertising was the Belch chapter you skimmed last night. You have notes due Friday. The work in front of you cost twenty thousand dollars to develop and will cost ten times that to produce. You did not write the script. You are about to tell the people who did what to change.

Most non-creatives default into one of two failure modes, and the failure modes are not symmetric. The first is the abdication. You squint at the storyboard, you say something polite about the third frame, and you sign off because you do not feel qualified to argue with people who have done this for fifteen years. The work goes through unchanged, which sounds like a win for the creative team until you realize that work no one is allowed to argue with is also work no one upstairs feels invested in — and that work tends to die in the second round of approvals you are not in.

The second failure mode is the one designers have complained about since the early eighties. The non-creative judge looks at the work, cannot find a vocabulary for what is or is not working, and reaches for the only notes they know how to give: can we make the logo bigger? Can we move it to the top-left corner so people see it first? Can we put the price in the corner of the hero shot? Can it be more — you know — pop? This is the kind of feedback that has become a running joke in the design community because it is the feedback of someone who has not been taught the difference between making a piece of work louder and making it more effective.

Here is the wrinkle that the joke leaves out. Sometimes the logo should be bigger. Sometimes it should be in the top-left corner. Sometimes it should be on screen in the first second instead of the last. We went through this in §6: a thirty-second branding spot can withhold the brand for twenty-eight seconds and pay it off in the last two, but a skippable YouTube pre-roll or a TikTok in-feed ad has to land the brand in the first three. The same instruction — logo earlier, logo bigger — is brilliant in one context and ruinous in the other. The job of the judge is not to refuse the instruction. The job of the judge is to know which kind of ad they are looking at. Most available “creative testing” tools answer a narrower question than the one you actually have; the rest of this section gives you the working vocabulary they leave out.

02
Two questions, never conflated

The single hardest discipline of judging creative work

Almost every wrong note given on a piece of creative work in the past twenty years comes from collapsing two judgments into one. The two judgments are:

  • Is it on strategy? — Is this the right idea for what we are trying to do? Does the work serve the brief? Does it carry the Big Idea (§3)? Does it speak to the audience we are trying to reach, at the funnel stage we are trying to reach them?
  • Is it executed well? — Is this idea well-made? Is the copy tight? Is the casting right? Does the music carry the scene or fight it? Are the colors, the type, the timing, the cut working together or against each other?

The non-creative in the room almost always collapses these into a third, much worse question: do I like it? The third question has no diagnostic value. A perfectly strategic, well-executed ad can be one you personally do not enjoy — you are not the target audience, the appeal is not aimed at you, the humor is one register off from yours — and that ad will still work brilliantly on the people you are paying to reach. Conversely, an ad you personally adore can be solving the wrong problem at the wrong stage of the funnel, and it will lose money. Your enjoyment of the work and the work’s effectiveness are correlated only loosely, and over-reading your own reaction is one of the most expensive mistakes in this discipline.

The Reinartz and Saffert framework we built around in §1 — originality, flexibility, elaboration, synthesis, artistic value — is a rubric for the execution question. It tells you whether the craft is doing serious work. It does not tell you whether the craft is in service of the right idea. The framework we are about to introduce, the WARC Creative Effectiveness Ladder, is a rubric for the strategy question. Together they give you a working vocabulary for both halves of the judgment. You need both, and you need to keep them separate.

A quick translation

The most common bad note — I don’t like it — is not a note. It is a feeling looking for a justification. Before you give the note, ask yourself which of the two questions it is actually about. If it is the strategy question, say so: I am not sure this is solving the right problem for the audience we want. If it is the execution question, say so: The pacing feels off; the joke doesn’t land for me until the third beat. Either of those is a note the team can work with. I don’t like it is the note that gets the meeting rebooked.

03
Match the judgment to the job

The WARC Creative Effectiveness Ladder, and why “great” is not a single thing

A “great” thirty-second Super Bowl spot and a “great” Black Friday Instagram carousel are not great by the same standard. They are not trying to do the same job. Judging one by the other’s metrics is the most common single error in creative review — and it is the error the WARC and Cannes Lions Effectiveness Code was written to fix.

In 2020, James Hurman and Peter Field published a white paper for Cannes Lions and WARC that analyzed nearly five thousand award-winning case studies and sorted the effects creative work can produce into six tiers, from least to most commercially impactful. They called it the Creative Effectiveness Ladder. The Ladder is now the judging framework for the Cannes Effectiveness Lions and the working vocabulary for the industry’s effectiveness conversation. The six rungs are below, in WARC’s own descriptions.

01 · Influential Idea
Over-achieve on campaign metrics
Creativity that maximizes engagement and sharing — the campaign over-indexes on recall, sharing, earned media, and media efficiency. Bang for the buck at the attention layer.
02 · Behaviour Breakthrough
Change consumer behaviour
Creativity that shifts customer purchase behaviour, or other brand-relevant behaviour — trial, penetration, switching, usage frequency, category-entry. The first commercial effect on the Ladder.
03 · Sales Spike
Short-term sales growth
Creativity that creates short-term, temporary growth in sales, market share, or profitability. Sales go up during the flight and come back down after. Real value, finite duration.
04 · Brand Builder
Improve brand health
Creativity that improves the fundamental measures of brand health — awareness, consideration, preference, purchase intent, image-attribute ownership. The leading indicator of long-term commercial outcomes.
05 · Commercial Triumph
Sustained sales success
Creativity that profitably increases sales and market share beyond the duration of the campaign — the lift holds for multiple quarters after the flight ends. Brand-building effects converted into commercial outcomes.
06 · Enduring Icon
Long-term brand and sales growth
Creativity that drives brand and sales growth consistently over three years or more, sticking with the same creative strategy or work throughout. Snickers’ “You’re Not You,” Dove’s “Real Beauty,” John Lewis at Christmas. The high watermarks.

The single most useful question the Ladder gives you is this: which rung is this campaign supposed to hit? Then judge the work against that rung, and not against the rung two above or two below it. WARC says it plainly in the paper: aim for the Ladder level that matches the objective you have set for the campaign you are embarking on.

This framework protects you from the most expensive non-creative judgment errors. A Sales Spike campaign for a flash sale is not failing because it is not an Enduring Icon. A Brand Builder TV spot is not failing because it did not produce a measurable lift in next-day store traffic — next-day store traffic is not what it was trying to produce. The work fails when it is poorly executed against the rung it was aimed at, or when it was aimed at a rung the business did not actually need. One more thing the Ladder forces into the open: most campaigns can show effects at two or three rungs at once, and the classification is by the highest rung the work can credibly prove, not the only one. A useful creative review asks the team to be honest about which rung is the primary target and which rungs are bonus — rather than letting the conversation drift into we hope it does everything.

04
Pre-flight testing

What the predictive-testing tools actually measure (and what they don’t)

A reasonable response to the previous section is: that’s nice in theory, but I have a creative review on Friday and I need a way to say something about the work in front of me. The industry has built a meaningful business around exactly that need, and the most visible category in 2026 is predictive creative testing — software that uses neuroscience-trained models to forecast how an ad will perform before any media is spent.

Neurons is the cleanest example. You upload an ad — static, video, multi-frame, social, OOH — and within minutes the platform returns an attention heatmap (where the eye goes), a cognitive-load reading (how hard the brain is working to parse it), and predictive scores for branding, recall, and engagement. The science behind it is real: years of eye-tracking and EEG data trained into a model that approximates how a representative human visual system will process the ad in the first few seconds.

Neurons’ own case for why this matters

The company lists five reasons creative testing earns its place in the workflow. The list is theirs; the framing applies to every tool in this category, not just to Neurons:

  1. Campaign efficiency. Testing identifies the strongest version of an ad before launch — reducing the costly trial-and-error of finding out what works in a live media environment.
  2. Audience attention. Eye-tracking research suggests only about one in three ads is actually seen by its intended audience. Pre-flight testing surfaces the ads that cut through in the first few seconds.
  3. Conversion lift. Refining specific elements — CTA placement, product imagery, headline position — can lift conversions by 20–50% depending on the channel.
  4. Faster decisions. Creative reviews get stuck in opinion debate. Testing introduces enough objectivity to break the loop and ship the work.
  5. Less creative fatigue. Overexposed ads decay quickly. A predictable testing rhythm keeps the rotation fresh and the iteration cycle honest.
Open the article → Neurons · Ad Creative Testing Worth a quick browse for the visuals alone. You’ll see a stack of heatmaps showing what people look at first, what they look at next, and where the eye dwells — what fixates them in the frame.
You can try this yourself, cheap

You do not need an enterprise contract to see this work. The built-in front-facing camera on a laptop or tablet, combined with one of the consumer-grade browser tools that have appeared in the last two years, can run a workable eye-tracking pass on your own ad in your own conference room. The data is not lab-quality, but it is good enough to settle a debate about whether the headline is being seen before the CTA, and it costs less than the lunch the creative review meeting will run on.

The limitation is structural and worth saying out loud. Every quantitative pre-flight tool has to operationalize “creative” as something measurable, and what is measurable in the first three seconds of an ad is attention — not memory, not meaning, not whether the joke lands, not whether the idea is fresh. An ad that scores brilliantly on the attention heatmap can still be a tired, derivative idea any sophisticated viewer has seen ten times. An ad that scores moderately can be the start of an Enduring Icon platform — the kind of work where the payoff comes from years of repetition, not from the first three seconds of the first execution.

Neurons knows this; their own best-practices page lists “use data to guide, not dictate” as principle four. The trap is not the tool. The trap is the non-creative judge who treats the attention score as the verdict. Predictive testing is good evidence about whether the ad is noticeable. It is weak evidence about whether the ad is creative in the Ladder-climbing sense. The first question gets answered in milliseconds. The second question gets answered in years.

05
The performance bench

Motion’s Creative Benchmarks 2026, and the math that should reset your expectations

The other major source of judgment data in 2026 sits on the opposite side of the funnel: post-flight performance benchmarks for paid social. The category leader is Motion, whose Creative Benchmarks 2026 report analyzed roughly 550,000 ads from over 6,000 advertisers, representing about $1.3 billion in spend on Facebook and Instagram between September 2025 and early January 2026. The report’s tagline is the headline finding: most ads are forgettable, and that’s okay.

The single most useful number for a non-creative judge: only about 5% of ads in a typical account become real winners — Motion defines a winner as a single creative that spends at least ten times the account’s median ad. Roughly half of all ads in any niche or spend size receive no significant spend at all. The bulk of any account’s spend flows through a small minority of creatives, and the dispersion looks the same whether the account is a scrappy DTC brand burning fifty dollars a day or a public company burning fifty thousand.

The implication for judgment is liberating. A low hit rate is not necessarily a sign of weak creative. It is the statistical shape of how performance advertising actually works. The algorithm is finding the small share of creatives that resonate with the audience it can reach at a profitable cost; the rest are testing inventory. Motion makes the point bluntly: a high hit rate may actually signal that the team is not testing enough to find its outliers. The strongest creative strategists are not the ones who predict winners. They are the ones who build systems for surfacing winners and noticing them early.

Open the report → Motion · Creative Benchmarks 2026 Go look at the visuals. Three patterns in the report data are worth seeing for yourself:
Highlights
  • Most ads spend very little; a few ads spend a lot. The distribution is heavily skewed, and the shape repeats at every account size in the dataset.
  • Winners become more common as volume rises — indicating that more testing increases the odds of producing breakout ads. The accounts producing more variation surface more outliers; the ones producing less hit fewer winners.
  • Winning ads make up a small share of any portfolio, even for higher-spend advertisers. The curve does not flatten with budget; the math is the math.

A few other findings worth carrying out of the report

Hooks that signal immediacy, clarity, or a concrete reason to act tend to win more often. Simple creative — text-only ads, product images with text overlays, plain GIFs — shows up over and over in the winners list, partly because it is cheap to produce and therefore easy to iterate on.

Here is the necessary caveat. Motion is measuring spend allocation by the Meta algorithm, which is itself optimizing for short-term conversion. The benchmarks are honest, useful, and category-defining for Sales Spike (rung 3) and short-window Behaviour Breakthrough (rung 2) work. They are not measuring whether any of these ads is building the brand, contributing to mental availability, or creating an asset that will compound across years. A non-creative judge who reads Motion’s numbers and applies them to a Brand Builder TV spot will systematically misjudge that work, because the numbers were built on a different rung of the Ladder. Read the right tool for the right question, and you have a useful frame. Read it for the wrong question, and you defund the most effective work in your portfolio.

06
The CEO’s blind spot

Why the numbers that look real are not the numbers that matter most

None of the above lives in a vacuum. In most companies, the non-creative judge sits between a creative team that wants to do Brand Builder work and an executive team that wants last-touch attribution numbers by quarter-end. The pull is asymmetric. The performance numbers look real, and the brand numbers look like vibes. A Sales Spike report has dollar signs in it. A Brand Builder report has shifts in awareness and consideration that the CFO has been trained to discount.

This is the part where the non-creative judge has to do unpopular work. The Sales Spike numbers are real — they are also incomplete. Last-touch attribution gives all the credit to the ad that closed the sale and none to the storyteller from §7 who introduced the brand at the wedding three months earlier. The branded-search lift, the mental availability, the distinctive brand asset that does work years from now — none of these show up in the dashboard the CEO is reading on Monday morning. If the judge does not push back, the company will systematically defund the work that produces the highest long-term returns, because that work shows up in a different report on a different timeline that nobody on the executive floor is reading.

The job is not to win the attribution argument — that argument is decades old and unwinnable in most rooms. The job is to push back enough that the creative portfolio does not collapse entirely into the rungs producing the cleanest short-term numbers. The Binet and Field 60/40 split (carried forward from §1) is the working heuristic: roughly sixty percent of paid-media budget into brand-building work, roughly forty percent into activation. The judge who has read this section knows what each half is for, and refuses to judge the brand-building half by the activation half’s scoreboard.

07
Friday morning

A working checklist for the non-creative in the review

The meeting is at ten. You have the storyboard, the script, the mood film, and the rate card on your desk. Here is the working order of operations.

One. Name the rung before you give the note.

Before you say anything substantive about the work, force yourself to name — out loud, on a sticky note, in your own head — which rung of the Ladder this campaign is supposed to hit. Sales Spike for a Black Friday push? Brand Builder for a category entry? Enduring Icon platform you intend to run for five years? Your notes will be different in each case, and conflating the brief is the single most expensive mistake in this room.

Two. Separate the two judgments.

For each note you want to give, ask which question it is about. Is it on strategy? — route those notes to the Big Idea, the audience, the appeal, the funnel stage. Is it executed well? — route those notes to the craft: pacing, casting, copy, music, type, color. A note that says “the pacing of frame three is undercutting the strategic intent of the opening hook” is a note the team can fix. A note that says “I’m not feeling it” is a note that delays the project two weeks.

Three. Translate “I don’t like it” before you say it.

The most common bad note is the one that begins with I don’t like or I’m not sure about. Before you say it, translate it: here is the specific thing that is not working, and here is the strategic reason it concerns me. If you cannot do the translation, the note is a feeling, not a judgment. Sit with the feeling. It may dissolve. It may also turn into a real note if you give it ten more minutes.

Four. Ask what they gave up — and what their second-best idea was.

Every creative choice closes other doors. The cast they chose meant a different cast they did not choose; the joke that is in meant a joke that is out. Ask what they considered and rejected, and ask what their second-best concept was and why this one beat it. The answer to the second question is almost always more revealing than the presentation of the chosen idea, because it forces the team to articulate the strategic logic that picked one over the other. If the answer is thin, the work is thin. If the answer is sharp, the work is probably better than your first reaction suggested.

Five. Apply the R&S five dimensions to the execution — lightly.

The Reinartz and Saffert dimensions from §1 — originality, flexibility, elaboration, synthesis, artistic value — are an execution rubric, not a checklist. Use them to find vocabulary for what is or is not working on the craft side. The originality is strong but the elaboration is thin — the idea is fresh but it doesn’t reward a second viewing. That is a real note. Better than can we make it pop?

Six. Be willing to defend the work upstream.

The notes you give in the room are only half the job; the other half is what you say in the next meeting, the one where the work has to survive your boss, the CFO, and the salesperson who wants more product shots. If you cannot articulate why you approved the work in language the executive floor will understand, the work will not survive contact with them. The Ladder vocabulary, the two-judgment frame, the 60/40 split — these are not for the creative review. They are for the meeting after, when the work needs an ally who is not a creative.

Quick-hit takeaway
  • The non-creative judge is either the ally of the work or its biggest obstacle. There is no neutral position.
  • Two judgments, never one: is it on strategy and is it executed well. Collapsing them into do I like it is the most expensive mistake in this discipline.
  • Name the Ladder rung before you give the note. Do not judge a Brand Builder by a Sales Spike’s scoreboard.
  • Pre-flight tools like Neurons answer is it noticeable well; they do not answer is it creative in the Ladder-climbing sense.
  • Performance benchmarks like Motion’s are honest and useful for the rungs they cover (Sales Spike, short-window behaviour). They are not measuring brand-building work, and applying them as if they were defunds the most effective work in the portfolio.
  • Translate I don’t like it before saying it. If the translation is hard, the note is a feeling and not a note.

Vocabulary

Glossary

Creative Effectiveness Ladder — the six-rung hierarchy of marketing effects developed by James Hurman and Peter Field for Cannes Lions and WARC in The Effectiveness Code (2020), based on nearly 5,000 case studies. The rungs, from least to most commercially impactful: Influential Idea, Behaviour Breakthrough, Sales Spike, Brand Builder, Commercial Triumph, Enduring Icon. Now the official judging framework for the Cannes Effectiveness Lions.

The two judgments — the working frame of this section. Strategy judgment (is it on strategy?) and execution judgment (is it executed well?) are separate questions and have to be evaluated separately. Collapsing them into a single “do I like it” question is the most common error of non-creative judges.

Five dimensions of creativity (Reinartz & Saffert) — carried forward from §1. The HBR rubric for evaluating execution: originality, flexibility, elaboration, synthesis, artistic value. Useful as a vocabulary for naming what is or is not working at the craft layer; elaboration outperforms originality in the underlying data.

Predictive creative testing — the pre-flight testing category exemplified by Neurons, Memorable AI, and similar platforms. Uses neuroscience-trained models (eye-tracking, EEG, attention) to forecast how an ad will perform before any media is spent. Strong on attention-layer questions; weaker on the higher rungs of the Ladder.

Hit rate — in Motion’s 2026 framing, the share of an account’s ads that spend at least 10× the account’s median single-ad spend. Roughly 5% across the dataset. A counter-intuitive note from the report: a high hit rate may indicate under-testing, not strong creative judgment.

Last-touch attribution — the attribution model that gives all conversion credit to the final ad a buyer interacted with before purchase. Convenient, popular with CFOs, and structurally biased against upper-funnel work, since the storyteller who introduced the brand months earlier does not appear in the report.

60/40 brand-to-activation split — the working budget heuristic from Les Binet and Peter Field’s long-running IPA research. Roughly 60% of paid-media budget into brand-building work, 40% into activation. Adjusts by category dynamics. Carried forward from §1.
