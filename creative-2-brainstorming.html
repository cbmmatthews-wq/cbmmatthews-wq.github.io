M544 · Creative · #2

Brainstorming Basics: The Controlled Daydream

There is a way brainstorming usually goes in companies that do not do it often. Someone says “let’s brainstorm,” everyone sits down at a table, ideas get thrown out for fifteen or twenty minutes, somebody says “that one — let’s go with that one,” the meeting ends, and the chosen idea is, almost without fail, a slightly reworded version of last year’s campaign. Two distinct failure modes are running at the same time in that meeting, and neither of them is solved by trying harder, sitting longer, or buying nicer Post-it notes. They are solved by understanding that brainstorming has structure — the unglamorous structure of a process with stages — and that the structure exists precisely because the alternative is the meeting that just happened.

Overview

The Argument

Brainstorming is the most under-rated and most poorly-executed activity in the creative process. Under-rated because it gets dismissed as fluff — the squishy part of the work that the “real” analysts skip. Poorly executed because the people who do it almost always treat it as a single activity that happens in a single sitting, when it is in fact a two-step process whose steps are mutually destructive if you try to do them at the same time. This piece is the case for treating brainstorming as a craft with stages, prompts, and disciplines — not as “sit at a whiteboard and shout things until something sticks.”

The position to argue against is what we’ll call the casual-brainstorm school: the view that brainstorming is what creative people happen to do when they are being creative, and that bringing structure to it kills the magic. This view is appealing because it sounds humble — don’t over-engineer it, just let the ideas come — and because it is the operating philosophy of almost everyone who has never produced an idea on demand. It is also, when examined, the philosophy under which most disappointing campaigns are conceived.

The position in one sentence

Brainstorming reliably produces useful ideas only when it is structured into two distinct phases — a generative phase where judgment is suspended and a separate evaluative phase where judgment is the entire point — and when the room walks into the generative phase already loaded with prep about the actual person the idea is being made for.

01
Setup

Two Traps That Pretend to Be Brainstorming

If you stay in business long enough, you will sit through hundreds of meetings called “brainstorms” and notice that almost all of them produce nothing memorable. The reason is usually one of two traps, and very often both at once. Neither is the participants’ fault. Both are structural — they are baked into how the meeting was set up before anyone walked in.

1
The No-Prep Brainstorm

“I just want to brainstorm with a clear head — I don’t want my brain filled with nonsense before we start.” This sounds disciplined. It is, in practice, the most reliable way to produce ideas that resonate only with the people in the room.

Without the prep, you brainstorm as yourself. Your own preferences, irritations, and convenient blind spots become the slate. The output is your private pro/con list about the brand, dressed up as creative ideas, aimed by accident at people who look and shop and think like you.

2
Generate-and-Judge in One Pass

The second trap is failing to separate the generative phase from the evaluative phase. They are different mental operations, and they cannot be done well at the same time. The room that tries to do both ends up doing neither.

Every interesting idea gets shot down before it has been built out. Every safe idea gets adopted by default. The safe idea is almost always a slight rewording of what already exists — which, depending on whether what already exists is working, is either a fine outcome or a quietly disastrous one.

Trap one, in detail: who is the brainstorm for?

Say the brief is for a mid-tier home-improvement retailer and the target is a couple in their thirties or forties, two kids, suburban, three bedrooms with the third one full of bins. You and four colleagues sit down without reading the brief carefully, without skimming the persona work the strategist did, without looking at last quarter’s qualitative research — because that would “bias” you. So you brainstorm. What comes out is what was already in your heads: ideas about the brand seen through your own consumer lens, your own gripes, your own aesthetics. None of you is that couple. None of you shops the way that couple shops, frets the way that couple frets, or laughs at the things that couple laughs at. The output is technically “ideas”; it just isn’t ideas for the person whose money you are trying to earn.

Worse, those ideas are now on the whiteboard. They are nearly impossible to evict. The next round of thinking gets anchored to them whether they are any good or not, because the room has already invested an hour in them. The prep wasn’t skipped because it didn’t matter; it was skipped because it felt slow. The cost shows up an hour later as ideas that cannot be killed.

The discipline this piece is going to keep pointing at is simple: before you start generating, spend real time on the target. Read the persona. Read the qualitative data. Look at the photos the research team has of where this person lives and how they shop. Then walk into the brainstorm having mentally swapped out your eyes for theirs. Piece #3 will return to this in the language of the Big Idea: the most resonant ideas come from the intersection of a brand truth and a human truth, and the human truth has to be the customer’s, not the room’s.

Trap two, in detail: the gravitational pull of the safe option

The mechanism behind the second trap is well documented in social psychology and well visible in any meeting room: when judgment is allowed in the generative phase, the loudest critic effectively becomes the room’s editor. Quieter people stop offering ideas. Riskier ideas get nipped at the bud. What survives the filter is what nobody objects to — which is almost always what the room has seen before. The drift toward the safest available option is not a bug in the process; it is the inevitable behavior of a room that has not separated the two phases.

And the other failure mode of an undisciplined room runs in the opposite direction: a person or two starts gravitating toward the deliberately weird, the “disruptive,” the kind of idea you cannot defend in any client meeting but which feels brave. Without the discipline of a later evaluative phase, that idea also has nothing pushing back on it. Both poles — safest possible and weirdest possible — are what an undisciplined brainstorm tends to converge on, depending on whose voice carries the day. Neither is the idea that actually solves the brief.

02
The Structure

Diverge Before You Converge

The fix for the second trap is the oldest and best-documented technique in the literature, and like a lot of foundational ideas about how to do marketing, it comes from inside the advertising industry. In 1939, with BBDO bleeding profits and one of his partners gone, Alex Osborn started developing a method for getting his copywriters to produce more ideas faster. He called it “thinking up” in his 1942 book of the same name, and codified the rule that made it work in Applied Imagination in 1953, by which point he had renamed the method brainstorming. The single most important rule from that book is the one almost every modern brainstorm violates: defer judgment during idea generation. Go for quantity. Welcome the weird. Build on others’ ideas. Withhold criticism until later. Critique is a separate meeting.

This is what the literature now calls divergent thinking: opening the field as wide as possible before narrowing it. The complementary phase is convergent thinking: evaluating, combining, killing, selecting. Both are necessary. They cannot occur simultaneously in the same head without one of them being suppressed, and the one that gets suppressed is always divergent thinking, because evaluation is the easier and more socially rewarded posture. This is why the typical undisciplined brainstorm is, on inspection, a convergence session wearing a divergence costume.

The visual the design world has standardized for the two-step structure is the diamond — ideation opens up wide, then narrows to a choice:

Phase 2 · Decide

→
Convergenarrow, judge, pick
Quantity. Welcome the weird. Defer judgment.
Quality. Critique. Combine. Kill the obvious.
The double diamond at its simplest. The British Design Council’s formal version stacks two of these — one for understanding the problem, one for solving it — but the binary diverge-then-converge move underneath both diamonds is the same one Osborn was writing about in 1953.

The double diamond also makes visible something experienced facilitators learn the hard way: if the room is small or contains a few dominant personalities, run the divergent phase silently first. Each person writes ideas on their own for ten or fifteen minutes; then ideas come to the wall. The technique has several names — brainwriting, the nominal group technique, “quiet brainstorming” — and it consistently produces more and better ideas than group-only brainstorming in the research literature, because it removes the loudest-critic effect from the generative phase. It is, again, the structure doing the work, not anyone’s talent.

Where this fits: Design Thinking

A wider process that the diamond lives inside

If you take the diverge-then-converge move and put it inside a longer arc that starts with the customer and ends with a tested prototype, you get what the design world calls Design Thinking. Codified at Stanford’s d.school and at IDEO in the 2000s and broadly adopted across product, service, and increasingly marketing design, it lays out five stages:

  • Empathize. Understand the user. Field research, interviews, observation — the human-truth half of any campaign.
  • Define. Reframe what you learned into a sharp problem statement. “Suburban parents avoid this category because…”
  • Ideate. The diverge-then-converge phase. This is where the brainstorming techniques in this piece live.
  • Prototype. Build a small, ugly version of the winning idea. A mock-up, a script, a paper interface, a 30-second pre-roll cut.
  • Test. Put the prototype in front of the actual target audience and watch what happens.

Design Thinking is not a linear process — you loop. Empathy reveals a problem; the prototype reveals you misunderstood the empathy; testing sends you back to ideate. The British Design Council’s double diamond formalizes this loop into two diamonds: one for “the right problem” (empathize + define) and one for “the right solution” (ideate + prototype + test).

The official version says you proceed left to right through the steps in a straight line; the curved arrows above the steps say what actually happens. Real creative work loops — a prototype reveals you misunderstood the empathy, a test sends you back to ideate, and sometimes the right move is back a step, or all the way to the beginning.
Figure: Sarah Gibbons, “Design Thinking 101,” Nielsen Norman Group. The NN Group version extends the canonical five-stage d.school model with a sixth “implement” stage for organizational adoption.

For our purposes in M544: brainstorming is the engine, but Design Thinking is the bigger structure inside which the engine is mounted. The two failure modes from the previous section are essentially Design-Thinking failures: trap one is skipping Empathize, and trap two is collapsing Ideate and Test into the same five-minute meeting.

03
The Stages

The Creative Process Is a Controlled Daydream

The structure Osborn was working with did not appear out of nowhere. He was reading the British social psychologist Graham Wallas, whose 1926 book The Art of Thought laid out a four-stage model of how creative ideas actually arrive in the mind: preparation (gathering the raw material), incubation (letting the mind work on the problem unconsciously), illumination (the moment the answer surfaces), and verification (testing whether the answer holds). James Webb Young, longtime creative VP at J. Walter Thompson, popularized the same idea in his short 1939 book A Technique for Producing Ideas, splitting Wallas’s preparation phase into two and giving the advertising industry the five-stage model it has used ever since: immersion, digestion, incubation, illumination, verification.

Young’s five stages of an idea

Immersion. Gather the raw material — the brief, the brand, the target, the category. Read everything. Talk to people. Use the product.
Digestion. Work the material over in your conscious mind. Turn it. Re-frame it. Make connections.
Incubation. Put it down. Go for a walk, take a shower, sleep. Let the unconscious do its share.
Illumination. The idea arrives, often when you are not looking for it. The “Eureka” moment.
Verification. Test the idea against the brief, against the target, against reality. Refine or kill.

The advertising copywriter Luke Sullivan, in his sixth edition of Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This, gives the same process a more useful name: a controlled daydream. The two-word phrase carries the whole argument. The controlled half is the work of stages one and two — you don’t just space out, you load your head with the right materials first so that when you do space out, your unconscious has something useful to work with. The daydream half is the part most professionals resist hardest: stage three. Incubation. Walking away. Letting the problem sit. It looks, from the outside, like not working — no document, no checked-off task, no Slack message — but the literature on creative cognition is consistent that insight problems are disproportionately solved during incubation, which is the “shower thought” effect that became an internet meme.

The practical version, again, is staged work with deliberate breaks. Sullivan’s slides put it plainly: don’t sit back and wait for the idea to come to you. Incubate after reading the brief about the objectives, the target, and what they think about you and the importance of the brand category. Then, GO. The work is rhythmic — load, push, walk away, return, push, walk away, return.

The production of ideas is just as definite a process as the production of Fords; the production of ideas, too, runs an assembly line; in this production the mind follows an operative technique which can be learned and controlled.
— James Webb Young, A Technique for Producing Ideas, 1939

The Young quote is older than every other source on this page and still says the thing that needs saying. Creative work is not a magical visitation. It is a craft with a technique. The technique can be taught and it can be practiced, which means it can also be done badly — and most of the bad work this section is arguing against is the work of people who think creativity is a personality trait rather than a process they could be running properly.

04
The Roles

Wear One Hat at a Time

The creativity consultant Roger von Oech — the same advertising-adjacent author who wrote A Whack on the Side of the Head and whose Creative Whack Pack cards still circulate in some agencies — offers the most useful working model for what the structured-brainstorming literature is actually asking of you. He says creative work requires four distinct mental roles, and that the people who reliably produce good ideas can switch between them on demand. The four are Explorer, Artist, Judge, and Warrior. Each owns a stage, and trying to wear two hats at once is functionally identical to the trap we have been calling generate-and-judge-in-one-pass.

Explorer
Gather · Search · Notice

The hat for immersion and digestion. The Explorer goes looking for raw material wherever it might be hiding — in qualitative research, in adjacent categories, in unusual patterns, in conversations with people who do not work in marketing. The Explorer’s primary discipline is curiosity without an immediate target.

Artist
Play · Combine · Generate

The hat for divergent thinking. The Artist plays with material the Explorer collected and recombines it in ways that may or may not be useful. The Artist’s primary discipline is suspending judgment long enough to follow an idea past its first awkward draft into the shape it could actually take.

Judge
Evaluate · Refine · Kill

The hat for convergent thinking. The Judge tests the Artist’s ideas against the brief, the target, the brand, and reality. The Judge’s primary discipline is honest assessment — willing to kill an idea the room is excited about, and willing to keep one that nobody else likes if it solves the brief.

Warrior
Defend · Sell · Execute

The hat for everything after the idea is chosen. The Warrior carries the surviving idea past skeptics, past the budget meeting, past the watered-down compromise the client will instinctively propose. The Warrior’s primary discipline is endurance: an idea that gets killed in the conference room produced nothing.

The point of the four-hat model is not the hats; it is the one-at-a-time. In a team, you can assign these as actual roles for a working session — one person facilitates divergence (Artist), a different person facilitates convergence (Judge). Working solo, you have to discipline yourself to switch, and the easiest way to do it is physically. Switch seats. Move to a different room. Use a different notebook for evaluation than for generation. Use a different pen color. The physical signal makes the mental switch easier; the hardest version of the discipline is trying to do all four hats simultaneously at the same desk, which is what most people default to and what produces the kind of mediocre output everyone is then surprised by.

05
The Tactics

Concepting Techniques That Actually Fill the Page

Everything up to this point is process — the architecture of a good brainstorm. This section is the tactics. These are the moves that, once the room is structurally ready, get ideas out of heads and onto walls. Most of what follows comes from Luke Sullivan’s Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This, with supporting material from the prompts-deck approach popularized at Deck of Brilliance. None of them is a silver bullet. Used together, they reliably solve the “I’m staring at a blank page” problem that every creative person has standing in for impostor syndrome.

Just get started.
Don’t lean back waiting for the muse. Lean into the page. Chase ideas as fast and aggressively as you can. The reason this works is mechanical: you cannot edit a blank page, and your brain stops being able to generate ideas when the screen has been blank for more than a few minutes. Bad ideas written down become the launchpad for better ones written down next to them.
Build a word-and-image list.
Before you try to generate ideas about an outboard engine, you make a list of every word adjacent to it: fish, water, boat, two-stroke, gas, throttle, jetsam, Atlantic, Titanic. On another page, sketches and memes — anything visually connected to the category. These are the molecular building blocks of ideas. Stare at them. Soak them in. Then start trying to combine.
Say it straight. Then say it great.
The boring sentence the brief gave you is a lump of clay. Don’t try to make it brilliant on the first stroke. Write it down flat. Then rewrite it shorter. Punch it up. Say it in slang. Add attitude. Spin it. The deliberate flat version is what unsticks people who are too embarrassed to write down anything that isn’t already clever.
Dig shallow holes.
The brief covers an acre. Don’t excavate the whole acre. Dip your shovel in a dozen different places to see where there’s a glint. This is the “wide initial exploration” version of the diverge phase — test many lines of thinking briefly, before you commit to deepening one. The wrong move is to fall into the first rabbit hole and spend two days on what was never going to work.
Give yourself permission to fail.
Write the terrible ideas down. The novelist Anne Lamott calls these “shitty first drafts.” Sullivan calls them the price of admission to good drafts. Almost everyone resists this out of vanity — they cannot picture themselves writing down anything that doesn’t quiver and glow with brilliance. The cost of that vanity is the better idea that never arrives because the worse idea was never written down to push against.
Use a prompt deck.
When you’re stuck, rotate through a set of forcing functions: use visuals only. Use words only. Show the product without it being there. Before and after. Substitute. Combine. Symbolize. Distort. Change perspective. Invert. Each prompt is a mental operation that opens a different door. Deck of Brilliance sells a physical deck; many creative directors keep a homemade version on their desk.
Avoid your category’s low-hanging fruit.
Every category has its tired visual clichés. In insurance: grandfathers flying kites with grandchildren. In tech: earnest people wearing glasses with computer screens reflected in them. In financial services: a couple smiling at a laptop. Learn what iconography is overused in your category — and then don’t do that. The cliché is what your audience tunes out before they have parsed it.
Go in the opposite direction.
Sometimes called Bizarro World or 180° thinking. If your brand wants to go North, briefly consider going South. Write the anti-ad. Make fun of the brand. Mock the category. Use every stereotype about your target that you would never put in real work. You will not use any of this. But forcing yourself South for fifteen minutes makes the actual North look different when you swing back, and the things you find at north-northeast or north-northwest — the ideas you would have missed if you only ever looked due North — are often where the real campaigns live.
Don’t lead with exaggeration.
Sullivan puts “exaggeration” at the bottom of his concepting techniques deliberately. Exaggeration is the first move junior creatives reach for: this water heater is so good, what if ice cubes came out of the wrong faucet? Ice cubes, man, get it? Because the water’s really cold. It is the default move because it is easy. The default move is also why most exaggeration-based ads feel like exaggeration-based ads. Reach for it after you have tried five other prompts, not before.
A working session schedule

What this all adds up to, in practical terms, for a 90-minute creative session on a real brief: 10 minutes immersion (re-read the brief, the persona, last quarter’s research); 5 minutes silent word-and-image list; 20 minutes divergent generation with the prompt deck on the table, judgment deferred; 10-minute break, away from the table — this is the incubation that almost no one schedules; 20 minutes more divergent generation, including a deliberate Bizarro pass; 15 minutes convergent evaluation against the brief; 10 minutes to pick the top three or four ideas to develop further. The break in the middle does more work than people expect.

A note on tools

AI as a Brainstorming Partner

One question that did not exist when Sullivan wrote his first edition and now sits in every creative meeting: where does the chatbot fit in this process? The honest answer, in 2026, is that it fits in exactly one place and that it is excellent at that place: the early divergent phase. A large language model is, structurally, a quantity-without-judgment machine. Ask it for forty headlines and it will produce forty headlines. Ask it to keep going after you have read those forty and want more, and it will produce forty more. It has no ego invested in any of them and will not get bored. For a phase whose explicit rule is “defer judgment, go for quantity, welcome the weird,” this is a remarkable fit.

The places it does not fit are equally clear. It cannot do the empathy work that should precede the brainstorm, because it has no model of the actual person you are designing for beyond what you have told it — and it tends to flatter your existing description rather than probe it. It is a poor Judge: in convergent thinking it produces plausible-sounding rationales for whichever idea you point it at, which is the opposite of what the Judge hat is for. And it tends to default to the kind of category clichés the previous section told you to avoid — the grandfathers flying kites, the smiling couple with the laptop — because those are statistically the most common training examples of advertising in its category. The whole point of running the Bizarro move and the prompt deck is to escape that default, and the chatbot pulls you back toward it unless you actively wrestle it elsewhere.

The working pattern most of the creative directors writing about this in 2026 have converged on: use the model as an Artist’s assistant in the divergent phase, push it past the obvious answers with explicit prompts (“none of those — what if the product were used at 3am? what if a child explained it? what if it were free?”), then put the model away entirely for the convergent phase and the prototype phase. Piece #10 — AI in Creative Work takes this all the way down: which agencies are using it for what, where the production-scale use cases are, where the embarrassing failure modes are, and what creative directors interviewed at the 2026 Cannes Lions are actually saying about it. For this piece, the takeaway is narrower: it is a useful divergent-phase tool used carefully, and a hazardous one used as a Judge.

One more honest note, especially for the student who is going to be doing most of this work alone in a dorm room or a basement office rather than in an agency conference room. Brainstorming by yourself is no fun and it rarely works as well as a room would. Consider using an AI as a prompting machine to get you started — the model is a tireless interlocutor that will throw forty headlines back at you and not be hurt when you reject thirty-nine of them. Don’t like one of its lists? Push back. Ask it for more like Z and less like Y. Ask it to try the Bizarro pass. Ask it to do a word-and-image list for an adjacent category and then translate. It is not the Artist; you are. But as company in the divergent phase it beats being lonely, and the company is what keeps the page from staying blank.

Pulling it together

What This Adds Up To

The case throughout has been that brainstorming is not the soft part of the creative process — it is the part with the most structure, and the part where the most common failures are structural rather than personal. Two traps to name, two phases to separate, five stages to walk through, four hats to wear one at a time, eight or nine concepting techniques to rotate through inside the generative phase, and one chatbot to use in exactly one of those phases. Held together by the conviction Young was already arguing in 1939: creativity on demand is a learnable craft.

What you should be able to do after this piece
  • Name the two traps — no-prep brainstorming and generate-and-judge-in-one-pass — and recognize when a meeting you are in has fallen into one or both.
  • Run a divergent phase that defers judgment, with a quantity target, a time box, and either silent generation up front or a facilitator who enforces no-criticism in real time.
  • Take an actual break — ten to fifteen minutes minimum, longer if the schedule allows — between divergence and convergence, and recognize that the break is part of the technique, not a sign you ran out of ideas.
  • Walk a session through Young’s five stages — immersion, digestion, incubation, illumination, verification — or the equivalent Wallas / Sullivan framing if those terms come up.
  • Switch deliberately between Von Oech’s four hats — Explorer, Artist, Judge, Warrior — and use physical signals (different room, different notebook) to make the switch easier.
  • Use the concepting-technique deck — word and image lists, dig shallow holes, say it straight then say it great, Bizarro, avoid category clichés, save exaggeration for last — as forcing functions when the obvious lines are exhausted.
  • Place Design Thinking as the wider arc inside which brainstorming sits, and recognize that the two traps map onto the Empathize and Ideate stages of that arc.
  • Use AI in the divergent phase only, push it past category clichés with explicit anti-prompts, and recognize that it is a hazardous Judge.

Skip if you’re short on time

Six Games for When the Room Is Stuck

Sometimes the techniques in §05 do not unstick the room. The prompt deck has been exhausted, the word-and-image list is bone-dry, somebody has already done the Bizarro pass, and you still have nothing usable. The fix in this situation is almost never “keep doing what you were doing harder.” The fix is to change the format of the activity entirely. The six games below are the ones that come up most often in working sessions when normal brainstorming has run out of road. None of them is sophisticated. Several of them feel silly. That is part of what makes them work — they break the room out of the polite-meeting posture that is the actual reason the obvious lines are exhausted. Treat this section as a menu, not a sequence. You only need one or two of these to land for the session to be saved.

Storyboarding
Tell the brand’s story as a sequence on sticky notes — not the ad you would make, but the customer’s journey through the category, from “never heard of it” to “owns it.” Pin the notes up; rearrange them; delete the ones that aren’t earning their place. The output is rarely the campaign itself; the output is the moment in the journey where the campaign should actually intervene, which is what an unstuck room is usually missing. You can start anywhere in the sequence and fill in around it.
Mind-Mapping
A visual map of related ideas around a central node. Useful in the convergent phase — after ideas exist — when you need to see which ideas are cousins and which are outliers. Not great in the divergent phase, because forcing a thought into a specific bubble slows down free-flowing generation. The mistake is reaching for it too early; the trick is reaching for it when you already have forty post-its on the wall and need to see the shape of what you have.
Word-Banking
Force associations with random words. Pull a card, roll a deck, generate a list of words unrelated to the brief, then try to bridge each one to the brand. Curtis’s classroom version is a deck of cards labeled with absurd phrases — the famous “Wash a Pig” deck — that act as a palate-cleanser between rounds. The point is the involuntary association: the brain throws bridges between unrelated nouns whether you want it to or not, and some of those bridges are the angle you would not have arrived at by thinking about the brand.
Wishing
Generate the most unattainable, extreme, and impractical solutions you can think of — what if budget were unlimited, what if physics worked differently, what if the audience would do absolutely anything. The wishes themselves are throwaway. The exercise of going there opens angles that the “realistic” default never opens. After the wishing round, you ratchet each impossible idea back toward something the brand can actually do. The interesting ideas live in that ratcheting.
Brain-Writing (Slip-Writing)
Each person writes a few ideas silently on a sheet of paper. The sheet gets passed to the next person, who reads silently and adds their own ideas to it. Repeat until every sheet has been touched by every person. This is the formal version of the “silent first” rule from §02 and it is the single best technique for any room where one or two voices have been dominating the discussion. The output is consistently more and weirder than what the same group would produce out loud.
Tombstone
Write the brand’s obituary. If the brand died today, what would the epitaph be? Would anyone care? What would the eulogy at the funeral actually say — the honest version, not the press-release version? This is uncomfortable on purpose. Most mission statements are mush; the tombstone exercise forces a room to articulate what the brand actually stands for in the customer’s life, which is the raw material a real campaign builds from. Useful when the brief is generic and the brand truth has gone missing.

One operating note that covers all six. These games work when the room treats them as games — not as a serious diagnostic, not as a deliverable to the client, not as something to apologize for in the recap. Take the silly seriously while you are inside it; take it lightly the moment you step out. The output worth keeping will declare itself.

Vocabulary

Glossary

The casual-brainstorm school — the position this piece argues against. Treats brainstorming as something creative people just do, with no structure required. Tends to produce a meeting where the safe option wins by default and the room mistakes that outcome for consensus.

The two traps — (1) brainstorming without preparation so that the room generates ideas as themselves rather than as the target audience, and (2) failing to separate the generative phase from the evaluative phase so that judgment kills divergence before it has done its work.

Divergent thinking — the generative phase. Quantity-focused, judgment-deferred, weird-welcomed. The half of the work most under-served by undisciplined brainstorms.

Convergent thinking — the evaluative phase. Quality-focused, judgment-required. Where ideas get tested against the brief, the target, the brand, and the budget. Most ideas should die here.

Double diamond — the visual model that pairs two diverge-then-converge phases: one for understanding the right problem, one for designing the right solution. Codified by the British Design Council and inherited from the larger Design Thinking tradition.

Design Thinking — the five-stage process (Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test) developed at Stanford’s d.school and IDEO and now widely used in product, service, and marketing design. The frame inside which brainstorming as a technique lives.

Young’s five stages — Immersion, Digestion, Incubation, Illumination, Verification. James Webb Young’s 1939 elaboration of Graham Wallas’s 1926 four-stage model of creative thought, and still the canonical advertising-industry framing of how an idea actually gets produced.

The controlled daydream — Luke Sullivan’s phrase for the creative process. The controlled half is the load-up: immersion in the brief, the brand, the target. The daydream half is the incubation: stepping back so the unconscious can do its share. Most professionals can do the first and skip the second.

Von Oech’s four hats — Explorer, Artist, Judge, Warrior. The four mental roles required for creative work, one at a time. The trap is wearing more than one hat at once; the discipline is switching deliberately, ideally with a physical signal (different chair, different notebook).

Bizarro World / 180° thinking — deliberately working in the opposite direction of where the brief is asking you to go, in order to see angles you would otherwise miss when you swing back. The anti-ad as a tool, not as a final output.

Brainwriting / nominal group technique — running the divergent phase silently and individually before bringing ideas to the group. Consistently outperforms group-only brainstorming in the research literature, because it removes the loudest-critic effect from the generative phase.
