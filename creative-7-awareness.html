Awareness Creative: The Top of the Funnel

01
A wedding, two strangers

The soft hello, the business card, and which one you remember

Picture this. You’re at a wedding reception, parked near the bar with a drink, and two people you have never met approach you within thirty seconds of each other.

The first one launches into a story. Something funny about how the groom’s grandfather almost forgot the rings. Something quietly poignant about how the bride’s college roommate gave the speech that everyone is still talking about. You laugh, you nod, you stay engaged. The conversation lasts four minutes. You walk away thinking I liked that person.

The second one introduces themselves, hands you a business card, and asks if you’d like to schedule a thirty-minute call next week to discuss their consulting practice. You say something polite. You drop the card in a half-empty water glass on your way to find your spouse.

An hour later, which one are you still thinking about? Which one would you recognize across the room if you saw them again at the next event? Which one would you actually take the call with — if they ever circled back?

The storyteller is doing the job of the top of the funnel. The business-card guy is doing the job of the bottom of the funnel. Both are real. Both are necessary. They are not the same job, and they cannot be done in the same moment, by the same ad, to the same person.
Awareness vs. Performance, in one wedding

This whole section is about the storyteller.

02
What ToFU creative is actually for

Three jobs, in order: get noticed, be remembered, get linked to the right thing

By the time you reached this section, you have already met the funnel a few times — the standard ToFU / MoFU / BoFU model where customers move from “I didn’t know this existed” to “I’m comparing my options” to “I’m ready to buy.” Awareness creative lives at the very top. The job is not to close the sale, not to qualify the lead, not to drive the click. The job is simpler and harder: be remembered.

Three things have to happen at the top of the funnel for the ad to do its work, and they happen in this order:

  1. Get noticed. The person scrolling the feed, the person commuting past the billboard, the person half-watching a basketball game on TV — they have to register that an ad happened. This is harder in 2026 than it has ever been.
  2. Be remembered. Not necessarily right then. Days later. A week later. The brand has to attach itself to a feeling, a phrase, a visual, a character — something that survives the gap between exposure and need.
  3. Get linked to the right thing. When the need finally shows up — when the person is hungry, when the family needs a new SUV, when the boss says “find us an HR platform” — the brand has to be one of the names that surfaces. Marketers call this mental availability: the probability that your brand comes to mind in the moment that matters.

Everything else awareness creative does — humor, emotion, story, surprise — is in service of those three things.

This is also where the elaboration finding from §1 lands the hardest. Reinartz and Saffert’s study of 437 European campaigns found that ads viewers had to work to interpret — ads that asked the brain to do a little something — moved sales nearly twice as much per euro as ads that handed everything to the viewer flat. The top of the funnel is where you can afford to ask for that work, because you aren’t trying to close anything yet. You are trying to be the ad someone remembers tomorrow.

03
Most of what you need, you already have

The synthesis: every craft idea in this section converges at the top of the funnel

Here is the candid part. We have talked about creative at the top of the funnel in almost every previous section of this unit, just under different headings. So most of this piece is a recap with the funnel label finally attached.

In §1 we argued that creative carries somewhere between 60 and 70 percent of advertising effectiveness. That number is mostly an awareness-stage statement, because awareness is the stage where being interesting earns the brand the right to be remembered later.

In §3 we walked through what a Big Idea is and used Snickers’ “You’re Not You When You’re Hungry” as the centerpiece. Snickers is an awareness-creative case study from start to finish. The Big Idea is what an awareness ad is carrying. The execution — Betty White on the football field, Mr. Bean playing kung fu — is how the carrying gets done. Without a Big Idea, awareness creative becomes a string of attractive moments with nothing connecting them brand to brand or year to year.

In §4 we sorted appeals into rational and emotional buckets and walked through twelve execution styles. Awareness creative leans almost entirely emotional, because emotion is the part that encodes — the rational appeals have their own seat at the table further down the funnel. In §5 we spent a long time on humor — when it works, when it fails, what the vampire effect costs you when the joke is bigger than the brand. Humor lives almost entirely at the top of the funnel; you do not see many BoFU performance ads built around a punchline, and that is not an accident.

In §6 we compared the brand-at-end narrative shape of a thirty-second TV spot with the brand-in-five-seconds discipline of a skippable pre-roll. Both shapes are awareness creative. The first is what awareness creative looked like in the era of captive attention; the second is what awareness creative has to look like in the era of the skip button.

1+1=3 Awareness creative is where every craft idea in this section converges. The Big Idea (§3), the appeal (§4), the humor decision (§5), the narrative shape (§6) — they all pile into the same brief, because the top of the funnel is the one stage that needs all of them at once.

04
The piece we haven’t named yet

Distinctive brand assets: the compounding effect awareness creative gets to bank

There is one concept we have not formally given you, and the top of the funnel is the right place to install it. The shorthand comes from Byron Sharp and especially Jenni Romaniuk at the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute, and the term is distinctive brand assets — DBAs for short.

Distinctive Brand Asset (DBA)

The cognitive shortcut that makes brand recognition possible without the brand name

Any element other than your brand name that, when a person encounters it, makes them think of your brand. A color combination. A character. A sonic signature. A visual style. A tagline.

The asset works like a cognitive shortcut — the brand attaches to a feeling, a face, or a sound that the brain has wired across thousands of exposures. The McDonald’s golden arch is a DBA so strong that two seconds of arch in the corner of a video — no logo, no brand name, no voiceover — is enough for nearly every viewer over the age of three to know whose ad they are watching.

Every awareness ad either spends the assets you’ve built or builds the assets you’ll spend.

A handful of examples, mixed across decades and categories so you can see the form. Older legacy assets first: Aflac has the duck, Allstate has Mayhem, Geico has the gecko, Mastercard has the red-and-yellow circle interlock, Target has the dog and the bullseye, the Energizer bunny is still going. More recently — and worth knowing because they are 2026-current — Liquid Death has built a heavy-metal visual language that no other water brand could plausibly use; Duolingo has the green owl that the brand itself made famous through social; Surreal cereal does sarcastic black-and-white outdoor ads that are recognizable from a block away.

Two things to understand about DBAs that matter for awareness creative.

One. They are built, not declared.

A logo is not automatically a DBA. A color is not automatically a DBA. An element becomes a distinctive brand asset only after enough exposures across enough years that the brain has wired it to your brand. Which means every awareness ad you run is either spending the assets you have already built, or it is building the assets you will spend later. There is no neutral position. Brands that change their visual language every two years to chase a fresh look are essentially resetting the clock on this compounding effect — which is why the marketing director who insists on a complete creative overhaul every time leadership changes is, almost without realizing it, kneecapping the next ten years of mental availability work.

Two. They are what makes the brand-at-end video shape from §6 even possible.

The reason a Nike ad can withhold the swoosh for twenty-eight seconds and pay it off in the final two is that the swoosh is a DBA. If a brand without distinctive assets tried the same trick, the viewer would simply forget who the ad was for halfway through. DBAs are the cognitive collateral that the brand-at-end shape borrows against.

And the reason DBAs matter most at the top of the funnel: an ad that uses the same color, the same character, the same sound, the same tagline that the brand has been using for years works harder than the production budget alone could explain. Each new exposure borrows equity from every exposure that came before. This is one of the few legitimate compounding effects in marketing. Performance creative cannot compound this way — every BoFU ad has to win in the moment or it loses. Awareness creative gets to bank.

05
A quick note on format

Sound-off, vertical, native: the working environment of the form

Most awareness creative in 2026 will be encountered on a phone, in a feed, with the sound off, somewhere between a friend’s vacation photos and a clip of someone’s dog. That is the working environment of the form, and it imposes a few non-negotiables on the craft:

  • The visual hook lands in the first second — or the thumb scrolls. This is the second-tier version of the brand-in-five-seconds discipline from §6.
  • The ad reads with the sound off. Captions are not optional. The story has to be legible without audio for the simple reason that most viewers will never hear it.
  • Vertical and square out-perform widescreen on phones. Widescreen creative shrunk into a phone’s vertical feed is an ad telling the platform it does not understand the platform.
  • The DBA shows up early. Color, character, sound mark — at least one of them in the first frame, so the brand attaches to the moment of attention rather than the moment of skip.

None of this replaces story or humor or emotion. It just sets the constraints inside which all of those things now have to function.

06
The wedding eventually asks about dinner

The moment the storyteller becomes the helpful, no-pressure friend

Back to the wedding. The storyteller did her job. She earned your attention, she earned a laugh, she made you remember her. An hour later you find yourselves at the bar again. So what do you actually do? you ask her. She tells you. And because you already like her, you actually want to know.

That moment — the moment the conversation shifts from I’m glad I met you to tell me what you do — is the handoff from the top of the funnel to the middle. The tone changes. The pace changes. The job changes. Awareness was the icebreaker; what comes next is what HubSpot calls the helpful, no-pressure friend. Different brief, different creative, different success criteria.

That is where §8 picks up.

Quick-hit takeaway
  • Awareness creative is the storyteller, not the salesperson.
  • Its job is to be noticed, remembered, and attached to the brand — in that order.
  • The Big Idea, the appeal, the humor decision, the narrative shape, and the distinctive brand assets all pile into the same brief, because the top of the funnel is the one stage that needs all of them at once.
  • Awareness creative gets to bank; performance creative has to win in the moment.

Glossary

Terms used in this section

Mental availability
The probability that your brand comes to mind when a buying need arises. Term popularized by Byron Sharp and the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute. The strategic outcome that awareness creative is trying to produce.
Distinctive brand asset (DBA)
Any non-name element — color, character, sound, tagline, visual style — that triggers brand recognition on its own. Built through consistent repetition across years, not declared in a brand book.
Top of the funnel (ToFU)
The awareness stage of the customer journey, where the prospect is not yet evaluating options. Creative job: be remembered, not close the sale.
Vampire effect
Carried over from §5. When a creative device — usually humor or a celebrity — is so attention-grabbing that it drains memory from the brand it is supposed to be selling. Awareness creative dies of this more than any other cause.
