How Can a Brand Earn More Publicity?

Publicity is a transaction third parties are always free to refuse. They cover stories, not brands — and the difference between earning coverage and being told to buy an ad instead usually comes down to whether the brand handed someone else a story their audience already had a reason to care about.

Working Definitions

PR, Publicity, and Earned Media

These three terms get used interchangeably in casual conversation and it costs nothing to keep them separate.

Public relations is the broad strategic process of building and maintaining relationships between an organization and the publics that matter to it — customers, employees, communities, government, investors, media, partners. PR includes reputation management, crisis communication, community relations, public affairs, events, employee communication, and support for cause programs. Trying to appear in the news is one slice of it, not the whole job (PRSA, n.d.-a).

Publicity is the narrower part of PR concerned with getting an independent third party — a newspaper, TV station, trade publication, podcaster, newsletter writer, creator, professional association, nonprofit, university, or community organization — to give a brand, person, or cause public attention. PRSA frames media relations as the work of building relationships with journalists to obtain that coverage and to respond when reporters come asking (PRSA, n.d.-b).

Earned media is the exposure that results when the publicity work lands. An article, an interview, a product mention, an independent review, a podcast discussion, a creator post, a customer recommendation, a mention by another organization. The defining trait is that the brand did not purchase the coverage and does not fully control it. A creator post is not earned media when the creator was paid or contractually required to publish. A partner post is not purely earned when the partnership guaranteed it. Earned is the word for what someone else chose to say about you without being obligated to.

The Central Rule

Third Parties Cover Stories, Not Brands

A brand earns publicity by giving another party something that will interest that party’s audience. The journalist, podcaster, association editor, or creator is not there to distribute the brand’s message. They are there to do their own job for their own readers, and a pitch lands when it makes their job easier.

One quick test that filters most pitches before they go out:

The remove-the-name test

Would this still be interesting if the company’s name were removed from the headline?

“Linz introduces a Father’s Day steak package” is mainly an advertisement. The story collapses the moment the brand name comes out. “Women are driving online premium-meat purchases, even though most meat advertising still targets men” is a category-trend story that a brand can credibly contribute data, customers, and expertise to. The brand is a source. The story is about something larger.

Two follow-up tests catch the rest. Is the main purpose to announce something, or to persuade people to buy it? And can the third party tell the story honestly without repeating the brand’s sales claims? If the answers point toward “persuade” and “no,” the right channel for that work is advertising, not publicity.

The Seven Hooks

What Actually Earns Coverage

Seven types of information reliably interest third parties. A given story rarely contains all seven. The strong ones tend to contain three or four.

1. Original data that reveals something new

Brands often hold customer, transaction, search, ordering, or usage information that outsiders cannot see. The strong findings are the ones that explain a larger behavior, not the ones that praise the company.

  • Weak “Ninety-two percent of our customers are satisfied.”
  • Better “Women account for 67% of our online steak purchasers.”
  • Stronger “Women increasingly buy premium meat online, while category advertising continues to portray men as the primary buyer.”

Methodology matters. Sample size, time period, who was included, how the questions were worded, what the data cannot prove. In Cision’s 2026 journalist survey, 47% said an ideal pitch should include compelling data (Reynolds, 2026).

2. A timely connection to something already happening

A brand becomes useful when it helps explain an issue already getting attention — inflation, tariffs, supply-chain shortages, new regulations, technology shifts, seasonal behavior, weather, a viral moment, a major cultural event. A local butcher can explain how cattle prices are hitting local shoppers. A national grocery company can provide purchasing data showing how baskets are changing. The brand contributes evidence, expertise, customers, or local context. It does not force its product into an unrelated news event.

3. Measurable impact

“Company partners with a food bank” is usually weaker than “A new cold-storage program will let three counties distribute 40,000 additional refrigerated meals.” Useful impact information names jobs created or lost, people served, money or time saved, prices changed, access expanded, waste reduced, neighborhoods affected, or an industry practice that moved. Specific consequences make a story more credible and less promotional in the same sentence.

4. Something surprising, unusual, or counterintuitive

A product launch is not automatically news. It becomes interesting when it is the first credible example of something, when it overturns a common assumption, when it solves a recognized problem differently, when it produces an unexpected result, or when it is visually distinctive enough to anchor a story.

“Company launches another barbecue sauce” belongs in advertising or a product roundup. “Company develops foods for people whose sense of taste changes during medical treatment” may contain scientific, human-interest, and product-development angles at once — provided the claims are responsible and supported.

5. A strong person-centered story

People are normally more interesting than organizations. A founder solving a problem from personal experience. An employee with an unusual path. A customer who represents a broader behavioral shift. A family-business succession. A company recovering from a fire, a flood, a failure, a reinvention. The person should help explain something larger about an industry, a community, or a cultural change. “An entrepreneur followed a dream” is too generic to land anywhere.

6. Expertise, access, and useful material

Brands earn recurring coverage by becoming reliable sources rather than constantly pitching company profiles. The useful contributions are credible experts, customers willing to be interviewed, access to facilities or unusual processes, original photographs and video, clear background material, fast commentary on a breaking issue, and an honest explanation of what is changing in the industry.

Cision reported that 63% of journalists saw value in PR professionals connecting them with appropriate sources, and 57% valued access to key people or places (Reynolds, 2025). The brand that helps a reporter file a better story this week is the brand that gets called first the next time the reporter needs a comment.

7. New, exclusive, or advance information

A third party has more reason to cover a story when it receives something that is not available everywhere else — an exclusive interview, advance research, an embargoed announcement, behind-the-scenes access, a unique case study, or a dataset it can analyze on its own terms. Cision found that 45% of journalists wanted embargoed or early-access information (Reynolds, 2026). The standard professional norm: do not offer the same “exclusive” to several outlets simultaneously. The word stops meaning anything the second time it gets used.

What usually leads to “buy an ad”

A sale or coupon. A routine product launch. An ordinary store opening or anniversary. A packaging redesign. A generic industry award. A minor executive hire. An influencer collaboration. A small charitable check. A sponsorship without measurable impact. An event whose actual purpose is to sell products. A release whose only message is “We are excited to announce.” These items may still fit shopping guides, product-review sections, community calendars, trade newsletters, owned social, or paid advertising. They simply do not earn general-interest coverage on their own.

The Broader Ecosystem

Publicity Beyond Traditional Media

Traditional news coverage still matters, but the third-party ecosystem is wider than it was a decade ago. Three overlapping categories are worth thinking about separately.

Media and independent publishers. Newspapers, TV, radio, magazines, trade publications, newsletters, podcasts, Substack writers, and specialist websites. The mainstream of earned media, with the standard practices that have governed it for a hundred years.

Content creators. Independent reviewers, educators, chefs, analysts, local personalities, and industry commentators. Their attention is earned only when they retain the choice of whether and what to publish — the moment a payment or contractual obligation enters the relationship, the post moves from earned to paid, even if the language stays casual.

Related organizations. Trade associations, chambers of commerce, nonprofits, universities, professional groups, community organizations, suppliers, distributors, tourism boards, economic-development organizations. These organizations talk about brands when doing so advances their own mission. A chamber will publicize job creation. A university will discuss a research partnership. A nonprofit will describe measurable community impact. A chef will demonstrate an unusual product or technique. The pattern is the same as with media: make the information useful to the third party rather than asking them to do you a favor.

Creator journalism deserves its own note. Muck Rack found that 82% of creator journalists said at least some of their work begins with PR pitches, although 72% said most pitches are irrelevant. Creator journalists especially valued relevant interview access, clear fit with their coverage, original research, quality images, and exclusive information (Muck Rack, 2025). The mechanics are familiar; the gatekeepers are new.

Scale

Small Brands vs. Large National Companies

Small and local brands

Small brands cannot compete through national scale or celebrity. They compete through proximity, specificity, access, and personality. Their strongest angles are a local business illustrating a national trend, unusual local customer data, meaningful hiring or investment, a founder or customer with a real story, a recognizable community problem being solved, or a visually interesting process most people have never seen.

“Local store celebrates its fifth anniversary” is weak. “Store reaches its fifth anniversary after surviving a flood and helping reactivate an empty commercial block” contains conflict, recovery, community impact, and local relevance — four hooks at once.

Small brands also benefit from working outward through a sequence of friendly outlets: community newsletters and neighborhood blogs first, then local newspapers, radio, and television, then regional business media, then trade publications, then national niche outlets, then broad national media. Each placement makes the next one easier because it provides credentialing the next editor can verify with a click.

Large national brands

Large brands already have prominence. The problem is that routine announcements face a higher editorial threshold — the bigger the company, the bigger the news has to be to clear the bar. National coverage typically requires scale or consequence: proprietary datasets, major shifts in pricing or consumer behavior, acquisitions or closures, labor and supply-chain effects, significant innovation, public-policy implications, category disruption, or a major strategic failure or reversal.

The large-brand test

What changes for customers, workers, competitors, communities, or the industry because this happened?

Large companies can also create recurring publicity through an annual consumer index, a quarterly price tracker, a regional behavior report, an industry benchmark, or a category forecast. The strongest of these eventually become resources journalists expect and cite by name — Mary Meeker’s old Internet Trends Report, Edelman’s Trust Barometer, the Stack Overflow Developer Survey. Recurring publicity beats one-off publicity, because the asset accumulates credibility every year it ships.

In Practice

Pitch the Right Story to the Right Source

Relevance matters more than the size of the media list. Cision found that 86% of journalists reject pitches that do not fit their audience or beat, while 79% are more likely to engage with relevant outreach (Reynolds, 2026). Muck Rack similarly reported that 73% reject pitches because the subject sits outside their coverage area, and 65% prefer pitches under 200 words (Dunleavy, 2025). A pitch list of fifty journalists who do not cover your category is worth less than a pitch list of five who do.

A strong pitch quickly answers six questions: What happened? Why does this particular audience care? What is new or surprising? What evidence is available? Who can be interviewed? What visuals, data, or access can the brand provide?

  • Weak Exciting news from the Linz Shop!
  • Stronger Indiana purchasing data shows women driving premium-meat sales

The weak version asks the reporter to care. The strong version tells the reporter what the story is and why their readers will read it.

The compact publicity scorecard

Before approaching any media outlet, creator, or outside organization, walk through five questions.

  • Audience Does this matter to the third party’s particular audience?
  • News Is it new, timely, surprising, or consequential?
  • Proof Do we have credible data, examples, and people who can verify it?
  • Independence Can the third party discuss it without sounding like a salesperson?
  • Assets Can we provide interviews, access, images, video, data, or exclusivity?

A story that satisfies four or five has a real earned-publicity opportunity. A story that satisfies one or two probably belongs in advertising, owned content, or direct promotion — and the honest move is to put it there.

The better question for the whole exercise is not the one most marketing teams start with. “How do we get publicity for what we want to announce?” tends to produce press releases that nobody runs. The more useful framing is:

What do we know, observe, measure, or provide that helps another party tell a story its audience already has a reason to care about?

Takeaway
  • PR is the relationship work. Publicity is the narrower piece of getting a third party to cover you. Earned media is what results when the publicity lands. Paid creator posts are not earned, even when the language sounds casual.
  • Third parties cover stories, not brands. The remove-the-name test — would this be interesting if the company name came out of the headline? — filters most pitches before they leave the building.
  • Seven hooks reliably earn coverage: original data, a timely angle, measurable impact, surprise, person-centered stories, expertise/access, and exclusivity. Strong stories carry three or four at once.
  • The ecosystem is wider than newspapers: independent publishers, content creators, and related organizations (associations, chambers, universities, nonprofits) all cover brands when doing so serves their own missions.
  • Small brands compete on proximity, specificity, and personality, and ladder outward from local to national. Large brands face a higher editorial threshold and benefit from recurring branded reports that journalists eventually cite by name.
  • Relevance beats list size. 86% of journalists reject off-beat pitches; 65% want pitches under 200 words. Pitch five people who cover your category before pitching fifty who don’t.
  • The reframe: stop asking “how do we get publicity for what we want to announce?” Start asking “what do we know that helps someone else tell a story their audience already cares about?”
