The Marketing Funnel — Where Each Tool Plays

Marketers have been modeling the customer's journey from "never heard of this product" to "bought it twice" since 1898 — and the framework has aged differently than you'd think.

Earlier Models Funnel Coverage

Marketers have been trying to model the customer's journey from "never heard of this product" to "bought it twice" since at least 1898, when an advertising writer named E. St. Elmo Lewis sketched out a four-stage model called AIDA: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. The model survived in textbooks for over a hundred and twenty years, which is a remarkable run for a framework developed when most ads were posters and most sales happened door-to-door. It survived partly because it was correct in its general shape (people do move through stages on the way to purchase) and partly because nothing simpler came along to replace it.

The modern reformulation collapses Interest and Desire into a single middle stage called Consideration, giving us three stages instead of four. Marketers refer to them as ToFU (Top of Funnel, Awareness), MoFU (Middle of Funnel, Consideration), and BoFU (Bottom of Funnel, Action). The relabeling sounds like jargon, but the three-stage version is cleaner. The original distinction between Interest and Desire was always slippery. Strong (1925), who popularized AIDA in marketing, admitted the two stages blurred into each other. Collapsing them lets the framework map directly onto the brand-versus-performance budget split that defines most modern marketing planning.

The shape of the funnel matters. It is a funnel and not a cylinder because every stage leaks. Of the people who become aware of your brand, only some will consider it. Of the people who consider, only some will buy. Of the people who buy once, only some will buy again. The job of marketing analytics, increasingly, is to measure the leak rate at each stage and figure out which stage is bleeding worst. A brand with weak awareness has a top-of-funnel problem. A brand with strong awareness and weak sales has a middle-or-bottom problem. The diagnosis matters because the fix is different in each case, and a lot of marketing budget gets wasted on solving the wrong problem.

A few things in this map deserve a note. Retargeting appears at two stages because it works in both places. It is the mechanic that pulls people from Consideration back down to Action when they have drifted. Search advertising sits at the bottom because search ads only appear when someone is already searching for you, which means they are already aware. You cannot build awareness with search; you can only harvest it. Sales promotions — coupons, discounts, limited-time offers — live at the bottom because they convert people who were already close, not because they pull cold prospects into consideration.

3.1 Earlier Models

The funnel above synthesizes more than a century of similar models. Three earlier formulations are worth knowing, both because they still appear in textbooks and because they show where the modern version came from.

Figure 3.2
AIDA — The Original (Lewis, 1898)
E. St. Elmo Lewis's original four-stage model. The ancestor of every modern funnel.

The 1898 version was about advertising copy specifically, but the structure generalized. Each stage names a job the message has to do to move a prospect to the next stage. "Attention" earns the look, "Interest" earns the engagement, "Desire" earns the want, "Action" earns the sale. The labels are dated but the underlying intuition has held up. Strong (1925) brought AIDA into mainstream marketing teaching, and from there it spread to nearly every advertising textbook published in the twentieth century.

Figure 3.3
Enhanced AIDA — With Loyalty and Advocacy
Interest and Desire collapse into Consideration; two new stages handle post-purchase. Blue tones mark the post-purchase phase as visually distinct from the acquisition funnel.

The next significant addition was the recognition that the funnel doesn't end at purchase. A satisfied customer becomes a loyal customer, and a loyal customer can become an advocate — someone whose word-of-mouth pulls new prospects into the top of the funnel. This is where the "funnel" metaphor starts to break down, because the bottom of the funnel begins feeding the top of it. Modern frameworks either extend the funnel downward (the version above) or replace it with a loop (the version below).

Figure 3.4
The Consumer Decision Journey — McKinsey's Loyalty Loop
Based on Court, D., Elzinga, D., Mulder, S., & Vetvik, O. J. (2009). The Consumer Decision Journey. McKinsey Quarterly, June 2009. Read the original.

McKinsey's reframe replaces the linear funnel with a loop. The four stages are still recognizable — they map roughly onto Awareness, Consideration, Action, and Postpurchase — but the diagram emphasizes that the journey doesn't end at purchase. The loyalty loop is the central insight: customers who are happy with the product skip the early stages entirely on their next purchase. They don't return to "initial consideration." They go straight to "moment of purchase." For a brand with strong retention, this inner loop is where most of the long-term revenue comes from.

The pedagogical use of these older models isn't that any of them is the "right" one. It's that each version of the funnel highlights something the others don't. AIDA is sharpest about the cognitive work of advertising copy. The expanded version foregrounds post-purchase. McKinsey's loop refuses to pretend the journey ends at the cash register. The modern three-stage funnel (Figure 3.1 above) is the working consensus, but knowing the variants helps explain why different marketing texts use slightly different vocabulary for the same underlying idea.

3.2 Funnel Coverage Is the Job

The argumentative point that comes out of all of this, and the one worth carrying forward into the rest of this course: most marketing failures are not creative failures. They are funnel-coverage failures. A brand that runs only bottom-funnel tactics eventually starves, because nothing is filling the top of the funnel with new prospects. A brand that runs only top-funnel tactics generates awareness with no path to purchase, which is the Solo Stove problem we will examine later. The marketing manager's real job, when they are being honest about it, is to look at the funnel and ask: where are we under-invested? Not which campaign won this week? The first question is strategic. The second is theater.

One preview of where this leads. The research on long-run marketing effectiveness, summarized most influentially by Binet and Field at the Institute of Practitioners in Advertising, suggests that established brands should be investing roughly 60% of their marketing budget in upper-funnel brand-building and 40% in lower-funnel performance activation. The 60/40 principle is not a universal law — startups and luxury brands and seasonal businesses each have good reasons to deviate — but it is the closest thing to a default that modern marketing has. The Brand vs. Performance page goes into the 60/40 framework in depth.

Takeaway
  • The modern funnel has three stages: ToFU (Awareness), MoFU (Consideration), BoFU (Action).
  • Earlier models — AIDA, the expanded loyalty/advocacy version, McKinsey's loyalty loop — each surface something the others miss. The modern three-stage version is the working consensus.
  • Different marketing tools do their best work at different stages. Coverage at every stage is the job.
  • Most marketing failures are not creative failures. They are funnel-coverage failures.
  • The 60/40 principle (60% brand-building, 40% performance) is the default starting point for established brands.
