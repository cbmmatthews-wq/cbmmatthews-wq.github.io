01
Overview

What Marketing Actually Looks Like

Walk into any room of business students or working professionals and ask them what they know about marketing, and you'll get a remarkably consistent set of answers. Marketing is dead. Influencers run everything now. Nobody watches TV ads anymore. Brick-and-mortar retail is finished. TikTok is the only thing that matters. Soon AI will write all the copy and we won't need agencies. Each of these claims contains a small grain of truth wrapped around a much larger error, and each of them gets repeated at conference panels, on LinkedIn, and in pitch decks by people who should know better.

Some of those people are your future colleagues. Some of them are your future bosses. A few of them, if your career goes the way you might want it to, will be you. The purpose of this course is to keep that last one from happening.

Marketing is not dead. American companies spent over $390 billion on marketing communications in 2024, the highest figure ever recorded and roughly twice what the same companies spent ten years ago. Influencers are real but represent a small slice of total ad spending, smaller than radio, smaller than direct mail, smaller than out-of-home. Television, despite a decade of obituaries, still attracts more ad revenue than any single digital channel except paid search. Direct mail, which everyone assumes is gone, remains a $40 billion industry and is consistently among the highest-ROI channels by category. Physical retail accounts for about 84% of U.S. consumer purchases. The "TikTok is everything" position, popular among the people who make their living advising companies on TikTok, has not survived contact with the data.

If this pattern feels familiar, it's because it repeats every generation. In the 1920s, radio was going to finish off newspapers. In the 1950s, television was going to finish off radio. In the 1990s, the internet was going to finish off all of them. In the 2010s, social media was going to finish off paid advertising. Each disruption was partly real and partly oversold, and each one was used by someone to sell something. The current AI-will-replace-everything story is the latest version of a pattern that has been running for a hundred years. Real change is happening. The story being sold about it is, as usual, mostly wrong.

This course is not built to defend traditional media against digital, or vice versa. That's not the interesting question, and it's not a question marketers actually face. The real question, the one that matters in every marketing decision a company will make, is how to layer the available tools so they work together for both long-term brand equity and short-term commercial impact. A good marketer does not pick the channel before figuring out the problem. They do not start with "we should be on TikTok" or "we need an influencer strategy" or "AI can write our content calendar." They start with: what is this brand's actual challenge, what is its actual opportunity, and what combination of tools is best suited to that specific situation? The channel is the last decision, not the first. Most of the bad marketing you see in the world is the result of getting that order backwards.

This is also not a course about how to use AI to automate your marketing. AI is a real tool, and the best marketers are using it for real work: research, analysis, draft iteration, asset variation, audience segmentation. But the version of AI marketing that promises "steal my content calendar" or "the seven hooks that go viral every time" is selling you exactly the slop that makes audiences trust brands less, not more. The companies winning right now are the ones using AI as a partner to faster, sharper, more disciplined work, not as a shortcut around the work itself.

What this course will try to give you is the basics, which turn out to be remarkably durable. The frameworks that explain why a great brand can charge more than a commodity. Why influencers fail more often than they succeed. Why measurement is harder than the dashboards make it look. Why coordinated campaigns outperform stand-alone tactics across nearly every category measured. The channels have changed. The economics have changed. The vocabulary keeps changing. But the underlying logic of how brands get built, how customers get reached, and how attention gets converted into purchase is, in its fundamentals, the same logic John Wanamaker was working with in 1900 when he made the famous complaint that half his advertising was wasted and he didn't know which half.

We still don't fully know which half. We know more than Wanamaker did, but less than the dashboard zealots claim. Reckoning honestly with what we know and what we don't is most of what marketing strategy actually is.

"Half the money I spend on advertising is wasted; the trouble is I don't know which half."
— John Wanamaker, ca. 1900

The Wanamaker quote is the foundational measurement problem of the field. It is also, more than a century later, still the most honest summary anyone has produced. The course will return to it.

Takeaway
  • Marketing is a strategic function, not a service function. It exists to figure out who would buy what you sell, why, and how to reach them.
  • The pop culture conversation about marketing — what's hot, what's dead, what's disrupting what — is mostly wrong, and mostly being sold by someone with something to gain.
  • Wanamaker's century-old problem, knowing what works, is still the problem. Most of what's changed is the vocabulary.

The Basics

The Four Ps, Briefly, Because Some of You Skipped This Class in Undergrad

Marketing, as a business discipline, is built around four interlocking decisions a company has to make about anything it sells. They were named in 1960 by a professor named Jerome McCarthy, who liked the alliteration. The framework has survived sixty-five years of academic critique because the alternatives proposed to replace it — the 7 Ps, the 4 Cs, the SAVE framework — keep collapsing back into it. The four Ps are durable because they describe the actual decisions a marketing department has to make, not the language we wish those decisions came in.

Product is what you sell, and increasingly it includes the service experience, the warranty, the support, the packaging, the unboxing, and the brand identity that surrounds the thing in the box. Apple sells iPhones, but it also sells a retail experience, an ecosystem, and a self-perception. Those aren't separate products. They're all part of the product, and pretending otherwise costs you the ability to compete with companies that understand the broader definition.

Price is what the customer gives up to acquire it. Usually money, but also time, attention, switching costs, and the cognitive effort of figuring out whether the purchase was a good idea. Price is the single fastest signal a company sends about how it wants to be perceived. A $400 sweater and a $40 sweater communicate different things before anyone has examined the fabric.

Place is where and how the customer can buy it: the distribution channel, the retail footprint, the e-commerce experience, the wholesale relationships, the geography of availability. A product on the shelf at Walmart and the same product on the shelf at Neiman Marcus is, in some real sense, not the same product. The channel is part of the meaning.

Promotion is everything the company does to communicate with current and prospective customers: advertising, public relations, sales promotion, direct marketing, digital and social media, personal selling. It is the only P that exists primarily to send information. The other three Ps also send information (price signals quality, distribution signals scarcity, the product itself signals competence), but Promotion is the P whose actual job is signaling. This is the P the course is about.

Here is the part most introductory treatments soft-pedal. The four Ps are not four independent dials a marketer can turn separately. They are four expressions of a single underlying positioning decision, which is something we will return to throughout the semester. A premium price with discount-channel distribution and performance-marketing-only promotion is incoherent, and consumers notice incoherence faster than marketers admit. If the price says luxury but the distribution says commodity and the promotion says clearance, the brand does not survive the contradiction. The marketing-mix decisions have to align, because the customer experiences them as a single message about what this thing is and who it is for.

Takeaway
  • The four Ps (Product, Price, Place, Promotion) are not four separate decisions. They are four expressions of a single positioning decision.
  • Promotion is the P this course focuses on, but the others are always present in the background.
  • Incoherence across the four Ps is the fastest way to confuse a market and damage a brand.
03
Integration

Promotion's Relationship with the Other Ps

The reason this course focuses on Promotion but keeps invoking the others is that Promotion is the most visible P to the consumer and the most entangled P with the rest of the marketing mix. You can have a great product, a smart price, and broad distribution, and still fail commercially because no one has heard of you. You can also have aggressive, well-funded promotion behind a mediocre product, price it correctly, distribute it widely, and watch the promotion accelerate your failure rather than prevent it. Promotion amplifies what's underneath it. If what's underneath is good, promotion compounds. If what's underneath is bad, promotion helps more people find out faster.

This is what the marketing literature calls promotion writes checks the product has to cash, and it explains why the smartest CMOs spend a meaningful share of their attention on Product decisions even though Product is technically someone else's job. A promotional campaign that promises an experience the product can't deliver does long-term damage that no amount of additional advertising can repair. The reverse is also true and underrated: a great product with no promotion behind it can sit in the market for years, slowly losing share to inferior competitors who simply showed up in more conversations. Quality alone does not sell. Quality plus salience sells. Promotion is how a brand stays salient.

The relationship between Promotion and Price deserves its own moment, because it generates more strategic confusion than any other intersection in the four Ps. Promotion can be used to justify a price (this product is premium and here is why), or it can be used to bypass a price decision through discounts and sales promotions (this product is on sale this week). Both are legitimate. Mixing them carelessly is not. Brands that alternate between premium-image advertising and constant discount promotion teach their customers to wait for the discount, which is a brand-equity problem that takes years to repair.

The relationship between Promotion and Place matters because the channel a product sells through limits and shapes what the promotion can credibly say. A direct-to-consumer brand can promise things — fit guarantees, return policies, personalization — that a brand sold through 8,000 retailers structurally cannot. A brand sold through Walmart has to promote itself differently from a brand sold through specialty boutiques, because the audiences in those channels are not the same people with the same expectations.

So when this course talks about Promotion, assume the other three Ps are quietly present in the background of every decision. The course is just not pretending the other three are independent.

Takeaway
  • Promotion amplifies what's underneath it. A great product gets compounded. A bad product gets exposed faster.
  • Promotion writes checks the product has to cash.
  • Quality alone does not sell. Quality plus salience does.
04
Targeting

A Quick Word About Who You're Talking To

We will come back to this in detail later in the course, including a hands-on exercise with Claritas' segmentation tools and Meta's audience-targeting features. For now, three concepts you need installed before the rest of the unit makes sense.

Segmentation is the recognition that no market is one undifferentiated mass of consumers. Even the most universal products — water, salt, electricity — get sold differently to different groups, because different groups want different things from the same product. Segmentation is the analytical work of dividing a market into groups that share something meaningful: a demographic profile, a set of preferences, a stage of life, a level of need. The bases for segmentation are many, and we will cover them later. The point for now is that segments exist whether or not the marketer acknowledges them, and a marketer who does not segment is just choosing to be average across a population that is not.

Targeting is the strategic choice of which segments to pursue. A company with a $50 million marketing budget cannot afford to be relevant to everyone, and the attempt to be relevant to everyone produces marketing that is relevant to no one. Targeting is the decision to invest disproportionately in the segments where you can win and to deliberately under-invest in the rest. This is harder than it sounds. The segments you don't target represent revenue you are choosing not to chase, and explaining that choice to a board or a sales team requires conviction.

Positioning is the answer to a single question: in the minds of the people we have chosen to target, what do we want to be known for? Positioning is not what your product is. It is what your product means to the customer, which is a different thing and a harder thing to control. Volvo positions itself around safety. Rolex positions itself around achievement. Liquid Death positions itself around making canned water absurd. None of these positions are properties of the physical product. They are deliberate constructions, built through years of consistent promotion, packaging, pricing, and distribution decisions that all reinforce the same idea.

Here is the connection that earns this section its space. Every decision you will see in this course — what message, what channel, what mix, what budget — is downstream of decisions about segmentation, targeting, and positioning. When we talk later about whether to spend on brand or performance marketing, the answer depends on which segment you are trying to reach and what you want them to think. When we talk about the marketing funnel, the funnel is a funnel for a specific target. STP is not a sidebar to IMC. STP is the input that makes IMC make sense. We will do it properly later. For now, just know it is there.

Takeaway
  • Segmentation: dividing a market into meaningfully different groups.
  • Targeting: picking which of those groups to invest in.
  • Positioning: deciding what you want to mean in their minds.
  • STP is the input that makes every later marketing decision coherent.
05
The Funnel

The Marketing Funnel — Where Each Tool Plays

Marketers have been trying to model the customer's journey from "never heard of this product" to "bought it twice" since at least 1898, when an advertising writer named E. St. Elmo Lewis sketched out a four-stage model called AIDA: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. The model survived in textbooks for over a hundred and twenty years, which is a remarkable run for a framework developed when most ads were posters and most sales happened door-to-door. It survived partly because it was correct in its general shape (people do move through stages on the way to purchase) and partly because nothing simpler came along to replace it.

The modern reformulation collapses Interest and Desire into a single middle stage called Consideration, giving us three stages instead of four. Marketers refer to them as ToFU (Top of Funnel, Awareness), MoFU (Middle of Funnel, Consideration), and BoFU (Bottom of Funnel, Action). The relabeling sounds like jargon, but the three-stage version is cleaner. The original distinction between Interest and Desire was always slippery. Strong (1925), who popularized AIDA in marketing, admitted the two stages blurred into each other. Collapsing them lets the framework map directly onto the brand-versus-performance budget split that defines most modern marketing planning.

The shape of the funnel matters. It is a funnel and not a cylinder because every stage leaks. Of the people who become aware of your brand, only some will consider it. Of the people who consider, only some will buy. Of the people who buy once, only some will buy again. The job of marketing analytics, increasingly, is to measure the leak rate at each stage and figure out which stage is bleeding worst. A brand with weak awareness has a top-of-funnel problem. A brand with strong awareness and weak sales has a middle-or-bottom problem. The diagnosis matters because the fix is different in each case, and a lot of marketing budget gets wasted on solving the wrong problem.

"I'm ready to buy."

A few things in this map deserve a note. Retargeting appears at two stages because it works in both places. It is the mechanic that pulls people from Consideration back down to Action when they have drifted. Search advertising sits at the bottom because search ads only appear when someone is already searching for you, which means they are already aware. You cannot build awareness with search; you can only harvest it. Sales promotions — coupons, discounts, limited-time offers — live at the bottom because they convert people who were already close, not because they pull cold prospects into consideration.

The argumentative point here, and the one worth carrying forward into the rest of this course: most marketing failures are not creative failures. They are funnel-coverage failures. A brand that runs only bottom-funnel tactics eventually starves, because nothing is filling the top of the funnel with new prospects. A brand that runs only top-funnel tactics generates awareness with no path to purchase, which is the Solo Stove problem we will examine later. The marketing manager's real job, when they are being honest about it, is to look at the funnel and ask: where are we under-invested? Not which campaign won this week? The first question is strategic. The second is theater.

One preview of where this leads. The research on long-run marketing effectiveness, summarized most influentially by Binet and Field at the Institute of Practitioners in Advertising, suggests that established brands should be investing roughly 60% of their marketing budget in upper-funnel brand-building and 40% in lower-funnel performance activation. The 60/40 principle is not a universal law — startups and luxury brands and seasonal businesses each have good reasons to deviate — but it is the closest thing to a default that modern marketing has. We will come back to it in real depth in Unit 1.

Takeaway
  • The funnel has three stages: ToFU (Awareness), MoFU (Consideration), BoFU (Action).
  • Different marketing tools do their best work at different stages. Coverage at every stage is the job.
  • Most marketing failures are not creative failures. They are funnel-coverage failures.
  • The 60/40 principle (60% brand-building, 40% performance) is the default starting point for established brands. We will examine it in detail later.

Where We Are Headed

The Intro has installed the working vocabulary the rest of the course will use. The four Ps, the relationships between them, the basics of STP, the marketing funnel, and the underlying argument that the channel is the last decision a marketer should make, not the first.

Unit 1 picks up from here and goes deep on the discipline of Integrated Marketing Communications: what IMC actually is, why the older definition got it half-wrong, how the PESO framework organizes the modern channel inventory, what the six elements of the promotional mix do and where they live in the funnel, how customers actually encounter brands across multiple touch points, why repetition compounds across channels but wears out within them, and how to think about the brand-versus-performance budget split that shapes nearly every marketing decision a company makes.

Unit 2 then turns to branding itself: what brand equity is, how it gets built, and how it becomes a financial asset on a company's balance sheet.

The rest of the course is built on these three foundations. Let's go.
