The Six Elements of the Promotional Mix

Six categories. Knowing them is knowing who to call when you need a specific kind of work done — and what they're going to try to sell you when they answer the phone.

Simplified Funnel Notes on Each Element Tools, Not Strategies More Views

Belch and most other introductory marketing texts organize promotional activity into six categories. The categories overlap at the edges and have gotten messier as digital channels have blurred the boundaries between them, but the taxonomy remains useful because it maps roughly onto how the marketing industry organizes itself. There are advertising agencies, PR firms, sales promotion shops, direct marketing specialists, digital agencies, and sales organizations, and each of these business types corresponds to one of the six categories below. Knowing the categories means knowing who to call when you need a specific kind of work done, and knowing what they're going to try to sell you when they answer the phone.

The table above is honest about the messiness. Most of the six elements operate at more than one funnel stage, depending on the tactic. Advertising can build awareness with a Super Bowl spot or close a sale with a retargeted display ad. PR can plant a brand in a magazine reader's head or rehabilitate a damaged reputation in the middle of a buying decision. Sales promotion usually lives at the bottom of the funnel but can occasionally pull a cold prospect into consideration if the offer is interesting enough.

That honesty is useful for analysis and useless for teaching. If everything overlaps with everything, students leave the chapter without a usable mental model. So here's the simplified version, the one you should keep in your head for the rest of the course:

This simplification is not strictly accurate. It is, however, useful. Here's the reasoning behind each placement.

Advertising and PR sit at the top because their primary job in most modern marketing organizations is awareness. A television advertising buy and a press placement in The New York Times are both trying to plant the brand in the consumer's head before the consumer is in a buying mode. Both can do work further down the funnel, but if you had to pick a single funnel stage where each tool earns its budget, the top of the funnel is the honest answer.

Digital marketing and personal selling sit in the middle for different reasons. Digital marketing is genuinely funnel-agnostic; a digital tactic can do almost any job depending on the targeting and creative. We place it in the middle because the unique contribution of digital is its ability to nurture a prospect through consideration in ways the other tools can't, with retargeting, email sequences, content marketing, and progressive personalization. Personal selling, especially in B2B, is the dominant tool during consideration, where the prospect has identified a need and is actively evaluating options.

Direct marketing and sales promotion sit at the bottom because both tools are built around producing immediate, measurable response. A direct mail piece with an offer and a deadline, a coupon, a limited-time discount, an abandoned-cart email — these are conversion tools. They work on people who are already close to buying. Asking them to build awareness from scratch is asking them to do a job they're not designed for.

The reason this simplification is worth installing, even though it papers over real complexity, is that it gives you a default. When you're planning a campaign and someone asks "what's the right tool for awareness?", the simplified funnel gives you a fast answer: advertising and PR, and then you can adjust based on the specifics. When someone asks "what closes the sale?", the simplified funnel gives you direct marketing and sales promotion as the starting point. The defaults are correct often enough to be useful, and knowing when to deviate from them is what experience teaches.

Working marketers move between the nuanced version and the simplified version constantly. In a planning meeting, the simplified funnel is the working vocabulary. In an actual media plan, the nuanced table is what gets built. The skill is knowing which conversation you're in.

4.2 Notes on Each Element

A few notes on each category, in slightly more detail than the table allows.

Advertising is paid, non-personal communication delivered through media that reach groups rather than individuals. Television, radio, magazines, newspapers, outdoor, and, increasingly, the various digital ad formats that have grown up over the past two decades. Advertising is what most non-marketers think of when they hear the word "marketing," which is itself a useful piece of intelligence about how the discipline is perceived. It's also the largest line item in most companies' promotional budgets, even though that share has been declining for fifteen years as digital and direct-response tactics have taken share. Advertising's strength is its reach and its image-building capacity. Its weakness is that consumers know it's advertising and discount it accordingly, which means an advertising message has to work harder to land than it did thirty years ago.

Direct marketing is communication aimed at producing an immediate, measurable response. Direct mail, telemarketing, email marketing, SMS, addressable digital advertising, and the various retargeting tactics that follow you around the internet after you've visited a website. Direct marketing's defining feature is that it's almost always one-to-one rather than one-to-many, even when it's automated at industrial scale. A direct mail piece is addressed to you. An email lands in your inbox specifically. A retargeting ad follows your browser. The strength of direct marketing is measurability and precision. The weakness is that consumers are increasingly hostile to feeling targeted, and the regulatory and platform constraints on direct marketing (GDPR, iOS privacy changes, email deliverability rules) have made the discipline harder to execute well.

Digital / internet marketing is a category that didn't exist in the original promotional-mix taxonomy and was bolted on later, which is why it overlaps awkwardly with advertising and direct marketing. The reason it's its own category is that the underlying media (search, social, programmatic display, content, mobile) behave differently from traditional advertising in measurement, targeting, and pacing, and they require their own expertise. Most modern marketing organizations have a dedicated digital function, separate from the traditional advertising function, even though the two are increasingly impossible to separate cleanly. We'll spend significant time on this category later in the course.

Sales promotion is short-term incentive activity designed to produce immediate purchase. Coupons, discounts, contests, sweepstakes, rebates, samples, trade allowances, and the various flavors of "limited-time offer." Sales promotion is the fastest-acting tool in the mix and the easiest one to measure, which is part of why it gets overused. The problem with sales promotion is that it trains customers to wait for deals, which is brand-equity damage that doesn't show up on the quarter's P&L but shows up in the long run as eroding pricing power. Sales promotion is also the category most likely to be in tension with brand-building activity, which is a tension we'll return to in Section 7.

Publicity / public relations is the discipline of earning coverage and managing relationships with the various publics that matter to a brand: journalists, analysts, investors, employees, communities, governments. PR is the closest the promotional mix gets to working entirely in the earned-media channel. Its great strength is credibility: when a respected outlet writes about you, the message lands with weight that no amount of paid advertising can buy. Its weaknesses are that it's slow, that it's only partially controllable, and that the working definition of "PR" has expanded so far that it now includes corporate communications, crisis management, influencer relations, content seeding, and a dozen other functions that didn't exist when the term was coined. A modern PR firm might do all of these things, or only some of them, and figuring out which is part of the buyer's job.

Personal selling is one-to-one communication, usually face-to-face or via direct phone contact, aimed at facilitating a specific transaction. It's the original promotional activity, predating advertising by several thousand years, and it remains the dominant promotional channel in B2B markets and high-consideration consumer purchases (cars, real estate, financial advice). Personal selling is the most persuasive tool in the mix, because the salesperson can adapt the message to the individual buyer in real time. It is also the most expensive per contact, which is why it gets reserved for high-value transactions. Most of the rest of the promotional mix exists, in some sense, to make personal selling more efficient by warming up the prospect before the salesperson arrives.

4.3 Tools, Not Strategies

Here's the argumentative move that should land before we move on. These six categories are tools, not strategies. The failure mode in most marketing organizations is that each tool defends itself. The advertising team believes the answer to every problem is more advertising. PR firms reflexively recommend more PR coverage. Digital agencies want you to spend more on paid social, and sales promotion shops always have another promotional idea ready to go. The salespeople, naturally, just need more leads. Each position is self-serving in a different way, and each is incomplete, because each tool defends the budget of the people who specialize in it.

The IMC manager's job, properly understood, is to be the person in the room who doesn't have a horse in any of those races. The question isn't which tool wins. The question is which combination of tools advances the strategic goal at the lowest total cost. That question is harder than the tool-defenders make it sound, and answering it honestly is what separates marketing strategy from marketing tactics.

Takeaway
  • The six elements (advertising, direct marketing, digital, sales promotion, PR, personal selling) map onto how the marketing industry organizes itself.
  • Each tool has natural strengths, natural funnel positions, and natural weaknesses.
  • Each tool's specialists will defend that tool as the answer to everything. They are usually wrong.
  • The IMC question is never "which tool?" It's "which combination?"

More Views

The framework above is Belch's, the most widely-taught taxonomy in U.S. marketing education. Other texts and practitioners group the same activity differently in ways worth knowing. Some textbooks still teach a five-element model, treating digital as a channel rather than a discipline; others have moved to seven elements by splitting digital into search and social, or by adding interactive/experiential as separate categories. Practitioners argue both ways on whether digital deserves its own category — the case for is that it has its own metrics, its own organizational home, and its own tactical playbook; the case against is that digital is so thoroughly absorbed into the others (advertising runs through digital channels, direct marketing happens through digital channels, even publicity now travels through digital channels) that listing it separately is misleading. The weighting of the six also varies enormously by industry and business model: B2B markets lean heavily on personal selling and content, while B2C markets lean on advertising and sales promotion. The four pieces below sample these positions: a modern practitioner update with a Spotify worked example, a five-element holdout that resists adding digital as its own category, a brand-example-heavy practitioner take, and a B2B-context view that re-weights everything.
