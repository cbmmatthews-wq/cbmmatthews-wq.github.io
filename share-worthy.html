What Makes Content Shareable?

Virality is an outcome, not a strategy. The more useful question is whether each post, activation, or event moment is share‑worthy on purpose — and the research on why people actually pass things along is more specific than most marketers realize.

People share things for themselves and for the people they care about. They do not share things for the brand. A post gets passed along when it helps someone express an identity, feel an emotion, help another person, or open a conversation — and almost never because a brand asked them to. This is the central asymmetry of social marketing. Most brands optimize for what they want to say. Only the share‑worthy ones optimize for what their audience would want to send.

The implication is that “make it go viral” is the wrong instruction. Virality is an outcome that depends on factors a marketer cannot control: timing, algorithm weather, what else happens to be in the feed that morning. What a marketer can control is whether a given piece of content has any reason to be shared at all. The right brief is not “make this go viral” but “give this enough share‑worthy material that virality is at least possible.”

The Data

Why People Share Brand Content

Hootsuite’s 2024 Social Media Consumer Report asked people who had privately shared a brand post why they did it. The answers are useful because they cut against the assumption most brand managers carry, which is that famous people drive sharing.

Forty‑four percent shared because they agreed with the post’s opinion or perspective. Twenty‑nine percent found it informative. Twenty‑seven percent found it funny or entertaining. Twenty‑four percent called it inspirational. Twenty‑one percent said it reflected their personal values. Twenty percent wanted someone else’s opinion on it. Nineteen percent wanted to start a conversation. Only eight percent shared because a recognizable influencer, celebrity, or personality was attached to it (Hootsuite, 2024).

The eight percent is the number to sit with. The presence of a famous face matters far less than what the post says, how the post feels, and whether the person passing it along sees themselves in it. The sharer’s relationship with the content matters more than the celebrity attached to it.

The funnel effect is also worth noting. Hootsuite found that shared brand posts prompted some kind of consideration behavior in 82% of respondents at least once during the previous year, compared with 74% for organic posts and 67% for paid posts. Seventy‑eight percent had themselves reshared a brand post that was originally sent to them by a family member or friend (Hootsuite, 2024). Shared content travels further down the funnel than posted content does, partly because it arrives through a trusted source rather than through a feed.

Framework

Berger’s Six STEPPS

Jonah Berger’s Contagious organizes the drivers of word of mouth into a six‑part framework (Berger, 2013). The whole framework rarely shows up in one piece of content, and forcing it to is usually a mistake. The useful version is diagnostic: a post or activation that earns two or three of these has a real reason to spread. A post that earns none has nothing to work with.

Social Currency. Sharing it makes the sharer look knowledgeable, interesting, funny, caring, connected, or in the know. The implicit question the sharer is answering is, what does it say about me that I’m the one passing this along?

Triggers. The content attaches to a recurring occasion, activity, phrase, object, or cultural moment that will remind people of it later. Triggered associations are why “Snickers, when you’re hungry” has lasted — hunger is a daily trigger that keeps reactivating the brand.

Emotion. When people care, they share. The relevant emotions are the high‑energy ones — awe, excitement, amusement, anger, anxiety — not the low‑energy ones like contentment or mild sadness. For most brands, the positive high‑energy emotions are safer to work with than the negative ones.

Public. Behavior is easier to copy when it is visible. If others are seen participating, more people participate. This is why event activations with crowds, lines, and physical artifacts spread better than activations that only exist in private.

Practical Value. Useful information gives a person a reason to help someone else. Tips, shortcuts, warnings, recommendations, “news you can use” — content with practical value gets shared because the sharer is doing a favor for the receiver, and being seen as helpful is its own social reward.

Stories. Information embedded in a story travels better than the same information presented as a brand claim. The story provides a vehicle; the claim rides along inside it. This is why testimonial advertising can outperform feature advertising on the same product.

In Practice

Making an Event More Shareable

Most of the same logic applies to events and activations, with a few additional levers that are specific to the physical context.

Create an “I was there” moment. Give attendees something that signals access, identity, accomplishment, or belonging. An unusual setting, an exclusive reveal, a personalized item, a recognizable photo backdrop. The shareable thing is not the event itself. It is the proof that the person was present at it.

Build one or two emotional peaks. Surprise, spectacle, humor, participation, transformation, a genuine human moment. Consistently pleasant events produce few shares. Events with a peak produce many, because the peak is the thing that gets recorded and discussed afterward.

Provide a shareable unit. A single striking photograph, a short demonstration, a quotable line, a 10‑second video. Attendees should not have to assemble their share from scratch — the event should hand them the asset.

Give people something to say. An opinion to take, a prediction to make, a vote, a reaction, a choice. Posts that open a conversation get further than posts that only announce that the event happened.

Remove the friction. Good lighting, working wifi, a short hashtag, the correct account handle visible, clear sharing prompts, easy access to event photos afterward. Repost attendee content quickly — with permission — so participation becomes publicly visible and pulls more participation in behind it.

Let the attendee remain the hero. The brand should be recognizable, but logos and slogans turned up too loud make the post feel like an ad the attendee is being asked to run for free. People share what an experience says about them. They are less interested in what it says about the sponsor.

Diagnostic

The Five‑Question Shareability Test

Before approving a post or signing off on an activation, walk through these five questions. They take less than a minute and they filter out a lot of work that was never going to do anything.

  1. Does sharing this help someone express who they are?
  2. Is there enough emotion, surprise, humor, or usefulness in it to justify passing it along?
  3. Could it help someone, or open a real conversation?
  4. Can someone understand it without attending the event or reading three paragraphs of context?
  5. Is the brand recognizable without overpowering the reason someone would share it?

The reframe these questions point at is simple. The default question most brands ask about a post is, “Why would someone look at this?” The better question is, “Why would someone send this to another person?” Almost nothing passes the second test that was only designed to pass the first.

Takeaway
  • People share for themselves, not for the brand. Identity, emotion, helpfulness, and conversation are the four reasons content moves. “The brand asked” is not on the list.
  • Hootsuite’s 2024 data: 44% share because they agree with the perspective, only 8% because of a celebrity or influencer attached. The sharer’s relationship to the content matters more than the famous face.
  • Shared brand content prompts consideration in 82% of respondents — meaningfully more than organic (74%) or paid (67%).
  • Berger’s STEPPS — Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories — are a diagnostic, not a recipe. Two or three strong reasons to share beat all six forced together.
  • For events: build one emotional peak, hand attendees a ready‑made shareable unit, remove friction, and let them remain the hero. The brand is the setting, not the protagonist.
  • The question to test every post against is not “why would someone look at this?” but “why would someone send this to another person?”
