Paid Social: Just Run Ads, Right?

By now the implicit argument from a hypothetical CFO is straightforward. OK — organic doesn’t reach anybody, that’s clear. Fine. Just run ads instead. Buy the impressions, target the people we want, get the clicks. We’ve got a budget. Let’s just put it into paid social. The argument has the same shape as the “just throw it on social” and “just post consistently” arguments the first two pieces dismantled. It’s reasonable on its face. It also describes a paid-social ecosystem that has changed substantially since most CFOs formed their working theory of how it works.

The unit’s organizing claim: social isn’t one channel; it’s a bundle of jobs. This piece is the case for what paid social does in that bundle — and what it costs you to use it for the wrong jobs.

Overview

The Fallback That Isn’t a Free Lunch Either

Part 2 made the case that organic social media isn’t broken — it’s narrower. It does research, customer service, community, voice, and listening, but it doesn’t do mass reach anymore, and treating it as a reach channel is what produces most of the program failures the unit has been cataloging. The natural follow-up question, the one the hypothetical CFO at the table is now asking, is straightforward: OK, so we can’t expect organic to drive growth. Fine. Let’s just put the budget into paid social instead. We’ll buy the impressions, target the people we want, get the clicks. That’s what paid social is for. Easy. That fallback has the same shape as the “just throw it on social” and “just post consistently” arguments the first two pieces dismantled. It’s reasonable on its face. It also describes a paid-social ecosystem that has changed substantially since most CFOs formed their working theory of how it works.

The first is cost. Meta CPMs increased roughly 20% year-over-year in 2025, every industry, no exceptions (Triple Whale, 2026). Some industries took 30–38% increases. The auctions are bid up; the cost to reach a thousand humans on Meta’s platforms is roughly double what it was four years ago and rising. The second is measurement. iOS 14.5 in April 2021 collapsed the tracking infrastructure that paid social ran on; subsequent Apple updates (iOS 17 in 2024, iOS 26 in 2025) tightened the screws further. Roughly 75% of iOS users opted out of cross-app tracking, and Meta’s ability to attribute conversions to specific ads dropped 30–40% almost immediately (LeoAds, 2026). The third is competition for the budget. In 2025, 36% of new Connected TV ad spend came from reallocated social budgets (IAB, 2025). A meaningful share of growth budgets is already moving from social to streaming.

None of this means paid social is broken in the way organic-as-reach-channel is broken. Paid social still works. The honest case for what it’s actually good at in 2026 sits at the end of this piece. But the path between “just run ads” and producing reliable returns is more complicated, more expensive, and requires more skill than it did when most marketing teams learned how to do it. The rest of this piece is the specifics.

What this piece is doing

Three questions, in order. First — what are you actually buying when you buy paid social, and how do the metrics actually work? Second — why has it gotten more expensive and less measurable? Third — given all that, what is it still good for, and where is the budget going when it isn’t going to social anymore?

01
The Cost Mechanics

CPM, CPC, CPA: Three Numbers Measuring Different Things

The single most common rookie misallocation in paid social is confusing the three cost metrics, which measure three completely different things and apply to three completely different campaign objectives. The whole rest of the piece depends on these definitions being clean, so they go first.

CPM
Cost per thousand impressions
The reach metric. Buy CPM when the goal is to put the brand in front of people — awareness, brand-building, message frequency. Judged on reach, recall, brand lift. Doesn’t care whether anyone clicked.
CPC
Cost per click
The response metric. Buy CPC when the goal is traffic to a destination — a landing page, a product page, a lead form. Judged on click-through rate (CTR) and on what happens after the click. Filters out impressions that didn’t earn engagement.
CPA
Cost per action / acquisition
The conversion metric. Buy CPA when the goal is a specific business outcome — a purchase, a signup, a download. Judged on conversion rate and ROAS (return on ad spend). The metric a CFO actually cares about; the hardest to measure post-ATT.

Why confusing them costs money

Suppose a brand is running a CPM-bid campaign optimized for reach — broad targeting, awareness creative, billed at $7.50 per thousand impressions. The team reports back at the end of the quarter and the CEO asks how many sales came from it. The honest answer is that no one knows, because reach campaigns aren’t architected for sales attribution, and asking that question of that campaign is a category error. The CEO concludes the campaign didn’t work. The campaign actually did the job it was supposed to do; the meeting just judged it on the wrong metric.

The reverse mistake is worse. A team runs a CPA-optimized campaign with conversion creative and tight targeting; the CFO asks why the reach number is so low. The team explains that conversion campaigns deliberately narrow the audience to people more likely to convert, so reach should be low. The CFO doesn’t accept the explanation, the team widens the targeting to make the reach number look better, conversion rate drops 30%, and the campaign starts losing money in real time. Both errors come from running one metric’s logic against another metric’s objective.

The working rule is the one every paid-media planner internalizes early: match the metric to the campaign objective, judge each campaign on its own metric, and don’t expect awareness work to defend itself in conversion terms or conversion work to defend itself in reach terms. This sounds obvious. In an actual budget meeting, with a CFO asking how the social spend tied to revenue, it’s the conversation paid teams lose roughly 60% of the time, because the right answer (“these three campaigns did three different things, each measured on a different metric”) is harder to defend than the wrong answer (“here’s one big number that didn’t move much”).

Current US benchmarks for context

Approximate central estimates from Meta benchmark trackers, 2025–26. Your numbers will vary by industry, audience, and bid strategy:

• CPM: Meta blended ~$6.50–$13.50, with Q4 peaks running 26% above annual average and Black Friday week 2–3× normal levels (Sovran, 2026).
• CPC: Facebook traffic campaigns averaging ~$0.70 in 2025, Instagram CPCs steeper at $1.83–$3.35 (WebFX, 2026).
• CPA: Median CPA across all industries on Meta landed at $38.17 in 2025, with ROAS at 1.93× (Triple Whale, 2026).

Two things to internalize from the table. First, paid CPC and paid CPM produce roughly the same number of conversions when CTR holds at ~1% — the $10 CPM / 1,000 impressions / 1% CTR math comes out to the same ~$1 per click that CPC would have purchased directly. The choice between them is not about price; it’s about whether you also want the impressions for the people who didn’t click (you do, for awareness work; you don’t, for narrow conversion work). Second, Owned is dramatically cheaper at scale — but the scale is fixed by the list size. A brand with no list has nothing to send; the work of list-building is what creates the asset. Part 5 §02 picks this up in the PESO context: Owned is structurally the most modern channel in a 2026 marketing program because it’s the only one whose distribution the brand actually controls.

02
Funnel Math

Click-Through Rate and Conversion Rate Aren’t the Same Number

A second piece of mechanical confusion catches teams that are sophisticated about the cost metrics. Click-through rate (CTR) is the share of impressions that produce a click. Conversion rate (CVR) is the share of clicks that produce the desired action (purchase, signup, etc.). They’re both percentages, they’re both reported on the same dashboard, and they measure two completely different audiences at two different funnel stages.

The math, on a typical ecommerce campaign in 2026, looks something like this:

  • 10,000 impressions delivered (CPM purchase).
  • ~1.0% click-through rate — roughly the Meta benchmark for traffic campaigns — yields about 100 clicks.
  • ~2–5% conversion rate on those clicks, depending on industry and landing page, yields 2–5 conversions.
  • Final yield: roughly 0.02–0.05% of impressions become conversions.

Two things to notice. First, the two rates compound multiplicatively, not additively, which means a 50% improvement in either CTR or CVR meaningfully moves the final number, but neither alone is sufficient to explain campaign performance. Second, the impressions that didn’t click and the clickers who didn’t convert are not failures — they’re audiences at different funnel stages. The impression that didn’t click may still have produced brand exposure that closes a sale six months later via a different channel; the clicker who didn’t convert today may convert next week after a retargeting ad nudges them back.

Both rates are subject to industry benchmarks that have themselves been declining. Average CTR on Meta traffic campaigns sits around 0.9–1.5% in 2025–2026 (WebFX, 2026); conversion rates vary widely but most ecommerce sees 1.8–3%, SaaS 2–5%, and platforms like TikTok where the user wasn’t actively looking for the product run lower (Lucky Orange, 2026). None of these are bad numbers in isolation; they’re the reality of intent-interruption advertising, which is what paid social fundamentally is.

The deeper consequence sits in the next section. The reason paid social converts at modest rates per impression isn’t that the advertising is bad. It’s that the audience wasn’t looking for the product when the ad reached them — which is also why paid social converts at higher rates than organic social, despite the intuition cutting the other way.

03
The Counterintuitive Bit, Continued

Why Paid Beats Organic on Conversion, Even Though Paid Reaches Strangers

Part 2 §03 previewed the finding that catches most undergrads off guard: paid social converts at higher rates than organic social, even though organic reaches your existing followers — people who chose to follow you, who are by definition warmer to your brand than strangers. The result feels backwards. It isn’t. The mechanics are worth unpacking in full because they’re also what makes paid social actually work in 2026.

Brand familiarity is not the same thing as purchase intent

An organic follower likes the brand, or is curious about it, or once bought something and stayed subscribed. None of that means they’re in the market for a purchase today. A Patagonia Instagram follower might love Patagonia and have no current need for a new jacket. A Mailchimp Twitter follower might use Mailchimp at work and not be evaluating new email tools this quarter. Brand familiarity is a stock; purchase intent is a flow. Organic distribution reaches the stock (the followers). Conversion measures the flow (the people currently buying).

Paid social, when configured competently, targets the flow

What paid social actually optimizes for — in the Advantage+ era especially, more on which below — is behavioral signal that indicates in-market intent. People who’ve been visiting product pages in the category. People who’ve abandoned a cart on a competitor’s site. People who match the behavioral profile of recent converters via lookalike modeling. These audiences may have lower brand familiarity with the advertiser specifically. They have substantially higher current intent to buy something in the category.

Per impression, intent beats familiarity for short-window conversion. Every time. The reason organic looks “warmer” in a casual analysis — the audience knows your brand — is the same reason it converts worse: knowing your brand and being in-market for what you sell are two different things, and the second is what closes a sale this week.

Organic reaches the people who like you. Paid reaches the people who are buying. These are mostly different people, and they convert at very different rates.

The implication for the brand-vs-performance debate

This is also where the brand-vs-performance framework you’ve already met in this course connects to social specifically. Organic social — doing the jobs catalogued in Part 2 §04 — is brand-building work. It pays off on multi-year horizons through familiarity, voice, trust, and dark social. Paid social is mostly activation work. It pays off this quarter through conversion of in-market intent. Treating either as a substitute for the other is the misallocation. The 60/40 brand-to-performance budget split from Binet & Field, which underlies your creative-effectiveness work, applies at the channel level too: organic social = the brand half; paid social = part of the activation half. Trying to extract activation results from organic and brand-building from paid is what most accounts have spent the past five years quietly failing at.

The honest comparison, condensed

Organic social: reaches a small, “warm,” mostly not-in-market audience. Best for research, service, voice, community. Low conversion rate per impression by structural design.

Paid social: reaches an audience matched on behavioral in-market signal. Best for activation, conversion, retargeting. Higher conversion rate per impression because the audience was already moving toward purchase.

Holding them to a single metric punishes whichever channel wasn’t built for that metric.

A retail client’s organic posts get 4% engagement, while their paid posts get a 1.1% click-through rate. The CMO asks, “If organic gets four times the engagement, why are we spending on paid at all?” How do you respond?
What to walk the CMO through
  • Different metrics measuring different things. The organic 4% engagement is interactions on a tiny slice of followers (~1–2% reach on Facebook, 4–9% on Instagram). The paid 1.1% CTR is clicks against a much larger impression base — and the post-click conversion rate on those clicks is what actually matters for sales.
  • Different audiences too. Organic reaches followers who like the brand but mostly aren’t in-market today. Paid reaches behavioral profiles matched on in-market intent. Engagement is high for organic because those people opted in; conversion is higher for paid because those people were already buying in the category.
  • The two channels do different jobs. Organic builds familiarity, voice, and trust over time. Paid converts in-market intent in the short window. Cutting paid because organic looks “more engaging” would collapse the conversion line within a quarter, and the dashboard engagement-rate number would not save the P&L.

Cost Inflation

Why Paid Social Got Expensive

The cost of buying impressions on Meta has been rising consistently for the past five years, and 2025 was the largest single-year jump documented. The data that surfaces it most clearly is the first anchor source for this piece.

Anchor Source #1
Facebook Ad Benchmarks by Industry (2025 Data)
Triple Whale · drawn from 35,000+ ad accounts · April 2026

The largest publicly-shared dataset on Meta ad performance, drawn from Triple Whale’s e-commerce client base. The headline finding from the 2025 data is that Meta CPMs increased 20% year-over-year across every industry tracked — no exceptions. The spread by vertical is wide: increases ranged from +8% (the lowest-pressure industries) to +38% (Health & Wellness, which also has the highest absolute CPM at $20.70). The platform-wide median CPM landed at $13.48, with CPA at $38.17 and ROAS at 1.93× (meaning brands earned $1.93 for every $1 spent, on average, before accounting for organic-conversion overlap and brand-building tail effects).

The mechanism is competition for a constrained supply of impressions. As more advertisers entered the auction (Meta continued to add advertisers throughout 2024–25), and as fewer signal-rich users remained available for precision targeting (ATT effect — see §05 below), the bids required to win each impression climbed. The 2025 holiday season pushed CPMs to 2–3× normal levels during Black Friday week, with some categories spiking 66% above their off-season averages. The seasonal effect compounds the structural increase: brands competing for fixed holiday inventory pay more on top of the rising baseline.

Read the full Triple Whale benchmarks → Triple Whale is a paid attribution / analytics platform for e-commerce, so their data skews toward DTC brands; the trends are directionally consistent with industry-wide reports from WordStream, Affect Group, and Sovran cited below.

What’s driving the increases

Three forces, all working in the same direction.

The first is straightforward demand. Global social ad spend reached $276.7 billion in 2025 and is projected to hit $300 billion in 2026 (Stackmatix, 2026). More dollars chasing roughly the same impressions inventory means higher per-impression prices — standard auction dynamics.

The second is the ATT effect on precise targeting (full mechanics in §05). Without the granular cross-app behavioral signal Meta used to have, lookalike audiences are less precise, retargeting is leakier, and the algorithm needs more impressions per conversion to find the right buyer. More wasted impressions per converted customer means higher effective CPA, which forces brands to bid higher on CPM to keep volume up — pushing the auction price up further for everyone.

The third is the algorithmic competitive bidding rollout. Meta’s Advantage+ campaigns (see §06) bid more aggressively for the impressions their AI predicts will convert, which works for the brand running the campaign but pushes prices up for everyone bidding against them. The system is, structurally, a race-to-the-top on prices for any given valuable impression.

05
The Privacy Chapter

How iOS 14.5 Through iOS 26 Broke Paid Social Measurement

Part 1 §02 introduced iOS 14.5 / App Tracking Transparency as one of the two platform pivots that ended the 2014-era social model. The full mechanics belong here. The short version is that between 2021 and 2026, Apple progressively dismantled the cross-app behavioral tracking infrastructure that paid social ran on, and Meta’s ability to attribute conversions to specific ads degraded substantially and hasn’t recovered.

April 26, 2021
iOS 14.5 — App Tracking Transparency launches

Apple requires every app to ask user permission before tracking them across other apps and websites, via a system-level prompt the developer cannot style or hide. Roughly 75% of iOS users opted out when prompted. Meta’s tracking accuracy dropped 30–40% almost immediately (LeoAds, 2026). For the apps most affected, opt-out rates above 70% cost an average 58% of advertising revenue (Medium / Bhuva, 2025).

2021–2024
Meta adapts — Conversions API, modeled conversions, Advantage+

Meta rebuilds the measurement model around server-side event tracking (CAPI), statistical inference for missing events (modeled conversions), and AI-driven broad targeting (Advantage+). Performance partially recovers. Granular user-level visibility doesn’t.

September 2024
iOS 17 — Link Tracking Protection

Apple adds Link Tracking Protection to Safari Private Browsing, which strips tracking parameters from URLs — including fbclid, the identifier Meta uses to connect an ad click to a conversion event on the advertiser’s site (DOJO AI, 2026). For iOS users in private browsing, the link between “clicked the ad” and “converted on the brand’s site” is severed at the URL level.

November 2025
iOS 26 — fingerprinting defenses tighten

Apple’s 2025 update adds advanced fingerprinting protections that further restrict the workarounds advertisers had been using to identify users without explicit tracking permission. The cumulative effect: most of the workarounds that survived ATT are now also degraded.

January 12, 2026
Meta removes 7-day and 28-day attribution windows

Meta’s Ads Manager drops the two longer attribution windows from the default reporting view, leaving advertisers with shorter (1-day click, 7-day view) windows that materially under-count delayed conversions (DOJO AI, 2026). Brands whose products have a multi-day consideration cycle now see less of their actual conversion volume in Meta’s reporting, regardless of which iOS the customer was on.

The practical consequence

Paid social attribution in 2026 is a mix of three kinds of data, blended into a single dashboard that reads as if it were one number:

  • Observed conversions — events the platform can actually see, mostly from users who didn’t opt out of tracking and used browsers without privacy protections. A shrinking share of the total.
  • Modeled conversions — statistical estimates of conversions that probably happened but couldn’t be observed. Reported as if they were real events. Increasingly the larger share of the “conversions” line on the dashboard.
  • Server-side events via CAPI — conversions tracked from the advertiser’s own server back to Meta, bypassing browser-level privacy restrictions. The most accurate source available in 2026, but requires technical implementation most brands haven’t fully invested in.

The implication for any reading of paid-social ROI is sharp: the conversion numbers in Meta’s dashboard are not a measurement, they’re an estimate. Specifically, an estimate produced by Meta, who has a financial incentive to model in the direction that makes campaigns look like they worked. None of this is conspiratorial — Meta is open about the methodology — but the implication for how seriously to take conversion attribution from social is that you should treat it the way you’d treat any other vendor-reported metric: as one input, not as ground truth.

06
The AI-Targeting Era

Advantage+, AI Broad Targeting, and Why Creative Matters More Than Ever

Meta’s response to the targeting and measurement collapse of 2021–2024 was, in retrospect, the most consequential strategic shift in paid social since the Facebook IPO. The company couldn’t recover the precise user-level targeting that ATT had broken, so instead it built an AI system — Advantage+ — that worked on broad audiences and aggregate behavioral patterns rather than individual user signal. By 2025, Advantage+ campaigns were the default recommendation for most advertisers on the platform.

The practical change is that the advertiser does less of the targeting work and Meta’s algorithm does more of it. Where a 2018 paid social campaign was configured with detailed interest, demographic, and behavioral targeting (and the success of the campaign turned heavily on how well the targeting was set up), a 2026 Advantage+ campaign is given a wide audience definition, a budget, a set of creative assets, and a conversion objective — and the algorithm figures out who to show the ads to. Some hand-targeting is still possible for specific tests, but the platform is unmistakably pushing toward AI-broad as the standard.

What this means for who actually does the work

Two things shift. First, the difference between a great media buyer and an average one narrows substantially, because most of the targeting decisions that used to be the buyer’s craft are now made by the algorithm. Second, and far more important — the difference between great creative and average creative widens, because creative is now the largest variable the advertiser still controls. The algorithm can’t optimize a bad ad into a good one; it can only optimize a good ad to a better audience.

Sovran / AppsFlyer · March 2026
Creative is now the dominant lever for Meta ad performance
The clearest statement of what the Advantage+ shift means for where performance comes from now.
  • AppsFlyer research estimates 70–80% of Meta ad performance is now driven by creative quality. Pre-ATT, the number was closer to 30–40% — the rest was targeting craft.
  • Motion’s analysis of 550,000+ ads finds that only 5–8% of ads become real winners, and approximately 50% never get meaningful spend at all. The performance distribution is sharply long-tailed.
  • The operational implication: brands that increase creative volume and creative quality (more variants, higher production value, more rapid testing) significantly outperform brands that don’t.

The Self-Defeating Loop the Vendor Industry Built on Top of This

Meta’s system demands genuinely new creative. The market is selling the opposite.

The trade press read the “creative is 70–80% of performance” finding correctly. The vendor industry that built on top of it didn’t. The dominant product category that emerged in 2024–2025 was AI-prompt libraries for social creative — courses, paid Substacks, prompt-pack downloads, agency offerings — all selling some version of the same pitch: use these proven hooks, plug them into ChatGPT, generate ten variants per hook, ship the volume.

The problem is that the prompts are convergent. Every team buying the same prompt library generates variations of the same generic hooks (“Tired of X? Try Y.” “Stop doing X. Start doing Y.” “The one thing nobody tells you about X.”). Ten variants of a generic hook aren’t ten variants — they’re the same dead idea ten times, dressed slightly differently. Meta’s algorithm doesn’t reward repetition at scale; it rewards creative that lands differently with different audiences. The vendor playbook produces, at industrial volume, exactly what Advantage+ punishes.

Two things follow. First, the brands that read the “creative is 70–80% of performance” finding and concluded “buy a prompt library and 10x the variants” ended up running ten times the AI slop — same idea, faster cycle time, slightly worse performance because the platform deprioritized convergent content. Second, the brands that read the same finding and concluded “invest in genuinely different creative work” opened a widening gap on the brands that took the cheaper interpretation. The Advantage+ era didn’t democratize creative; it made the difference between real creative judgment and AI-templated output more financially consequential, not less.

The right way to use AI in this environment is the way Part 4 §04 lays out: AI for ideation, drafting, and variation inside a creatively novel modular system that the brand built. The variation is only as creative as the framework feeding it. If the framework is a generic hook from a prompt pack, the variation is generic too. If the framework is original brand work, AI multiplies original brand work.

The lever moved from targeting to creative. The market figured this out and immediately started selling tools that defeat the point.

This is the moment to make the callback this piece has been building toward. The argument the creative-effectiveness work made — that creative carries 60–70% of marketing effectiveness, that it’s the controllable lever the brand owns outright, that it deserves more investment than most teams give it — isn’t just still true in paid social. It’s more true than when those studies were originally done. The targeting variable that creative effectiveness research had to compete against in 2013 is mostly gone now. What’s left is creative, audience selection done by Meta’s algorithm, and budget. The brand’s decision space has narrowed to mostly creative.

The decline of precise targeting hasn’t reduced the importance of creative in paid social. It has roughly doubled it.

The reverse is also true: brands that under-invest in creative production now pay the cost twice. The first cost is direct — their ads perform worse on the platform’s relevance scoring, so they pay higher CPMs to reach the same audience (Meta rewards relevance with lower auction prices). The second cost is indirect — the variance between top-performing creative and bottom-performing creative on the same product is now wider than at any point in the platform’s history, so brands without enough creative volume to find their winners are running entirely on losers and middlers. Neither is fatal individually. Together they describe the gap between brands that grew through paid social in 2025 and brands that quietly stalled.

Where the Budget Is Going

Connected TV Is Taking Social’s Money

The second anchor source for this piece is the one that surfaces the strategic story most CMOs aren’t yet looking at clearly. In 2025, a substantial share of the ad budget that used to grow on social got reallocated to Connected TV instead.

Anchor Source #2
2025 Digital Video Ad Spend & Strategy Report
Interactive Advertising Bureau (IAB), in partnership with Advertiser Perceptions and Guideline · April 2025

The industry-standard tracking of digital video ad spend, now in its twelfth year. The 2025 edition’s headline finding is the reallocation pattern: of every new dollar flowing into CTV in 2025, 36% came from linear TV budgets, 36% came from social media budgets, and the remaining 28% came from other digital channels (online video, paid search, display). CTV ad spend grew 16% year-over-year in 2024 and is projected to keep growing double-digits through 2027 — outpacing every other major ad channel.

The mechanism is straightforward. CTV (streaming on smart TVs — Disney+, Netflix with ads, Hulu, Prime Video, Tubi, YouTube on TV, etc.) offers something paid social can’t at current CPMs: large-screen, sound-on, full-attention exposure to audiences identified via streaming-service first-party data. 53% of marketers in IAB’s survey called CTV more brand-safe than social. 69% described it as “must-buy” for their 2025 media plans. The brands moving budget aren’t leaving social entirely; they’re reallocating the awareness-oriented portion of their social spend to a channel that does awareness work better at the current price points.

The number worth pausing on is the second one. Roughly one out of every three new dollars going into CTV in 2025 used to be social budget. The counter-trend worth noting

It would be easy to read the CTV reallocation as the end of social, but it isn’t. The IAB also reports that social video ad spending is projected to grow 13% in 2026 — outpacing CTV’s 11% projected growth for the first time (Digiday, 2026). The story isn’t that social is dying; the story is that the part of social that’s growing is short-form video (Reels, Shorts, TikTok), while the part that’s contracting is feed-based static ads on Facebook and Instagram. The brand that’s moving its full social budget into CTV is overcorrecting. The brand that’s keeping everything in feed ads on Meta is undercorrecting. The right move is more nuanced, and it’s what the next section is for.

Useful for the longer-horizon picture beyond 2026.
  • CTV ad spending will reach $33.35 billion in 2025 and $46.89 billion by 2028 — the second number surpassing traditional linear TV ad spending for the first time.
  • The market leaders by 2026 share: YouTube ~12%, Amazon >10%, Disney >10% — with YouTube, Amazon (Prime Video, Fire TV, Twitch), and Disney all leveraging existing first-party data systems to drive growth.
  • Retail media CTV (Amazon, Walmart Connect, etc.) is growing fastest as a share — from 6.6% of CTV in 2024 to a projected 10.5% by 2028.

The Honest Pitch

What Paid Social Is Actually Good For in 2026

After seven sections of complications, the natural reaction is to write paid social off the way most readers were tempted to write organic off after Part 1. That would be wrong. Paid social is still one of the most effective conversion-oriented channels available to most brands, and the brands abandoning it entirely in favor of CTV are mostly making the same mistake the brands abandoning organic for paid made — misreading what each channel is actually for.

Here’s the honest case for what paid social does well in 2026.

  • Activation of in-market intent. Paid social remains an excellent channel for converting people who are already moving toward purchase in the category. The Advantage+ algorithm, despite its imperfections, is genuinely good at finding behavioral signal that indicates current intent. For an e-commerce brand running a known-good product against a competitive in-market audience, paid social is still among the highest-ROAS channels available.
  • Retargeting and re-engagement. The combination of behavioral data on visitors who didn’t convert and the reach of the major platforms makes retargeting via paid social one of the most reliable conversion-recovery mechanisms there is. The CPMs are higher than for cold prospecting; the conversion rates are also dramatically higher, so the math usually works.
  • Lookalike acquisition at scale. When the brand has a clean first-party customer list (email signups, purchasers, app users), Meta’s ability to find behavioral lookalikes at scale is still meaningfully better than most alternatives. CTV doesn’t do this; search does it differently; paid social is uniquely good at it.
  • Creative testing. The volume, speed, and per-test cost of paid social make it the cheapest, fastest creative-testing surface available to a brand. Even brands shifting awareness budget to CTV often use paid social to find their winning creative variants first, then port them to CTV at the higher production values that channel rewards.
  • Specific platforms for specific audiences. Each major platform has its own strengths. TikTok’s algorithmic discovery still produces real top-of-funnel reach for brands with creative that fits the platform. LinkedIn paid is still excellent for B2B audiences that don’t live on Meta. Pinterest still works for visual-discovery categories. Treating “paid social” as one channel is the same kind of category error as treating “organic social” as one channel.

Where paid social is not the right answer

Three places. Top-of-funnel awareness — at current CPMs, CTV and podcast advertising mostly do this work better per dollar. High-intent search — the search-engine paid auction (Google Ads, Microsoft Ads) is generally a better fit for capturing intent-led queries, since paid search visitors convert at (eMarketer, 2024) roughly 4× the rate of paid social visitors. Long-cycle B2B consideration — for products with multi-month sales cycles, paid social can support the work but rarely closes it; the demand-gen heavy lifting usually happens elsewhere.

The working answer is the same as it’s been across this whole unit: don’t treat any single channel as the answer, and don’t treat any channel as broken just because it’s harder than it used to be. Paid social is still a real channel for real jobs. It’s also no longer the universal acquisition solution it was treated as in the late 2010s, and the brands that learned that two years ago are the ones outperforming the brands still figuring it out.

If you do nothing else with paid social, do these
  • Invest in creative production, not targeting craft. The Advantage+ era means creative is roughly 70–80% of performance now. Most marketing budgets are still allocated against the pre-2022 ratio. Move the dollar.
  • Implement Conversions API (CAPI) and stop trusting in-platform attribution alone. Server-side event tracking is now the difference between knowing what your campaigns are doing and guessing.
  • Bid for the metric matching your campaign objective. Awareness on CPM, traffic on CPC, conversion on CPA. Don’t let CFOs evaluate awareness campaigns on conversion metrics, and don’t let media buyers run conversion campaigns wide enough to satisfy a reach KPI they were never trying to hit.
  • Re-evaluate the budget split against CTV. If you’re spending heavily on CPM-bought reach campaigns on Meta, CTV is mostly a better deal at current price points. Move the awareness budget there and let paid social focus on activation.
Takeaways from Part 3
  • CPM, CPC, CPA measure different things. CPM is the reach metric, CPC the response metric, CPA the conversion metric. Match the metric to the campaign objective; don’t judge awareness work on conversion or vice versa.
  • Paid social got more expensive, less measurable, and less universal. Meta CPMs up 20% YoY in 2025; iOS 14.5–26 broke click attribution; the 2018-era playbook produces worse results at higher cost.
  • The conversion paradox is structural, not accidental. Paid reaches in-market intent; organic reaches brand familiarity; intent beats familiarity for short-window conversion.
  • Attribution is now an estimate, not a measurement. Meta’s dashboard reports modeled conversions alongside observed ones. Treat platform attribution as one input, not ground truth.
  • Advantage+ moved the lever from targeting to creative. Creative quality now drives 70–80% of paid social performance. The brand with more creative variants in market has more chances to land in the top-performing 5–8%.
  • 36% of new CTV ad spend in 2025 came from social budgets. The awareness-oriented portion of social spending has found a better home at current price points; treat paid social primarily as an activation channel, not awareness.
  • What paid social still does well: activation of in-market intent, retargeting, lookalike acquisition, creative testing, platform-specific audiences. What it doesn’t: top-of-funnel awareness at current CPMs, intent-led search capture, long-cycle B2B consideration.

Vocabulary

Glossary

CPM — cost per thousand impressions. The reach metric. Used for awareness, brand-building, and message-frequency campaigns. Median US Meta CPM in 2025: ~$13.50, with Q4 peaks at 26% above and Black Friday week at 2–3× normal.

CPC — cost per click. The response metric. Used for traffic-driving campaigns. Median Meta CPC in 2025 ran ~$0.70 on Facebook traffic campaigns and $1.83–$3.35 on Instagram, depending on placement.

CPA — cost per action / cost per acquisition. The conversion metric. Used for the campaigns CFOs care about most. Median Meta CPA across industries in 2025: $38.17. Hardest to measure honestly post-ATT.

CTR — click-through rate. Clicks divided by impressions. Meta average for traffic campaigns: ~0.9–1.5% in 2025–26.

CVR — conversion rate. Conversions divided by clicks (note: not divided by impressions). Varies widely by industry; ecommerce typically 1.8–3%, SaaS 2–5%.

ROAS — return on ad spend. Revenue earned divided by ad spend. Median Meta ROAS across industries in 2025: 1.93× (each $1 of ad spend produced $1.93 in revenue, before brand-building tail effects).

App Tracking Transparency (ATT) — the iOS 14.5 framework requiring apps to ask user permission before tracking them across other apps and websites. Roughly 75% of iOS users opted out. The single most consequential platform change affecting paid social since 2018.

Conversions API (CAPI) — Meta’s server-side conversion tracking system. Captures events directly from the advertiser’s server (bypassing browser-level privacy restrictions) and feeds them back to Meta for attribution and optimization. The most accurate measurement available in 2026, but requires technical implementation most brands haven’t fully invested in.

Modeled conversions — statistical inferences of conversions that probably happened but couldn’t be observed directly. Increasingly the larger share of the “conversions” line on Meta’s dashboard. Reported as if observed, but produced by Meta’s model with Meta’s assumptions.

Advantage+ — Meta’s AI-driven campaign system that does most of the audience-selection work the human media buyer used to do. The standard recommended campaign type for most advertisers on the platform in 2026. Performs best with high-quality, abundant creative; performs worst with thin creative and narrow targeting overrides.

Connected TV (CTV) — streaming video on smart TVs, including ad-supported tiers of Disney+, Netflix, Hulu, Prime Video, Peacock, Tubi, and YouTube on TV. Projected to reach $33.4B in US ad spend in 2025 and $46.9B by 2028. In 2025, 36% of new CTV ad dollars came from reallocated social budgets.
