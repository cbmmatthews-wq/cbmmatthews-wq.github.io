Is Your Social Media Agenda Weakening Your Brand?

Or: the chase for virality — is there a downside?

Your CEO sees a format hit forty million views on TikTok and wants to know why your brand isn’t on it yet. The honest answer — the one that won’t survive five minutes of that meeting — is that most of the brands chasing the trend are losing more than they’re gaining. The pieces below make the case from a few different angles: trend-jumping dilutes brand equity, audiences increasingly find the participation embarrassing, and the viral hit itself can damage what comes next.

  • The trend is mostly over by the time you’ve gotten approval to post it.
  • Reach without relevance is just noise — high view counts on the wrong viewers don’t move sales.
  • Sprout’s consumer data: roughly a third of people now find brands jumping on viral trends embarrassing.
  • Algorithms reward novelty, which means your participation reads as derivative, not original.
  • The Jake Mitchell argument below: going viral once can actually drag down your engagement on everything after it.

Social Media Trend-Chasing Can Damage Your Brand

A Forbes Agency Council piece making the foundational version of the argument: when a brand jumps on a trend that has nothing to do with what it actually does or stands for, the short-term reach comes at the cost of long-term clarity. New audiences arrive expecting the trend, not the brand; existing audiences see a brand that doesn’t know what it’s about. Older piece, but it’s the reference point most of the later coverage is arguing from.

https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbesagencycouncil/2020/12/23/social-media-trend-chasing-can-damage-your-brand/

Chasing Every Trend Is Ruining Your Brand. Do This Instead.

Entrepreneur’s version of the argument, written for founders and small-brand operators rather than agencies. The frame is consistency over chasing: a brand that posts on-format every week beats a brand that posts on-trend every month, because the audience eventually learns what to expect from you. The “do this instead” in the headline is roughly: pick a point of view, post against it consistently, let the trends pass you by unless they map cleanly to what you already do.

https://www.entrepreneur.com/starting-a-business/chasing-every-trend-is-ruining-your-brand-do-this-instead/489587

Stop Chasing Trends: A Brand Authenticity Strategy

Clutch frames the same problem in authenticity terms. The piece argues that the brands winning attention on social right now aren’t the ones jumping on every audio clip and meme format — they’re the ones with a distinctive voice that audiences recognize across posts. Useful if you want the “build a voice, not a trend calendar” framing in plain language, with a few examples of brands doing it well.

https://clutch.co/resources/stop-chasing-trends-brand-authenticity-strategy

The Days of Trend-Chasing Are Over: A Third of Consumers Think Jumping on Viral Trends Is Embarrassing for Brands

Sprout Social’s announcement of consumer research finding that roughly one in three people now actively cringe when brands they follow hop on viral formats. The number itself is the headline, but the underlying shift is more interesting: audiences have gotten fluent at reading what a brand is for and what it’s opportunistically attaching itself to. The trend-jump used to feel current; now it often reads as desperate.

https://sproutsocial.com/insights/press/the-days-of-trend-chasing-are-over-new-research-from-sprout-social-reveals-a-third-of-consumers-think-jumping-on-viral-trends-is-embarrassing-for-brands/

Why Trend-Chasing Is Failing Brands on Social

UK trade publication New Digital Age looking at why the standard playbook — spot the trend, brief the creative, ship the post — isn’t producing the returns it used to. Some of the answer is audience fatigue; some of it is the platforms’ algorithmic preference for native creator content over brand attempts at the same format. The piece is useful as a non-US perspective; the dynamic isn’t purely an American phenomenon.

https://newdigitalage.co/social-media/why-trend-chasing-is-failing-brands-on-social/

TLDR: Going Viral Can Hurt Your Engagement (Jake Mitchell)

A short LinkedIn video from Jake Mitchell laying out the mechanical version of the “virality has a cost” argument: when you go viral, the algorithm pushes your post to a much wider audience than your usual one, that wider audience engages at lower rates than your core, and your account’s engagement rate — the metric the algorithm uses to decide who to show you to next time — drops. So your next post reaches fewer people. He also walks through the downside of paying for likes and followers, which is a different version of the same problem (artificial signals the algorithm eventually punishes).

What Mitchell is arguing

Chasing virality can help in the short term but damage your engagement and views in the long run. He also flags the downside of paying for likes and followers — the inflated numbers look good on a screenshot but suppress your reach on everything after.

https://www.linkedin.com/videos/jakemitchellsmm_tldr-going-viral-can-hurt-your-engagement-activity-7291571918602678273-7Sib/

The shift away from one-and-done

Recent Influencer Trends: Abandoning the “One and Done” Approach for Serialized Content

The standard influencer deal of the last decade — pay a creator a flat fee for one post, hope it converts — is quietly being replaced by something closer to TV production. Brands are funding ongoing series with creators. Creators are building shows, not posts. The vocabulary is shifting from campaign to season, from follower to viewer, from reach to audience. The thesis captured in Zack DeVans’s post below is that 2026 is the year this stops being a fringe approach and becomes the default.

  • The “follow” is decoupling from distribution — people follow formats and consistent ideas inside the For You page, not accounts.
  • A creator with a serialized show is functionally a TV programmer with a different distribution model.
  • One-off sponsored posts are losing efficacy as feeds get more saturated; recurring series build the kind of audience relationship a single post can’t.
  • The brand investment starts to look more like a sponsorship deal (think title sponsor of a podcast) and less like media buying.
If you’re not ready to build a series — go deeper with the microinfluencer instead

Or if a serialized approach isn’t in the budget yet, make a deeper connection with a smaller circle of microinfluencers. Don’t just pay for one post. Hire them over a two-month window. Ask them to use your product casually in a post without mentioning it — or just have it sitting on the counter beside them. Combine that more natural product placement with a few specific posts about your product. The relationship reads as more authentic, and you stretch the benefit of the partnership far past a single insertion.

Remember: you’re renting their time, not buying a post.

The Future of Creator Content Is Serialized (Zack DeVans)

DeVans’s LinkedIn post is the cleanest articulation of the thesis: 2026 is the year of the short-form show, and the creators (and brands) who recognize this early will define the next era of social. The post is short and worth reading in full, but the core move is treating content like programming — episodes with a premise, a world, a clear promise to the viewer — rather than as random posts or “personality scatter.”

The argument, in DeVans’s words

“The future of creator content is serialized, scalable, and bingeable, and the creators who embrace this shift early will define the next era. 2026 is going to be the year of the short-form show. We’re officially past the follower era. You don’t follow people anymore. You follow formats. You follow consistent ideas that make sense inside the ‘For You’ ecosystem.”

“The internet is the new television. A feed is a channel. A show is a format. A creator is a programmer. The ones who recognize this will start treating their content like actual programming. Episodes with a premise. A world. A clear promise to the viewer. Not random posts or personality scatter. A show.”

https://www.linkedin.com/posts/zackdevans_creativestrategy-digitalproduction-audiencedevelopment-activity-7415111592222306305-2HJy/

Why More Brands Are Ditching One-Off Influencer Campaigns

Forbes contributor Kristen Bousquet on the brand-side version of the shift: marketers are increasingly skeptical of the single-post deal and moving budget toward longer-term creator relationships and recurring content commitments. The piece walks through why one-off posts now under-deliver (audiences tune out an obvious sponcon insert), and what the alternative looks like in practice — multi-month partnerships, ongoing series, creators integrated into the brand’s broader content slate rather than dropped in once.

https://www.forbes.com/sites/kristenbousquet/2026/04/29/why-more-brands-are-ditching-one-off-influencer-campaigns/

Brands Are Producing Original Series for Social — Here’s Why

Marketing Brew on the production side of the same trend: brands aren’t just sponsoring creators’ shows, they’re producing their own. Episodic, recurring, with a recognizable cast and format. The economics work because a series amortizes the cost of audience-building across many posts rather than starting from zero each time — and because a returning viewer is worth more to a brand than an algorithmic stranger.

https://www.marketingbrew.com/stories/2025/08/18/brands-are-producing-original-series-for-social-here-s-why

Brands Are Betting on Creators to Make Their Next Hit Series

Digiday’s angle on the same shift, with more on the deal structures: brands fronting production money for creator-led series, equity-style splits on the resulting audience, formal development processes that look more like television commissioning than influencer marketing. Useful if you want the inside-baseball view of how these arrangements actually get done.

https://digiday.com/marketing/brands-are-betting-on-creators-to-make-their-next-hit-series/

As Engagement Drops, Brands Sponsor Social Series

AdAge connecting two threads that are usually reported separately — declining engagement on standard branded content, and the rise of brand-sponsored social series — and showing them as cause and effect. When the single-post format stops working, the budget has to go somewhere; series-style sponsorship is where a meaningful share of it is going.

https://adage.com/influencers-creators/aa-engagement-drops-brands-sponsor-social-series/
