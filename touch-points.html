Touch Points, Control, and the Impact Tradeoff

A different way of thinking about marketing communications: not what tools you have, but where the customer actually meets your brand — and how much you control each of those moments.

Four Types The Pattern More Views

There's a way of thinking about marketing communications that doesn't start with the six elements at all. It starts instead with the customer, and asks: what are all the places where this person might encounter our brand, and how much do we control each of them?

That question produces a different taxonomy, sometimes called the touch points framework, which is more useful than the promotional-mix taxonomy for diagnosing actual problems in actual companies. The six-elements model tells you what tools you have. The touch points model tells you where the customer is meeting your brand, which is a different and more strategic question.

5.1 The Four Categories

Touch points fall into four categories, distinguished by how much the company controls them and how much impact each tends to have on the customer's perception.

Company-created touch points are the planned ones. Advertising. The website. The packaging. The press release. The point-of-purchase display. The branded social channel. These are the touch points the marketing department actually runs. The defining feature of company-created touch points is that the company controls the message entirely. The defining limitation is that the customer knows the company controls the message, and discounts it accordingly. Most of a company's marketing budget gets spent here, even though the impact per dollar is among the lowest in the framework.

Intrinsic touch points are interactions that happen during the actual process of buying or using the product. The conversation with the salesperson. The experience of unboxing. The interaction with customer service. The wait time at the checkout. The flavor of the product, the smell of the store, the politeness of the call-center agent. Intrinsic touch points are partly controllable, partly not, and they live in the operational guts of the business rather than in the marketing department. They are also, per dollar spent, among the highest-impact communications a brand has, because they happen at the moment the customer is most attentive: the moment of actual contact with the product.

Unexpected touch points are everything the customer encounters that the company didn't plan and doesn't control. A news article. A friend's opinion. A negative review on a comparison site. A viral TikTok mocking the brand. An influencer recommendation. A casual mention in a podcast. Unexpected touch points are the lowest-control category and, in many cases, the highest-impact one. The reason is third-party credibility: a stranger's complaint about your customer service carries weight that no amount of "we care about customers" advertising can match. The marketing department generally treats unexpected touch points as somebody else's problem, which is part of why they tend to be the source of the brand's largest unmanaged risks.

Customer-initiated touch points are the ones that happen when the customer reaches out to the company. The call to the support line. The email to customer service. The complaint posted on Twitter. The product question submitted through the website. The chat with the AI agent. These touch points are largely controllable, because the company is the one responding, but they're also the touch points where the customer arrives with the highest expectations and the lowest patience. A botched customer-initiated interaction tends to do disproportionate damage, because the customer was already engaged enough to reach out.

5.2 The Pattern

The pattern in this table is the point. The touch points the company controls most are, on average, the ones that move the customer least. The touch points that move the customer most are, on average, the ones the company controls least. This is not an accident. It's a feature of how human persuasion works: a message gains credibility in proportion to how disinterested its source appears to be. A brand telling you it's great is the least credible source possible. A friend telling you it's great is among the most credible.

For an IMC program, the implication is that budget allocation can be misleading. Companies tend to spend most of their money in the high-control / low-impact quadrant, because that's where the marketing department's traditional tools live. The bigger leverage is often in the lower-control / higher-impact quadrants, which require working with parts of the business outside the marketing department: operations, customer service, product development, HR. The CMOs who treat IMC as purely a marketing function miss the largest source of brand equity available to them, which is the experience of actually being a customer.

There's a useful diagnostic that comes out of this framework. When a brand is performing poorly in the market and the marketing department's first instinct is to spend more on advertising, the touch-point framework asks a different question: is the problem actually in the company-created touch points, or is it in the intrinsic ones? A brand whose product is fine and whose customer service is excellent rarely fails for lack of advertising. A brand whose product is mediocre and whose customer service is broken will not be saved by a better ad campaign. Diagnosing which kind of problem you have is part of what separates strategic marketing from reactive marketing.

Takeaway
  • Four touch-point types: company-created, intrinsic, unexpected, customer-initiated.
  • Control and impact run in opposite directions. The touch points you control most move customers least.
  • High-impact touch points often live outside the marketing department: in operations, service, product.
  • When marketing isn't working, ask whether the problem is actually in the ads or somewhere upstream.

More Views

The touch-points framework above is one way to organize the customer's encounters with a brand. The literature has multiple competing versions of the same idea, and the differences are worth noticing. Some treatments organize touchpoints by degree of brand control (what we did above), others by lifecycle stage (pre-purchase, during purchase, post-purchase), and others by media type (owned, earned, paid, shared). Each organization surfaces different problems. The control framework forces the conversation about where impact comes from; the lifecycle framework forces the conversation about where customers actually are in their journey; the media-type framework forces the conversation about how each moment is funded and managed. Working IMC practitioners tend to use more than one in parallel. The four pieces below sample these positions: a practitioner take built around degree of control, a clean reference definition cataloging touchpoints by who controls them, a modern digital-era treatment organized by lifecycle and ownership, and a customer-journey view that adds the privacy and compliance angle most introductory treatments skip.
