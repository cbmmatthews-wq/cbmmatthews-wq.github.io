Overview

What This Unit Does

The Intro installed the working vocabulary. The four Ps. The funnel. The basics of STP. The argument that the channel is the last decision, not the first. Unit 1 takes that foundation and goes deep on the discipline that organizes it: Integrated Marketing Communications, or IMC.

IMC is the part of marketing that decides how all the available tools — advertising, PR, direct, digital, sales promotion, personal selling — work together to advance a brand's strategic goals. It is also the part of marketing that gets talked about most loosely. Half the people who use the term mean "we coordinate our messaging across channels." The other half mean something closer to "we have a strategic process for managing the relationship between every customer touch point and the company's brand." These are different claims, and most companies that say they do the second are actually doing the first.

This unit will try to get the distinction right. It will also push back on the version of IMC that has shown up in textbooks for thirty years, which captures part of what IMC is and misses the part that actually matters now.

01
IMC Fundamentals

What IMC Actually Is (and What the Old Definition Got Wrong)

If you ask three marketing professors to define integrated marketing communications, you will get four definitions, and at least one of them will be wrong. The phrase has been in circulation since the late 1980s, and like most concepts that survive that long in business, it has accumulated multiple meanings, several of which contradict each other.

1.1 The Old Definition and What It Got Right

Here is the definition the American Association of Advertising Agencies put forward in 1989, which is the one most older textbooks still print:

A concept of marketing communications planning that recognizes the added value of a comprehensive plan that evaluates the strategic roles of a variety of communication disciplines, for example, general advertising, direct response, sales promotion, and public relations, and combines these disciplines to provide clarity, consistency, and maximum communications impact.

That definition is not wrong, exactly. It correctly identified a real problem. In the 1980s, most large companies ran their marketing communications in silos. The advertising department made advertising. The PR department made PR. The direct mail people made direct mail. The sales promotion team made coupons. Each group reported to a different person, defended a different budget, and produced work that often contradicted the work coming out of the other groups. A consumer might see a sophisticated brand-image television commercial in the evening and then receive a chaotic discount coupon in the mail the next morning, and the two pieces of marketing would feel like they came from different companies. They essentially did.

IMC, in its 1989 formulation, was the recognition that this was insane. The slogan that emerged to describe the fix was one look, one voice. Everything the company said should look like it came from the same company. The TV ad and the coupon and the press release and the trade-show booth should all share a visual identity, a tone, a positioning. This was a real improvement over the chaos that preceded it, and it remains the part of IMC that working marketers reach for first when they're trying to explain the concept to a new hire.

1.2 Where One-Look-One-Voice Goes Wrong: Parrot Marketing

The problem is that one look, one voice, taken literally, has aged badly. It produces what we will call parrot marketing: the same message, in the same tone, with the same visual treatment, repeated across every channel with only the dimensions of the creative asset changing. You see this everywhere now. A brand produces a thirty-second television commercial and then crops the same footage into a fifteen-second YouTube pre-roll, a six-second bumper, a square Instagram post, a vertical TikTok, an email banner, and an out-of-home billboard. Same script. Same imagery. Same call to action. The creative team calls this "asset adaptation" and counts it as efficient. The consumer experiences it as the brand shouting the same sentence at them in six different rooms.

Parrot marketing fails for a reason the 1989 definition didn't anticipate: modern audiences are sophisticated consumers of media, and they can tell when a brand has nothing to say and is just saying it more times. Repetition that adds no new information feels manipulative, because it is. The consumer notices the seams. The brand starts to seem less authentic, not more coherent. The thing the strategy was supposed to achieve, a unified impression, gets undermined by the strategy itself.

1.3 A Caveat: When One-Look-One-Voice Is Actually Right

Before we move on, an important qualification. Everything just said applies to brands that customers already recognize. For a small brand or a startup that nobody has heard of, the math is different in a specific way.

When you have not yet crossed the awareness threshold — when customers cannot reliably identify your brand in the wild — the bottleneck is not message fatigue. The bottleneck is recognition. Every exposure has to do double duty. It has to deliver whatever message it's carrying, and it has to teach the audience what your brand looks like, sounds like, and stands for. A small brand that varies its visual identity, its tone, and its core message across every channel is not running an integrated campaign. It's running seven different campaigns for seven different brands, and the audience cannot connect any of them to each other.

So for an unknown brand, one look, one voice is closer to right, at least until recognition is established. The original 1989 framework, which is now wrong for the big brands it was developed to serve, is most defensible for the small brands it was never written for. The irony is worth noting.

The practical rule: lean toward consistency until customers can reliably identify your brand in the wild. Once they can, you have permission to start using the band-not-parrot framing we're about to introduce. Until then, the priority is making sure people can recognize you the second time they see you.

1.4 The Modern Definition

The contemporary view of IMC, articulated most clearly by Don Schultz at Northwestern in the early 2000s, replaces one look, one voice with something more useful and more demanding:

Integrated marketing communication is a strategic business process used to plan, develop, execute and evaluate coordinated, measurable, persuasive brand communications programs over time with consumers, customers, prospects, employees, associates and other targeted relevant external and internal audiences. The goal is to generate both short-term financial returns and build long-term brand and shareholder value.

That's a longer sentence. The important word in it is coordinated, which is doing different work than consistent. Consistency means everything looks the same. Coordination means everything is working toward the same strategic goal, in ways that take advantage of what each channel does best, without requiring that the executions match each other line for line.

Here's a useful mental model. Think of an IMC program as a band, not a parrot. A band has multiple instruments. The bass and the drums and the guitar and the vocals are not playing the same notes. If they were, the music would be unlistenable. What they share is a key, a tempo, a song. Each instrument does what it does best, and the result is coherent because the underlying composition is coherent, not because the instruments are playing in unison.

Television does things that email cannot. Email does things that out-of-home cannot. A TikTok influencer does things that a CFO presenting at an investor day cannot. The IMC manager's job is to make sure all of those executions are reinforcing the same strategic story, not to make sure they all sound the same.

Same Soul, Different Words

Nike, the brand that never repeats itself but always says the same thing

The textbook example of band-not-parrot done right is Nike, which is interesting because most people assume the opposite. The popular read is that Nike is locked into "Just Do It" and repeats it endlessly. The actual practice is more sophisticated. Nike is consistent at the level of idea. It is highly adaptable at the level of execution.

Over the past twenty years, Nike has repeatedly stepped away from the literal "Just Do It" phrase while still reinforcing the same underlying meaning: effort, identity, and pushing limits. Many of the brand's most recognized campaigns operate under headlines that don't prominently feature the original tagline at all:

  • Find Your Greatness (2012)
  • Dream Crazy (2018, the Kaepernick campaign)
  • You Can't Stop Us (2020)
  • Play New (2021)
  • Better For It (women-focused, 2015)
  • Why Do It? (a more recent reframe aimed at younger audiences)

Each of these is a different headline, a different tone, and a different story angle. None of them sounds like the others. All of them are unmistakably Nike. The brand is not coordinating around a repeated tagline. It is coordinating around a shared emotional and cultural space. Athletes, stories, and cultural moments carry the message, which means a long-form film, a social post, and a retail activation can all reinforce the same idea without any of them sounding alike.

This flexibility gives Nike a structural advantage in digital and social environments. By varying headlines and angles across campaigns, the brand effectively A/B tests at scale. Performance data shapes which messages get amplified, refined, or retired, rather than broadcasting one line across every touch point. A campaign built to run two months in traditional media can run alongside social executions where something new trends every few days. On social, surprise and delight fade fast. Post the same idea for a week and it's already stale. Repetition simply functions differently online than off. Nike has understood this for at least a decade. Many of its competitors are still figuring it out.

What Nike does is considerably harder than it looks. Maintaining a consistent core idea — the belief in effort, identity, and human potential — while allowing the wording and visuals to shift across campaigns requires real discipline. It is too easy for less focused brands to scatter, chasing likes or trending formats. It is too easy for individual teams to push in their own creative directions without anchoring back to the central idea. Brand cohesion is not a natural state. It is something that has to be actively protected.

The takeaway: Nike doesn't succeed by rigidly repeating "Just Do It." It succeeds by anchoring everything in a central idea strong enough to evolve, varied enough in execution that audiences never tune it out. The phrase may change. The feeling never does.

Don't chase. Anchor.

1.5 A Word About AI and Brand Voice

A contemporary application of all of this, worth flagging here even though we will return to it properly in Unit 2: the rise of AI brand-voice tools.

There are now dozens of platforms that promise to maintain consistent brand voice across all your marketing content. You upload a brand guide, train the model on your existing assets, and the output stays "on brand" forever. The technology actually works. The problem is what it's optimizing for.

These tools produce surface consistency: vocabulary, tone, sentence rhythm, formatting. They are excellent at making everything sound like the brand. What they cannot produce, because no AI tool currently can, is the strategic judgment about what each individual piece is supposed to do. Without that judgment driving the work, AI brand voice produces exactly what we called parrot marketing earlier in this section. Everything sounds the same. Nothing says anything.

There's a second problem, more recent and more important. The complaint that comes up repeatedly when audiences encounter AI-generated marketing content is that it feels soulless. The word is doing real work. Advertising, at its best, makes an emotional connection. Brands that audiences love are brands that have made the audience feel something. Numbing your messages down into perfectly on-brand AI prose to maintain voice consistency is self-defeating. You have traded the thing that actually moves customers — emotional resonance — for a kind of corporate hygiene that nobody asked for.

The brands using AI well are using it as a research and drafting tool, with human judgment driving what gets said and how it should land. The brands using AI poorly are letting the tool drive, and producing content that is consistent, on-brand, and forgettable. Unit 2 will return to this in the context of brand equity. For now, the principle: consistency is not the goal. Coherence in service of strategic impact is the goal. AI can help with the first. It cannot, yet, do the second.

1.6 The Organizational Reality

There is one more thing worth saying about IMC that neither the 1989 definition nor the modern one quite captures. IMC is fundamentally about who decides what.

In a typical marketing organization, the brand team decides positioning, the media team decides where ads run, the creative team decides what they say, the digital team decides what happens online, the PR team handles press, and the sales promotion team handles deals. Each of these groups has its own success metrics, its own vendor relationships, and its own quiet conviction that its work matters most. IMC, in practice, is the political and organizational work of getting those groups to coordinate without anyone feeling subordinated. The strategy part is easier than the management part. We will see this again later in the course when we look at how agencies are structured and compensated.

For the rest of this unit, when we use the term IMC, we mean Schultz's version: a coordinated, multi-channel program in which each element is doing what it does best, all of them are reinforcing the same strategic story, and the program is being managed as a single integrated effort rather than as a collection of campaigns that happen to share a logo.

Takeaway
  • IMC in 1989: "one look, one voice." A real improvement over the siloed chaos that preceded it.
  • IMC today: coordinated, complementary executions that share a strategic foundation, not a script.
  • Caveat: For small brands and startups, lean toward consistency until customers can reliably identify your brand. Variation is a privilege of established brands.
  • The band-not-parrot metaphor: different instruments, same song.
  • AI brand-voice tools produce surface consistency but tend toward soulless output. Consistency is not the goal. Coherence in service of strategic impact is.
  • The hard part of IMC isn't the strategy. It's the operational and political work of getting multiple teams to coordinate.

The Modern Media Taxonomy

PESO — How Brands Actually Get Exposure

The promotional channels available to a modern marketer are too many to list, and the list keeps growing. There were television, radio, magazines, newspapers, and outdoor advertising for most of the twentieth century, and that was roughly it. The current inventory adds search engines, social platforms, programmatic display, connected TV, podcasts, influencer content, branded content, owned email, owned apps, SMS, push notifications, retargeting networks, retail media, and whatever new format has launched in the time between when this paragraph was written and when you're reading it. Trying to organize this inventory by listing it is hopeless. The list is too long and changes too fast.

The framework that actually works, and that working marketers use, is called PESO, which stands for Paid, Earned, Shared, and Owned. PESO is not a list of channels. It's a classification system for how a brand obtains exposure on any given channel.

Most older marketing frameworks, including the ones in textbooks like Belch, collapse Earned and Shared into a single category. They are not the same thing, and the distinction matters enough that the full PESO breakdown is worth installing.

The center, where all four converge, is what a truly integrated program produces — a campaign that runs paid, gets covered by the press, gets amplified by the audience, and lives on the brand's owned channels long after the paid budget is spent. That center is what good IMC looks like in practice.

Paid media is anything you buy. Television spots, search ads, paid social, display banners, programmatic video, sponsored content, podcast read-arounds, billboards, magazine pages. The defining feature of paid media is that you control the placement, the timing, and the message. The moment you stop paying, the exposure stops. Paid media scales fast and can be targeted with precision, but consumers know they're seeing paid media and discount it accordingly. Trust is low. Reach is high.

Earned media is exposure you didn't pay for, generated by third parties who chose to talk about you. A journalist writes about your product. An industry analyst includes you in a report. A podcast host mentions you because they actually use the thing. A review site rates you. Earned media is the most credible form of marketing communication, because the source has no obvious commercial stake in your success. It is also the least controllable. You cannot make a journalist write a positive story about you, and the attempts to do so usually backfire. Earned media has to be cultivated through actual newsworthiness, actual product quality, and actual relationships, none of which scale on demand.

Shared media is exposure generated by your audience: likes, shares, reposts, user-generated content, comments, organic mentions on social platforms. Shared media looks like earned media at first glance, and many texts treat the two as the same category. They aren't. Earned media is one credible source talking about you to an audience. Shared media is the audience itself amplifying you to other people in the audience. The mechanics are different. The trust profile is different. The failure modes are different. A bad earned-media moment is a critical article. A bad shared-media moment is a viral pile-on. Shared media is fast and cheap when it works and impossible to manufacture when it doesn't.

Most of what marketing departments report as "engagement" is shared media at best, and most of what they call "viral" is wishful thinking applied to a normal week.

Owned media is the channels you control entirely. Your website. Your email list. Your app. Your loyalty program. Your retail store, if you have one. Your YouTube channel. Owned media is the slowest to build and the most valuable in the long run, because you don't rent the relationship from a platform. The drawback is that owned media doesn't reach people who haven't already decided to come to you, which means it can't do top-of-funnel work on its own.

2.2 The Flywheel

The strategic point that comes out of putting these two figures side by side is that the four quadrants feed each other. Paid seeds the audience that builds the owned channels. Quality content on owned channels eventually earns coverage. Earned coverage and quality content together produce shared activity. Shared activity, when it's working, reduces the amount of paid spend needed to reach the next layer of audience. A healthy IMC program is not four separate motions running in parallel. It's a flywheel.

2.3 Channel Monoculture Is Amateur Hour

A healthy IMC program uses all four PESO quadrants. The channels are not interchangeable, and treating any one of them as the whole program is the marker of an amateur.

The agency that pitches you on paid social as the answer to everything is selling you the only thing they know how to do. The PR firm that tells you earned media is the only credible form of marketing is defending its own margins. The social media consultant promising to make your brand "go viral" through shared content is selling you a lottery ticket and calling it a strategy. The content marketer evangelizing owned channels is correct about the long-run value and wrong about the short-run sufficiency.

Each of these positions is half right. None of them is enough.

The PESO framework is also where IMC and digital marketing finally connect. The 1989 definition of IMC was built for a world dominated by paid media, with PR as the only meaningful earned channel and direct mail as the only meaningful owned one. The framework didn't accommodate the digital revolution because the digital revolution hadn't happened yet. PESO is what IMC looks like when you take the same coordination principle — multiple complementary channels working toward a single strategic goal — and apply it to a media environment that includes social, search, content, and influencer ecosystems alongside the legacy media.

PESO is, in this sense, the digital and social application of IMC. Same logic, broader inventory.

Takeaway
  • Paid: you buy it. High control, low trust.
  • Earned: third parties talk about you. High trust, low control.
  • Shared: your audience amplifies you. Fast and cheap when it works, unmanufacturable when it doesn't.
  • Owned: channels you control entirely. Slow to build, valuable to compound.
  • A healthy IMC program uses all four. Channel monoculture is the marker of an amateur or someone selling you something.

2024 Ad Spend

Where the Money Actually Goes

Before we get into the six elements of the promotional mix, it is worth pausing on a simple question that pop culture consistently gets wrong: where does marketing money actually go?

The $390 billion advertising figure from the Intro is the standard number for pure advertising. The broader category — ad plus marketing including events, sponsorships, direct mail, shopper marketing, and other below-the-line activity — came to $551.9 billion in 2024 in the United States alone. Either way the scale is large enough to be hard to internalize. What matters here is not the headline number but the breakdown, because the breakdown contradicts almost everything the public conversation about marketing tells you.

Four charts. Four reality checks. Each one is included because each one corrects a specific piece of pop-culture marketing wisdom. We will return to this material in more depth later in the course when we get to media planning. For now, the goal is to make sure you are looking at the same map the people actually allocating budgets are looking at.

The single largest line in U.S. marketing is digital search — Google and Bing ads, mostly — at $123.6 billion, which is bigger than the next three channels combined. The "TikTok is everything" position is contradicted on the first chart of the breakdown. TikTok is a real channel, but it lives inside social media, which itself is the second-largest category overall, not the first.

Digital has clearly won the aggregate fight. Roughly 65 cents of every advertising dollar in 2024 went to a digital channel, and that share grows a little more every year. But traditional did not disappear. Linear TV alone is still nearly $59 billion. Direct mail remains a $37.5 billion industry. Radio is $10.9 billion. The honest framing is that digital won in volume while specific traditional channels remain disproportionately effective for specific purposes, which is why sophisticated marketers still use them.

The social media ad market looks competitive from outside and consolidated from inside. Meta — Facebook and Instagram combined — captured $64.6 billion, roughly 74% of all U.S. social media advertising in 2024. TikTok, despite the cultural noise around it, sits at $8.7 billion. The platforms that get the most conversation are not the platforms that get the most budget, and the gap between them is enormous.

Influencer marketing is real and growing fast — about 24% year over year — but the total is $8.1 billion. That is roughly 10% of what Meta alone collected in platform advertising, and meaningfully less than what U.S. companies spent on radio. Meta's ad business is about eight times the size of all U.S. influencer marketing combined. The public conversation about influencers dramatically overstates their share of actual marketing budgets, and the assumption that brands are "moving all their money to influencers" is contradicted by every serious data source.

3.1 What This Map Actually Tells You

A few patterns worth carrying forward.

First, the channels people talk about most are not the channels brands spend the most on. Cultural attention and economic scale are almost completely decoupled. TikTok dominates conversation; search dominates spend. Influencers dominate LinkedIn thought leadership; direct mail dominates actual budgets in many B2B categories.

Second, "digital vs. traditional" is the wrong framing. Within digital, search behaves nothing like social, which behaves nothing like CTV, which behaves nothing like programmatic display. Within traditional, linear TV behaves nothing like direct mail, which behaves nothing like out-of-home. The interesting tradeoffs are inside each category, not between the two big categories.

Third, none of these allocations are necessarily correct. They are what U.S. marketers actually spent. Some of that spending was strategic. Some of it was inertia. Some of it was vendor capture, where a brand keeps buying a channel because they've always bought it and switching feels risky. Just because $123 billion went to search doesn't mean that was the right $123 billion. Part of what you'll learn in this course is how to look at allocations like these and ask the harder question: was the spend actually advancing the brand's strategic goals, or was it just easier to defend?

We will return to this breakdown in real depth later in the course in the section on media planning. For now, when you see someone confidently claim that marketing has moved entirely to social, or that nobody does TV anymore, or that influencer marketing has displaced advertising, you have the numbers to push back.

Takeaway
  • Total U.S. ad + marketing spend in 2024: $551.9B (about $390B if you count only pure advertising).
  • Digital search is the single largest channel, larger than the next three combined.
  • Meta captured roughly 74% of all U.S. social media ad spend. The "competitive social market" is mostly a story.
  • Influencer marketing is real and growing, but at $8.1B is smaller than radio, smaller than direct mail, and about one-eighth the size of Meta's ad business.
  • Cultural attention and economic scale are decoupled. The channels people talk about most are not the channels brands spend the most on.

The Promotional Mix

The Six Elements of the Promotional Mix

Belch and most other introductory marketing texts organize promotional activity into six categories. The categories overlap at the edges and have gotten messier as digital channels have blurred the boundaries between them, but the taxonomy remains useful because it maps roughly onto how the marketing industry organizes itself. There are advertising agencies, PR firms, sales promotion shops, direct marketing specialists, digital agencies, and sales organizations, and each of these business types corresponds to one of the six categories below. Knowing the categories means knowing who to call when you need a specific kind of work done, and knowing what they're going to try to sell you when they answer the phone.

Digital / Internet All paid and organic activity through digital channels: search, social, display, mobile, email All three stages, depending on tactic Targetable, measurable, fast to test and adjust Crowded, increasingly expensive, platform-dependent

The table above is honest about the messiness. Most of the six elements operate at more than one funnel stage, depending on the tactic. Advertising can build awareness with a Super Bowl spot or close a sale with a retargeted display ad. PR can plant a brand in a magazine reader's head or rehabilitate a damaged reputation in the middle of a buying decision. Sales promotion usually lives at the bottom of the funnel but can occasionally pull a cold prospect into consideration if the offer is interesting enough.

That honesty is useful for analysis and useless for teaching. If everything overlaps with everything, students leave the chapter without a usable mental model. So here's the simplified version, the one you should keep in your head for the rest of the course:

4.1 The Simplified Funnel View

Figure 1.8
The Six Elements, Simplified into the Funnel
ToFU · Awareness

BoFU · Action

This simplification is not strictly accurate. It is, however, useful. Here's the reasoning behind each placement.

Advertising and PR sit at the top because their primary job in most modern marketing organizations is awareness. A television advertising buy and a press placement in The New York Times are both trying to plant the brand in the consumer's head before the consumer is in a buying mode. Both can do work further down the funnel, but if you had to pick a single funnel stage where each tool earns its budget, the top of the funnel is the honest answer.

Digital marketing and personal selling sit in the middle for different reasons. Digital marketing is genuinely funnel-agnostic; a digital tactic can do almost any job depending on the targeting and creative. We place it in the middle because the unique contribution of digital is its ability to nurture a prospect through consideration in ways the other tools can't, with retargeting, email sequences, content marketing, and progressive personalization. Personal selling, especially in B2B, is the dominant tool during consideration, where the prospect has identified a need and is actively evaluating options.

Direct marketing and sales promotion sit at the bottom because both tools are built around producing immediate, measurable response. A direct mail piece with an offer and a deadline, a coupon, a limited-time discount, an abandoned-cart email — these are conversion tools. They work on people who are already close to buying. Asking them to build awareness from scratch is asking them to do a job they're not designed for.

The reason this simplification is worth installing, even though it papers over real complexity, is that it gives you a default. When you're planning a campaign and someone asks "what's the right tool for awareness?", the simplified funnel gives you a fast answer: advertising and PR, and then you can adjust based on the specifics. When someone asks "what closes the sale?", the simplified funnel gives you direct marketing and sales promotion as the starting point. The defaults are correct often enough to be useful, and knowing when to deviate from them is what experience teaches.

Working marketers move between the nuanced version and the simplified version constantly. In a planning meeting, the simplified funnel is the working vocabulary. In an actual media plan, the nuanced table is what gets built. The skill is knowing which conversation you're in.

4.2 Notes on Each Element

A few notes on each category, in slightly more detail than the table allows.

Advertising is paid, non-personal communication delivered through media that reach groups rather than individuals. Television, radio, magazines, newspapers, outdoor, and, increasingly, the various digital ad formats that have grown up over the past two decades. Advertising is what most non-marketers think of when they hear the word "marketing," which is itself a useful piece of intelligence about how the discipline is perceived. It's also the largest line item in most companies' promotional budgets, even though that share has been declining for fifteen years as digital and direct-response tactics have taken share. Advertising's strength is its reach and its image-building capacity. Its weakness is that consumers know it's advertising and discount it accordingly, which means an advertising message has to work harder to land than it did thirty years ago.

Direct marketing is communication aimed at producing an immediate, measurable response. Direct mail, telemarketing, email marketing, SMS, addressable digital advertising, and the various retargeting tactics that follow you around the internet after you've visited a website. Direct marketing's defining feature is that it's almost always one-to-one rather than one-to-many, even when it's automated at industrial scale. A direct mail piece is addressed to you. An email lands in your inbox specifically. A retargeting ad follows your browser. The strength of direct marketing is measurability and precision. The weakness is that consumers are increasingly hostile to feeling targeted, and the regulatory and platform constraints on direct marketing (GDPR, iOS privacy changes, email deliverability rules) have made the discipline harder to execute well.

Digital / internet marketing is a category that didn't exist in the original promotional-mix taxonomy and was bolted on later, which is why it overlaps awkwardly with advertising and direct marketing. The reason it's its own category is that the underlying media (search, social, programmatic display, content, mobile) behave differently from traditional advertising in measurement, targeting, and pacing, and they require their own expertise. Most modern marketing organizations have a dedicated digital function, separate from the traditional advertising function, even though the two are increasingly impossible to separate cleanly. We'll spend significant time on this category later in the course.

Sales promotion is short-term incentive activity designed to produce immediate purchase. Coupons, discounts, contests, sweepstakes, rebates, samples, trade allowances, and the various flavors of "limited-time offer." Sales promotion is the fastest-acting tool in the mix and the easiest one to measure, which is part of why it gets overused. The problem with sales promotion is that it trains customers to wait for deals, which is brand-equity damage that doesn't show up on the quarter's P&L but shows up in the long run as eroding pricing power. Sales promotion is also the category most likely to be in tension with brand-building activity, which is a tension we'll return to in Section 7.

Publicity / public relations is the discipline of earning coverage and managing relationships with the various publics that matter to a brand: journalists, analysts, investors, employees, communities, governments. PR is the closest the promotional mix gets to working entirely in the earned-media channel. Its great strength is credibility: when a respected outlet writes about you, the message lands with weight that no amount of paid advertising can buy. Its weaknesses are that it's slow, that it's only partially controllable, and that the working definition of "PR" has expanded so far that it now includes corporate communications, crisis management, influencer relations, content seeding, and a dozen other functions that didn't exist when the term was coined. A modern PR firm might do all of these things, or only some of them, and figuring out which is part of the buyer's job.

Personal selling is one-to-one communication, usually face-to-face or via direct phone contact, aimed at facilitating a specific transaction. It's the original promotional activity, predating advertising by several thousand years, and it remains the dominant promotional channel in B2B markets and high-consideration consumer purchases (cars, real estate, financial advice). Personal selling is the most persuasive tool in the mix, because the salesperson can adapt the message to the individual buyer in real time. It is also the most expensive per contact, which is why it gets reserved for high-value transactions. Most of the rest of the promotional mix exists, in some sense, to make personal selling more efficient by warming up the prospect before the salesperson arrives.

4.3 Tools, Not Strategies

Here's the argumentative move that should land before we move on. These six categories are tools, not strategies. The failure mode in most marketing organizations is that each tool defends itself. The advertising team believes the answer to every problem is more advertising. PR firms reflexively recommend more PR coverage. Digital agencies want you to spend more on paid social, and sales promotion shops always have another promotional idea ready to go. The salespeople, naturally, just need more leads. Each position is self-serving in a different way, and each is incomplete, because each tool defends the budget of the people who specialize in it.

The IMC manager's job, properly understood, is to be the person in the room who doesn't have a horse in any of those races. The question isn't which tool wins. The question is which combination of tools advances the strategic goal at the lowest total cost. That question is harder than the tool-defenders make it sound, and answering it honestly is what separates marketing strategy from marketing tactics.

Takeaway
  • The six elements (advertising, direct marketing, digital, sales promotion, PR, personal selling) map onto how the marketing industry organizes itself.
  • Each tool has natural strengths, natural funnel positions, and natural weaknesses.
  • Each tool's specialists will defend that tool as the answer to everything. They are usually wrong.
  • The IMC question is never "which tool?" It's "which combination?"

The Customer's Map

Touch Points, Control, and the Impact Tradeoff

There's a way of thinking about marketing communications that doesn't start with the six elements at all. It starts instead with the customer, and asks: what are all the places where this person might encounter our brand, and how much do we control each of them?

That question produces a different taxonomy, sometimes called the touch points framework, which is more useful than the promotional-mix taxonomy for diagnosing actual problems in actual companies. The six-elements model tells you what tools you have. The touch points model tells you where the customer is meeting your brand, which is a different and more strategic question.

5.1 The Four Categories

Touch points fall into four categories, distinguished by how much the company controls them and how much impact each tends to have on the customer's perception.

Company-created touch points are the planned ones. Advertising. The website. The packaging. The press release. The point-of-purchase display. The branded social channel. These are the touch points the marketing department actually runs. The defining feature of company-created touch points is that the company controls the message entirely. The defining limitation is that the customer knows the company controls the message, and discounts it accordingly. Most of a company's marketing budget gets spent here, even though the impact per dollar is among the lowest in the framework.

Intrinsic touch points are interactions that happen during the actual process of buying or using the product. The conversation with the salesperson. The experience of unboxing. The interaction with customer service. The wait time at the checkout. The flavor of the product, the smell of the store, the politeness of the call-center agent. Intrinsic touch points are partly controllable, partly not, and they live in the operational guts of the business rather than in the marketing department. They are also, per dollar spent, among the highest-impact communications a brand has, because they happen at the moment the customer is most attentive: the moment of actual contact with the product.

Unexpected touch points are everything the customer encounters that the company didn't plan and doesn't control. A news article. A friend's opinion. A negative review on a comparison site. A viral TikTok mocking the brand. An influencer recommendation. A casual mention in a podcast. Unexpected touch points are the lowest-control category and, in many cases, the highest-impact one. The reason is third-party credibility: a stranger's complaint about your customer service carries weight that no amount of "we care about customers" advertising can match. The marketing department generally treats unexpected touch points as somebody else's problem, which is part of why they tend to be the source of the brand's largest unmanaged risks.

Customer-initiated touch points are the ones that happen when the customer reaches out to the company. The call to the support line. The email to customer service. The complaint posted on Twitter. The product question submitted through the website. The chat with the AI agent. These touch points are largely controllable, because the company is the one responding, but they're also the touch points where the customer arrives with the highest expectations and the lowest patience. A botched customer-initiated interaction tends to do disproportionate damage, because the customer was already engaged enough to reach out.

5.2 The Pattern

The pattern in this table is the point. The touch points the company controls most are, on average, the ones that move the customer least. The touch points that move the customer most are, on average, the ones the company controls least. This is not an accident. It's a feature of how human persuasion works: a message gains credibility in proportion to how disinterested its source appears to be. A brand telling you it's great is the least credible source possible. A friend telling you it's great is among the most credible.

For an IMC program, the implication is that budget allocation can be misleading. Companies tend to spend most of their money in the high-control / low-impact quadrant, because that's where the marketing department's traditional tools live. The bigger leverage is often in the lower-control / higher-impact quadrants, which require working with parts of the business outside the marketing department: operations, customer service, product development, HR. The CMOs who treat IMC as purely a marketing function miss the largest source of brand equity available to them, which is the experience of actually being a customer.

There's a useful diagnostic that comes out of this framework. When a brand is performing poorly in the market and the marketing department's first instinct is to spend more on advertising, the touch-point framework asks a different question: is the problem actually in the company-created touch points, or is it in the intrinsic ones? A brand whose product is fine and whose customer service is excellent rarely fails for lack of advertising. A brand whose product is mediocre and whose customer service is broken will not be saved by a better ad campaign. Diagnosing which kind of problem you have is part of what separates strategic marketing from reactive marketing.

Takeaway
  • Four touch-point types: company-created, intrinsic, unexpected, customer-initiated.
  • Control and impact run in opposite directions. The touch points you control most move customers least.
  • High-impact touch points often live outside the marketing department: in operations, service, product.
  • When marketing isn't working, ask whether the problem is actually in the ads or somewhere upstream.

Frequency & Compounding

Repetition, Wearout, and the Amplifier Effect

There's a piece of folk wisdom in advertising called the Rule of Seven, which says a consumer needs to see a message roughly seven times before it sinks in. Like most folk wisdom in marketing, the specific number is wrong, the underlying intuition is right, and the working version that practitioners actually use is more nuanced than either. The agency Assemblo published a widely-circulated visualization of the Rule of Seven that walks a consumer through a typical day's worth of exposures: a TV ad in the morning, a bus stop ad on the commute, a magazine ad at the coffee shop, an Instagram ad on the phone, a billboard during the afternoon, a radio spot in the car, a TV ad at night. Seven touches across one day, across formats and contexts, before the message has had enough exposures to take hold.

You can see the full graphic at assemblo.com. Meta's internal research, working with newer behavioral data, suggests the number on Instagram and Facebook is closer to thirteen exposures before consumer action against an ad spikes. Different channels and different purchase categories produce different numbers. What stays constant across all the research is that one exposure almost never works, and that this fact has shaped the entire economic model of advertising for a hundred years.

6.1 Why One Exposure Doesn't Work

The reason one exposure doesn't work is not, despite what your intuition might say, that consumers are stupid or inattentive. It's that the first exposure to a brand message is doing different work than later exposures. The first time you see an ad for a brand you've never heard of, your brain is doing identification work: figuring out what the thing is, what category it competes in, what the visual identity looks like, what the basic claim is. That cognitive work uses up most of the attention you're willing to give the ad. Persuasion happens later, on the second and third and seventh exposures, once identification is no longer the bottleneck and the message can do its actual job. An ad that runs once and disappears never gets past the identification stage, which is why one-time advertising buys, no matter how creative or expensive, rarely move sales.

6.2 Wearout

This is the case for repetition. But the case has a counterweight, which is wearout: the phenomenon where a message that's been seen too many times stops working and eventually starts working against the brand. Consumers tune out, get irritated, or begin to associate the brand with the experience of being annoyed by it. Wearout is real, and in modern fragmented media it happens faster than it used to. In the network-television era, a national advertiser might run the same thirty-second spot for two years before wearout became a measurable problem. On social media in 2026, a single piece of creative can wear out in days.

The traditional answer to wearout was message variation: produce multiple executions of the same campaign, rotate them, keep the strategic core consistent while the surface details change. Ally Financial's "Finally" campaign, which we'll look at more closely later in the course, is a textbook example. The agency shot eight to ten distinct scenes and edited them into different five-scene combinations, producing many variant spots from a single production budget. The strategic message stayed constant (life's small "finally" moments deserve a bank that helps you save). The surface kept rotating. Wearout slowed dramatically.

6.3 The Amplifier Effect

The other answer to wearout, and the one that matters more for understanding IMC, is what we will call the amplifier effect. Look again at the Assemblo journey. Most people see seven exposures and read it as straightforward repetition: see it seven times, message sticks. That's the first lesson, and it's correct as far as it goes. The second lesson, the one that matters more, is that the seven exposures are not the same exposure repeated seven times. They happen in different contexts, through different media, with different cognitive demands and different emotional textures.

Each exposure does work the previous ones couldn't. The television commercial activates one kind of memory. The billboard activates another. A passive ambient exposure works differently from an active attentive one. An audio-only exposure builds brand voice in a way that visual exposures never can. A social media exposure adds the implicit social context of being in someone's feed alongside content from their friends. The exposures are doing complementary jobs, not redundant ones.

This is the amplifier effect: repetition across coordinated channels compounds. Each new exposure adds dimension to the brand impression in a way that the same exposure repeated seven times in the same channel cannot. One plus one equals three. Seven Instagram ads in a row do less work than one Instagram ad, one television spot, one podcast read, one billboard, one earned-media story, one influencer mention, and one friend-of-a-friend recommendation, in roughly that order. The total impressions are the same. The persuasive impact is dramatically different.

The mechanism here is partly cognitive and partly social. Cognitively, different media activate different memory pathways. A jingle and a logo and a story are stored differently in the brain, and a brand that lives in all three pathways is harder to forget than a brand that lives in only one. Socially, different media carry different credibility signals. A friend's recommendation is processed differently than a paid ad, even when the underlying message is identical. The amplifier effect exploits both of these mechanisms simultaneously.

This is also why the parrot-marketing failure mode we identified back in Section 1 hurts brands so much. Parrot marketing produces the appearance of repetition without the amplifier effect. The brand gets the cost of running the message many times, without the compounding benefit of running it in genuinely different contexts. Coordinated repetition compounds. Identical repetition wears out.

6.4 "I Don't Like Advertising"

A digression that turns out to be important. One thing students consistently say when they show up to a course like this is some version of "I don't like advertising." Maybe more specifically: "I skip pre-roll, I block display ads, I trust influencers more than commercials, and I tune out anything that feels like marketing."

Two things to say about this.

First, the claim is largely accurate as a description of what you believe about your own behavior. Second, the claim is largely inaccurate as a description of what your behavior actually is. Consumer self-reports about marketing are among the least reliable data sources in social science. People tell pollsters they ignore advertising. The same people, measured behaviorally, demonstrate that advertising shapes which brands they consider, which they trust, which they pay premium prices for, and which they recommend to friends. The gap between what consumers say about marketing and what marketing actually does to them is enormous, and it's been measured by every serious researcher in the field for the past sixty years.

This matters because the version of the conversation that goes "audiences want authenticity, so brands should pivot to influencers and away from traditional advertising" is built on the self-report half of the equation. It treats what consumers claim to value as identical to what consumers actually respond to. In practice, the two are different, and the brands that have made bets on the self-report version have been losing those bets visibly. Nike's recent struggles, which we touched on earlier, are partly a story about a brand that listened too closely to what people said about emotional storytelling being dead and not closely enough to what its sales numbers said about it being foundational.

The point isn't that influencers are bad or that authenticity doesn't matter. Both are real phenomena that real marketers should pay attention to. The point is that what we want as consumers and what we want as marketing professionals are different things. As consumers, we are entitled to have aesthetic preferences and to ignore advertising we find annoying. As marketing professionals, our job is to figure out what actually works, even when what works is something we personally claim to dislike. The discipline lives in that gap.

This is also why frameworks like the 60/40 principle, which we'll cover in the next section, matter so much. They are built on behavioral evidence — what consumers actually do across thousands of campaigns measured over decades — rather than on what consumers report about their preferences. The behavioral evidence and the self-report evidence consistently disagree. The behavioral evidence is the one to trust.

6.5 Practical Rules

There are practical rules of thumb for using repetition without burning out the audience. Use similar messages rather than identical ones; each touch should add a little. Spread exposures across channels and formats rather than stacking them on one platform. Too little frequency and the message never registers. Too much and it stops being persuasion and starts feeling like a hard sell, which gets ignored. Getting this balance right, deciding how many times each channel should touch a customer and in what sequence, is exactly what good IMC media planners get paid for. We'll come back to the mechanics of frequency and reach planning later in the course.

For now, the working takeaway is that the question "how many times should we run this?" is the wrong question. The right question is "across how many genuinely different contexts can we land a coordinated message before wearout starts to bite?" The first is a media buyer's question. The second is an IMC manager's question.

Takeaway
  • One exposure almost never works. The first exposure does identification, not persuasion.
  • The Rule of Seven is a rough heuristic; Meta's data suggests it's closer to thirteen on social platforms. The exact number matters less than the underlying principle.
  • Wearout is real and faster in fragmented modern media than in the mass-media era.
  • The amplifier effect: coordinated repetition across different channels compounds. Identical repetition wears out.
  • What consumers say about marketing and what consumers do in response to marketing are different data sets. The behavioral data is what to trust.

The Big Tension

Brand vs. Performance, and the 60/40 Principle

We've been gesturing at this argument for most of the unit. It's time to make it explicit, because the brand-versus-performance debate is the single most consequential strategic argument in modern marketing, and it shapes almost every budget decision a marketing organization makes.

7.1 The Two Camps

The two camps, drawn at their cleanest, look like this.

Brand marketing, sometimes called awareness marketing, and informally and somewhat dismissively called squishy marketing, is the activity of building long-term equity in the brand: emotional connection, recognition, preference, and the kind of cultural presence that makes a consumer think of you before they're shopping. Brand marketing works slowly. It plants seeds for future purchase decisions that haven't been triggered yet. It's measured in awareness scores, brand health surveys, share of voice, and qualitative impressions, none of which a CFO finds entirely satisfying. The Ally "Finally" campaign is brand marketing. The Apple "Think Different" campaign was brand marketing. The Nike swoosh on a billboard with no call to action is brand marketing.

Performance marketing is the activity of producing immediate, measurable response: clicks, conversions, sign-ups, purchases. Performance marketing works fast. Every dollar can be traced, at least in theory, to a specific outcome. Search ads, retargeting, paid social with conversion objectives, affiliate links, abandoned-cart emails, promotional offers. The Google Ads dashboard is the natural habitat of performance marketing. The metrics are clean. The attribution is plausible. The CFO is happy.

7.2 Both Camps Are Half Right

Both camps are correct about something the other camp misses, and both camps overplay their hand when they're left in charge.

The performance side is correct that measurement matters and that brand marketers have, for decades, hidden behind unfalsifiable claims about "awareness" and "consideration" while spending real money on work that nobody could prove was working. The performance discipline has imposed a level of accountability on marketing that the discipline genuinely needed. Wanamaker's quote about not knowing which half of his advertising worked is less embarrassing to modern marketers than it was, because we now have at least some honest answer to the question, at least for some channels.

The brand side is correct that performance marketing systematically undervalues anything that doesn't convert within a measurement window, which means it systematically undervalues the work that creates future customers as opposed to harvesting existing demand. A brand with no equity has no pricing power, no defense against competitors who decide to outspend it on the same performance channels, and no pool of warm prospects for the performance ads to convert. Performance marketing without brand investment upstream is, in a memorable phrase, fishing in a shrinking pond.

7.3 What the Research Actually Says

The research evidence on this debate is unusually clear, for marketing research. Les Binet and Peter Field, working from the IPA Effectiveness Databank in the United Kingdom, have spent two decades analyzing what actually drives long-run marketing effectiveness across thousands of campaigns. Their headline finding, replicated in multiple studies and across multiple categories, is that established brands optimize long-run growth at roughly a 60/40 split: about 60% of marketing budget in brand-building activity and about 40% in performance activation.

The 60/40 principle is the closest thing modern marketing has to a default. It is not, however, a universal law. Several classes of business have good reasons to deviate from it, and understanding the deviations is more useful than memorizing the headline number.

7.4 The Deviations That Matter

A few of these deviations deserve a closer look, because they reveal something about how the principle actually operates.

Luxury brands run brand-dominant for a structural reason, not a stylistic one. A click-to-purchase retargeting ad for a $12,000 Rolex isn't just inefficient. It's actively destructive, because the entire commercial proposition of a luxury good rests on the appearance of scarcity, exclusivity, and the implicit suggestion that you don't need customers, they need you. Performance marketing in luxury isn't a different choice from brand marketing. It's an admission that the brand has failed.

Startups run performance-dominant out of necessity. When you have eighteen months of runway and need to prove unit economics before the next funding round, you cannot afford the long lag time of brand investment. Performance marketing buys you measurable feedback in days, which is what the early stage requires. The trap, and it is a trap that has killed many otherwise promising companies, is staying at the startup ratio permanently. The DTC supplement and apparel brands of 2020 to 2023 are the textbook cases: heavily performance-dependent for years, eventually unable to grow past the ceiling that paid-channel CPAs imposed, because they never built the brand recognition that would let them acquire customers more cheaply. They were efficient until they weren't, and by the time the efficiency broke, the brand asset they would have needed to fall back on had never been built.

Seasonal businesses flip the ratio during their active window. H&R Block runs brand-light campaigns through most of the year and floods the channel with performance activity from January through April. Halloween costume retailers do the same thing in October. The seasonal pattern is the inverse of the steady-state model: brand investment in the off-season enables performance efficiency during the window, because the brand is in the consumer's mind when the deadline triggers the buying decision.

The deeper point in all of these deviations is that the 60/40 split is a long-run average across an established brand's normal operations. It is not a recipe to be applied to every business. The right ratio for any specific company depends on its category, its maturity, its competitive position, and what part of the customer relationship it currently needs to invest in. What stays constant across all the deviations is the underlying mechanic: brand investment fills the pool, performance investment harvests the pool, and businesses that abandon the first activity eventually run out of fish.

We'll see this principle in action when we look at Ally Financial and Solo Stove later in the course as extended cases. For now, here's the working framing to carry forward: when someone proposes a marketing plan that's all performance, ask them what they're doing about the top of the funnel. A plan that's all brand deserves the inverse question: how are you harvesting the demand you're creating? The right answer is almost never only one. The right answer is almost always both, in proportions that depend on what kind of business this is and where it currently sits in its lifecycle.

Takeaway
  • Brand marketing builds long-term equity, works slowly, hard to measure.
  • Performance marketing drives short-term conversion, works fast, easy to measure.
  • The 60/40 principle (60% brand, 40% performance) is the default for established brands.
  • Deviations are real: luxury runs 85/15, startups run 20/80, seasonal businesses flip during their window.
  • The right question isn't "brand or performance?" It's "what does this business currently need most?"

What You Should Be Able to Do Now

You've just installed a vocabulary that the rest of the course will draw on continuously. Here's the working list of things you should now be able to do, regardless of whether you go on to work in marketing or watch it happen from another department.

You should be able to define IMC as something more substantive than "consistent messaging across channels." You should be able to explain why the band-not-parrot framing — coordinated complementary executions — is more demanding and more accurate than the older one-look-one-voice slogan, while also knowing the caveat about when one-look-one-voice is still right for small or unknown brands.

You should be able to classify any marketing activity in PESO terms and explain whether it's paid, earned, shared, or owned, and what that classification tells you about the activity's strengths and weaknesses.

You should be able to place a piece of marketing activity on the funnel and explain what job it's trying to do. You should be able to look at a campaign that's all bottom-funnel and ask where the awareness is coming from. You should be able to look at a campaign that's all top-funnel and ask where the conversion path is.

You should be able to distinguish brand marketing from performance marketing without taking sides reflexively, and you should understand why the long-run answer is almost always both, in proportions that depend on the specifics of the business.

You should be able to read a marketing plan and ask the harder questions. Where is this brand under-invested? Which touch points is the marketing department ignoring? Which tool's specialists are quietly defending their own budget rather than the company's strategic interest? Is the measurement story being told actually measuring what matters, or just what's easy to measure?

The rest of the course is built on this vocabulary. Unit 2 will take us into branding, where most of the assumptions we've been making about "equity" and "long-term value" will get a more formal treatment. After that, we'll move into the tactical machinery of how marketing actually gets planned, budgeted, executed, and measured. By the end of the semester, you'll have the working fluency to be useful in a marketing conversation, even if marketing isn't your function.

Wanamaker's problem hasn't been solved in a hundred and twenty-five years. It probably won't be solved in your career either. What you can do is be the person in the room who understands the problem honestly, which is more than most marketing departments can claim.

Glossary

60/40 principle
The finding, drawn from Binet and Field's analysis of the IPA Effectiveness Databank, that established brands tend to optimize long-run effectiveness at a budget split of roughly 60% brand-building and 40% performance activation. Deviations are common and often justified, particularly in luxury, startup, and seasonal categories.
Advertising
Paid, non-personal communication delivered through media that reach groups rather than individuals. Television, radio, print, outdoor, and digital display all qualify.
AIDA
An older model of the customer journey: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. Largely replaced in modern usage by the three-stage ToFU / MoFU / BoFU funnel.
Amplifier effect
The compounding impact of coordinated repetition across different channels and contexts, as opposed to the diminishing returns of identical repetition in the same channel.
Awareness threshold
The point at which customers can reliably recognize a brand in the wild. Brands below this threshold should lean toward consistent messaging across channels; brands above it can use varied executions in service of a single underlying idea.
Band-not-parrot
Mental model for modern IMC: different channels playing complementary parts in service of a shared strategic foundation, as opposed to repeating identical content across every channel.
BoFU
Bottom of Funnel. The action stage of the customer journey, where prospects convert into customers. Search advertising, retargeting, sales promotion, and direct marketing all do their primary work here.
Brand equity
The intangible value a brand carries beyond the functional attributes of its products, often expressed as pricing power, customer loyalty, and resistance to competitive pressure.
Brand marketing
Marketing activity aimed at building long-term equity, recognition, and emotional connection. Sometimes called awareness marketing or squishy marketing. Works slowly, hard to measure short-term, foundational for long-run growth.
Customer-initiated touch point
A brand interaction triggered by the customer, typically through support, service, or inquiry channels. Largely controllable by the company, but carrying high stakes because the customer arrived with expectations.
Direct marketing
One-to-one communication aimed at generating an immediate, measurable response. Direct mail, email marketing, telemarketing, SMS, addressable digital ads.
Earned media
Exposure generated by third parties who choose to talk about the brand, with no payment involved. Press coverage, analyst reports, podcast mentions, expert reviews. High credibility, low control.
IMC (Integrated Marketing Communications)
The strategic coordination of multiple marketing communication channels and tactics so that each element reinforces the same underlying strategic story, taking advantage of what each channel does best.
Intrinsic touch point
A brand interaction that happens during the actual experience of buying or using the product. The sales conversation, the unboxing, the customer service call, the product experience itself.
Marketing funnel
A model of the customer journey from initial awareness through purchase, in stages: ToFU (awareness), MoFU (consideration), BoFU (action). Each stage leaks; the funnel narrows.
MoFU
Middle of Funnel. The consideration stage, where prospects are evaluating options. Email, retargeting, content marketing, reviews, and mid-funnel video all do their primary work here.
Owned media
Marketing channels the company controls entirely: its website, email list, app, owned social channels, retail stores, loyalty program.
Paid media
Marketing channels the company pays to access, where the placement, timing, and message are under the company's control. Television, radio, search ads, paid social, programmatic display, out-of-home.
Parrot marketing
The failure mode of one-look-one-voice IMC, where the same message gets repeated identically across multiple channels with only the creative dimensions adjusted. Wears out fast, feels inauthentic, undermines the coordination it was supposed to achieve.
Performance marketing
Marketing activity aimed at producing immediate, measurable response: clicks, conversions, sign-ups, purchases. Works fast, easy to measure, optimizes short-term return at the potential expense of long-term equity.
Personal selling
One-to-one communication, typically face-to-face or by phone, aimed at facilitating a specific transaction. Dominant in B2B and high-consideration consumer purchases.
PESO
The modern media taxonomy: Paid, Earned, Shared, Owned. A framework for classifying how a brand obtains exposure on any given channel.
Positioning
The decision about what a brand should mean in the minds of its target customers. Distinct from what the product is; concerned with what the product means.
Promotional mix
The traditional six categories of marketing communications: advertising, direct marketing, digital marketing, sales promotion, publicity / public relations, and personal selling.
Publicity / Public Relations
The discipline of earning coverage and managing relationships with the various publics relevant to a brand: journalists, analysts, investors, employees, communities, governments.
Sales promotion
Short-term incentive activity aimed at producing immediate purchase: coupons, discounts, rebates, contests, sweepstakes, samples.
Segmentation
The analytical work of dividing a market into groups that share meaningful characteristics: demographic, psychographic, behavioral, geographic.
Shared media
Exposure generated by the audience itself through likes, shares, reposts, user-generated content, and organic mentions. Distinct from earned media in that the source is the audience, not a third-party authority.
Targeting
The strategic choice of which segments to invest marketing resources against, and which to deliberately under-invest in.
ToFU
Top of Funnel. The awareness stage of the customer journey. Television, out-of-home, display advertising, PR, sponsorships, and brand-driven social all do their primary work here.
Touch point
Any place where a customer or prospect encounters the brand. Categorized as company-created, intrinsic, unexpected, or customer-initiated, with control and impact tending to run in opposite directions.
Unexpected touch point
Brand exposure the company didn't plan and doesn't control: news coverage, online reviews, word of mouth, viral content. Often the highest-impact touch point category.
Wanamaker's problem
The acknowledgment, attributed to retailer John Wanamaker around 1900, that "half the money I spend on advertising is wasted; the trouble is I don't know which half." Still the foundational measurement problem in marketing.
Wearout
The point at which a marketing message has been seen too many times and stops working, eventually beginning to work against the brand by irritating or alienating the audience.
