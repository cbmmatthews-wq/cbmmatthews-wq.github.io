Overview

From Meaning to Choice

Unit 4A ended at decoding — the moment a message has been rebuilt inside the receiver's head and means whatever it now means to them. Unit 4B begins with the next question, which is the one that matters commercially: now what? The consumer has the meaning. What do they do with it? That question opens out into the rest of consumer behavior — recognizing that something is off, looking for options, weighing them, choosing one, and living with the choice afterward. The standard name for this is the consumer decision process, and it is the spine this unit hangs on.

There is a comfortable version of the process where the consumer is a careful rationalist, gathering information, weighing alternatives, and picking the optimum. That version is occasionally true — for the cars and houses and surgical decisions that justify the effort. For most of the purchases people actually make, it is closer to fiction. The real consumer is busy, half-attentive, working from memory and shortcuts, swayed by mood, primed by what showed up in their feed an hour ago, and just as likely to grab the familiar package as to compare three brands on a spec sheet. The model still applies. The consumer just runs it on a much smaller budget of effort than the model assumes.

This module covers both versions, because both are real. We will walk the five-stage spine — problem recognition, search, evaluation, purchase, post-purchase — and then look at the internal psychological processes running underneath: motivation, perception, attitude, integration, learning. The aim is not to make you memorize the stages but to give you a diagnostic: when a purchase isn't happening, you should be able to point at the stage where the journey broke, and at the process running underneath it that needs work.

One framing assumption runs underneath the whole module, same as in 4A. Assume a contemporary consumer: attention-scarce, algorithmically-mediated, and mobile-first, meeting brands across fragmented paid, owned, earned, and shared touchpoints, trusting peers and creators over polished advertising, and expecting friction-free action. The five-stage model was written before that consumer existed in full. It still holds. The conditions it operates under have shifted, and the Current View at the end of each section is where we say how.

For the non-marketers in the room

Most of you are not in marketing — operations, HR, management, accounting. This module is still about your work, because consumer behavior is just decision behavior with a price tag attached. The five stages explain why a perfectly engineered new HRIS rollout still has forty-percent non-adoption six months in: no felt problem recognition (the old system was fine), weak search incentive, high switching friction, and no positive post-purchase feedback loop closing back to the people who built it. It is the same anatomy as a failed product launch. Learn the model here; apply it to systems, policies, hires, and process changes everywhere else.

The Journey

Five Stages, One Spine

The model is older than marketing. It descends from John Dewey's 1910 How We Think, where the steps of solving a problem were laid out as a general account of reflective thought, and it was formalized for consumers in the 1960s by Engel, Kollat, and Blackwell. The version most textbooks now teach (Belch & Belch, 2023) is five stages, in this order — with five internal psychological processes running underneath at every stage.

Problem recognition is the gap that starts everything: the consumer perceives a difference between how things are and how they want them to be. Sometimes the gap is small (out of milk) and sometimes large (this job is killing me); sometimes it is internal and felt, and sometimes a marketer planted it (you didn't know you needed noise-cancelling earbuds until you saw someone wearing them on a flight). Without a gap, the journey doesn't start. §2 builds this out as the modern pain-point reframe.

Information search is what the consumer does once the gap is named. It starts internally — what do I already know about this? — and only moves to external sources if the internal scan comes up short. The external sources used to fit on a short list (asked a friend, looked in the Yellow Pages, walked the store). Now they sprawl across paid, owned, earned, and shared touchpoints, mediated by algorithms that decide what gets surfaced. §4 covers search alongside perception, because they are the same act seen from two angles: search is what the consumer is doing; perception is what is happening to the information while they do it.

Alternative evaluation is where the comparison happens: which of the considered options best meets the criteria that matter to this consumer? This is where decision rules — sometimes careful, more often heuristic — narrow the candidates to one. §5 covers this. It is also where the classical model and the real consumer diverge most sharply, because the model assumes more deliberation than most purchases actually get.

Purchase decision is not the same as the actual purchase, and the gap between the two is where a lot of marketing money goes to die. A consumer can fully decide to buy something and still not buy it. They get distracted, the checkout line is too long, the website crashes at the payment step, a friend mentions a different brand on the way to the store. This is why operations and friction matter as much as the persuasion that produced the decision in the first place — a point most marketing organizations underweight, and most operations teams understand intuitively.

Postpurchase evaluation closes the loop. The consumer compares the actual experience to what they expected, lands somewhere between satisfied and disappointed, and — crucially — remembers. That memory is the single most important input to the next problem-recognition phase. A satisfied customer makes the next purchase decision in milliseconds with no search at all. A disappointed one runs the whole journey from the start, with the previous brand removed from consideration. §7 covers post-purchase along with the external influences that frame the whole journey.

The decision model is older than marketing. The consumer is busier than the model assumes. Both observations are true, and the rest of this module sits in the space between them.

Two things to hold onto before we leave this overview of the spine. First, the stages are a map, not a script. Real consumers skip stages (an impulse buy collapses search and evaluation into a single moment in the checkout aisle), loop back (post-purchase regret kicks off a new problem recognition for the next purchase, often immediately), abandon mid-journey (most cart abandonment is exactly this), and run different stages at very different intensities depending on the stakes. A new car gets the full deliberative treatment; a coffee on the way to work gets habit. Second, the stages map cleanly onto the funnel you have already met in this course. Awareness and interest at the top correspond roughly to problem recognition plus early search. Consideration in the middle is alternative evaluation. Conversion at the bottom is the purchase decision. Loyalty and advocacy below the funnel are post-purchase running well. The two diagrams describe the same phenomenon from opposite sides of the transaction.

The Funnel, From the Other Chair

Why the decision process and the marketing funnel are the same diagram

Throughout this course we talk about the marketing funnel: awareness, interest, consideration, conversion, loyalty. It is worth seeing right now that the funnel and the decision process are not two separate things. They are the same phenomenon described from opposite seats. The decision process is what the consumer experiences while moving toward a purchase. The funnel is what the marketer sees while measuring the same population thinning as it converts. The consumer asks at each stage what to do next. The marketer counts heads at each stage and asks where is the leakage.

The diagnostic value is in the alignment. A common failure mode is a campaign aimed at one stage while the actual audience is in another — running a hard-conversion ad ("buy now, 24-hour sale") at an audience still in problem recognition, or running an awareness-building brand ad at people who already know the brand and are stuck on the comparison. It is the same diagram drawn from opposite chairs, and if you can name which chair you are sitting in and which stage your audience is actually in, half the diagnostic work is done.

The funnel is the decision process the marketer can count.

The Enduring Idea
  • The consumer decision process is a five-stage sequence: problem recognition → information search → alternative evaluation → purchase decision → postpurchase evaluation. Each stage is a behavior the consumer performs; the journey from stage one to stage five is what we call buying.
  • Underneath those visible stages run five internal psychological processes: motivation, perception, attitude formation, integration, learning. The stages explain what the consumer does. The processes explain how they do it.
  • The stages are a map, not a script. Consumers skip stages, loop back, abandon mid-stream, and run different stages at very different intensities. A high-stakes purchase gets the full deliberative treatment; a low-stakes one gets habit.
  • The five stages are the marketing funnel seen from the other chair. The consumer experiences a journey; the marketer measures a thinning population. Same diagram, opposite seats.
The Current View

The model still holds, but the path it describes no longer does. The five-stage spine assumed a roughly linear journey, with a discrete number of touchpoints between problem and purchase. That assumption was never quite right, but it was close enough to be useful. It isn't anymore. McKinsey's 2024 B2B Pulse Survey found that B2B buyers now use an average of 10.2 interaction channels across a single purchase, up from just five in 2016 — the channel mix has doubled in under a decade (McKinsey & Company, 2024). Consumer journeys show the same pattern. And a non-human gatekeeper has joined the trip: Adobe's May 2025 survey of US users found that 36% have discovered a new product or brand through ChatGPT, with the figure climbing to 47% among Gen Z — a discovery channel that did not exist when the canonical decision model was last revised (Search Engine Journal, 2025). The modern face of the five-stage model is the customer journey map (Figure 4B.2 above) — same stages, redrawn as a non-linear loop across fragmented touchpoints and drawn per persona, because the same brand serves different people running materially different journeys to the same outcome. §3 builds out the persona move. The rest of this module fills in what happens at each stage of the journey those personas walk.

02
Pain Points

It Starts with a Gap

Every purchase a textbook talks about starts the same way: the consumer notices that the situation they are in is not the situation they want to be in. Standard treatments call this problem recognition and treat it as a gap between an actual state and an ideal state — the difference, in other words, between where things are and where the consumer believes they should be (Belch & Belch, 2023). The gap can be tiny and immediate (the coffee pot is empty) or large and slow-building (this job is wearing me out), and the word problem should not be taken too literally — the gap can be the pull of a goal as easily as the push of a complaint. What matters is the difference. Without a felt gap, the decision journey doesn't start.

The canonical model is correct as far as it goes, but it is a bit thin for practical work. "Gap between actual and ideal" is true and generic, and it does not tell a product team what to actually do about it. The vocabulary that has overtaken it in modern practice — pain points, jobs to be done, friction — does essentially the same conceptual work, but with the added discipline of forcing the marketer to be specific: whose gap, which kind, and what is in the way of closing it. The reframe does not replace problem recognition; it operationalizes it. This section walks the canonical model first, then the modern vocabulary, and then the place where the two views collide most usefully — the friction column, which is where most of the contemporary money and engineering effort actually lives.

2.1 Where Gaps Come From

Sources catalog several common origins for the gap. The consumer runs out of something they were using. Their existing solution stops working well, or never quite did. Life circumstances change — a new job, a baby, a move — and what was adequate yesterday isn't today. A related purchase creates a new sub-need (the new phone needs a case). And, most pointed for marketers, the marketer themselves can plant a gap by introducing a category or product the consumer didn't previously know they should want (Belch & Belch, 2023).

Notice the range. The first kinds — running out, dissatisfaction, life change — are gaps the consumer would have arrived at on their own; the marketer's job is to be the first option that comes to mind when the gap surfaces. The last kind, marketer-induced, is structurally different and more aggressive: the gap does not exist until the marketer creates it. Most personal-hygiene categories, most fashion cycles, and a substantial fraction of consumer electronics live in this territory. We will come back to the ethics in a moment. The mechanism is the same either way; what changes is the source.

2.2 The Pain-Point Reframe

The vocabulary used in product strategy, UX research, and customer-success work looks slightly different, but it is doing the same conceptual job. It calls the gap a pain point — a specific, articulable thing the customer experiences as not-right about their current situation. The discipline is to be specific enough about the pain that you could write it as a sentence in the customer's own voice. "I can't easily compare prices across vendors." "I feel embarrassed every time I have to ask for help." "My team's tools make us look amateurish to clients." Each of those is a problem recognition; each is also doing something the abstract textbook version doesn't — naming the kind of pain in a way that points at a specific marketing or product response.

The most useful taxonomy in modern practice sorts pain points into three kinds, plus a fourth category that is structurally different and worth keeping separate.

Not a fourth kind of pain so much as the thing that sits between a recognized pain and the action that would resolve it. Three kinds of pain, plus the barrier category. The first diagnostic question for any campaign or product effort is which column is the consumer actually in — because the column tells you what kind of intervention will move them.

Functional pains are about the job not getting done well enough. The software crashes on big files; the commute eats two hours a day; the product wastes electricity; the report takes a week to assemble manually. These are usually the easiest to surface in customer interviews because customers will volunteer them — the situation is objectively inconvenient and they know it. Most B2B marketing addresses functional pains as the primary lever. Make the job easier, faster, cheaper, more reliable, and the case writes itself.

Emotional pains live one layer down. They are about how the situation makes the consumer feel: anxious, embarrassed, overwhelmed, bored, resentful, lonely. Emotional pain usually runs underneath functional pain — the software crashing is functional, but the feeling of humiliation in front of the client when it crashed during the demo is the emotional pain attached to it, and is usually what actually drives the switch. Emotional pains rarely get volunteered directly. They show up obliquely, in the words customers reach for when describing the situation.

Social pains are the column where identity and consumption fuse. They run on how the consumer is seen by others, or how they want to be seen. My team's tools make us look unprofessional. I'm worried what my neighbors think about my old car. I want to be the kind of person who reads more. Most of what we call "brand-building" operates in this column — the brand offers a self-image the consumer can wear in front of others. Social pains can be uncomfortable to admit out loud, which is why they are often the hardest to elicit in research. They are also, often, the strongest drivers of preference in premium and luxury categories, where the functional and emotional pains have largely already been solved by the price-of-entry features.

Naming the Pain: How Snickers Built a Decade on "Hangry"

A worked example of the emotional column from Figure 4B.3

From 2007 to 2009, Snickers was losing share in a saturated candy-bar category where every brand looked roughly the same and nobody had a defensible reason to pick one over the next. The brief Mars gave its agency, BBDO New York, was unremarkable: rebuild Snickers around a single ownable idea. The work, launched at Super Bowl XLIV on February 7, 2010 with a now-iconic spot of Betty White getting tackled into a mud puddle, did something most marketing campaigns never attempt — it gave a name to a pain consumers already felt but had no shorthand for. The pain was the personality shift that comes with hunger: the irritable, irrational version of yourself that low blood sugar produces. The campaign called it being hangry, made the personality shift its dramatic premise (a man on the field morphs into Betty White until he eats a Snickers and "becomes himself again"), and let the audience finish the joke.

The big idea had enough legs to sustain dozens of versions across markets over the next decade — different cultures, same named pain. When a pain-point articulation is right, it travels.

By the end of 2012 the campaign had driven a $376 million sales increase, lifted internal confidence in the brand among Mars marketing directors by 25 percentage points (from 43% who felt the brand had momentum, to 93%), and collected Cannes Lions and Effie Awards along the way (The Brand Hopper, 2024). The portmanteau itself entered the dictionary. The mechanism is a textbook case for the emotional column in §2.2. Hunger framed as a functional pain is a dead brief: "I need calories" is not a brand opportunity. Hunger reframed as an emotional pain — "I am not myself, and the people around me notice" — is a brand opportunity, because the consumer can now articulate what is wrong and what would fix it. Snickers did not invent the feeling; they named it, and then made themselves the named solution. That move — finding a felt pain that lacks an articulation, providing the articulation, and attaching the brand to the resolution — is the single most repeatable play in the pain-point playbook.

One honest note before moving on. The campaign was not flawless. In August 2021 a Spanish variant featuring influencer Aless Gibaja drew accusations of homophobia for implying that hunger could change someone's sexual orientation; Snickers pulled the spot and apologized (History Oasis, 2024). The case sits inside a broader pattern: campaigns built around "X is not the real you" carry an inherent risk of implying that some identities are not the real version of a person. The mechanism works precisely because it is identity-adjacent, which is also where it goes wrong when the writers aren't paying attention.

When you can name the pain better than the consumer can, the brand becomes the named solution.

Those are the three kinds of pain. The fourth category — friction — is structurally different and deserves its own treatment, because the modern frontier of marketing investment is increasingly built around it.

Problem recognition has always been the same idea: the consumer notices a gap. The modern vocabulary just forces the marketer to be specific about whose gap, which kind, and what is in the way of closing it.

2.3 Friction: The War on Stage Four

Friction is not really a fourth pain. It is the thing that sits between a recognized pain and the action that would resolve it — the gap, in other words, between purchase decision (Stage 4 of the canonical model) and the actual purchase that a textbook treats as the same event. They are not the same event. A consumer can be fully persuaded, fully decided, and still not act, because the friction of acting is too costly. From the marketer's chair, a perfectly persuaded prospect who walks away at checkout looks like a marketing failure. From the consumer's chair, they just had a Saturday they didn't want to spend.

The Trillion-Dollar Bet on the Friction Column

Why marketing and operations have stopped being separate disciplines

For most of marketing's history, friction was treated as an operations problem — a tax on conversion to be reduced where convenient, but largely a separate discipline from the persuasion side of the house. The last twenty years have collapsed that distinction. Amazon's 1-Click checkout, patented in 1999, was the first major consumer demonstration that removing a friction step is worth more than a marketing campaign. At the patent's expiration in 2017, roughly half of all Amazon app customers were using 1-Click regularly, and the frictionless brand it built remained Amazon's most durable competitive asset long after the underlying technique became industry-standard (Nonobvious, 2023). Apple Pay, Google Pay, Shop Pay, and the entire mobile-wallet category emerged on the same logic.

Buy-now-pay-later took the friction-reduction play further: split the cost into four payments, skip the credit application, decide at the moment of want rather than the moment of available cash. The category — Affirm, Afterpay, Klarna, PayPal's installment products — reached an estimated $560 billion in global transaction volume in 2025, growing 13.7% year over year, with especially heavy use among younger consumers (ResearchAndMarkets, 2025). The CFPB and several US states have responded with new rules; New York's 2025 Buy-Now-Pay-Later Act is the first state-level licensing framework (National Law Review, 2025). The friction-reduction case is real. So is the case that some of the friction being removed was actually reflection — pauses that gave consumers time to decide whether they wanted the thing at all. Where the line falls between useful frictionlessness and predatory frictionlessness is now a live regulatory question, and probably a generational ethical one.

Friction is a category of pain point in its own right, and often the one the marketer can most directly fix.

The cross-functional payoff for the non-marketers in this room is right here, in this column. The operations team and the marketing team are working on the same conversion problem from different angles — marketing is producing the decision; operations is making sure nothing between the decision and the action gets in the way. Forty percent of an HRIS rollout failing is a friction problem dressed up as a change-management problem. A new internal tool that nobody uses is a friction problem dressed up as a training problem. Once you have named the pain, the next question is always what is between the user and the action, and that question rarely belongs to one department alone.

2.4 When the Marketer Plants the Gap

A note on the most ethically loaded part of this section. Problem recognition does not require that the consumer recognize the problem on their own. The marketer can, and often does, plant the gap — running advertising designed to make the consumer feel a lack they had not previously felt: hygiene products that imply you smell bad, fashion cycles that reframe last season's clothes as embarrassing, status-anxiety pitches in luxury categories. The mechanism is identical to natural problem recognition; the source is the marketer rather than the world. The legitimate version of the move is introducing a category the consumer would value if they knew about it — most genuinely new products live here, from antibiotics to smartphones to electric vehicles. The illegitimate version is manufacturing a gap that doesn't exist in order to sell a remedy for it. The line between the two is real and uncomfortable to draw in practice, and most marketers will spend their careers somewhere along it. The model doesn't tell you where the line is. It just tells you the move exists, and that you should be honest with yourself about which side of it any particular campaign is sitting on.

The Enduring Idea
  • Problem recognition is the gap between the consumer's actual state and ideal state. Without a felt gap, no decision journey starts. The gap can be the pull of a goal as easily as the push of a complaint.
  • The gap can arise naturally (running out, dissatisfaction, life changes, related purchases) or be planted by the marketer. The mechanism is the same; the source is what differs.
  • The canonical model treats the gap as a single undifferentiated concept. In practice, gaps are functional, emotional, or social — and the first diagnostic question for any campaign is which column the target consumer is actually in.
  • A gap that is felt but not acted on is usually a friction problem: the pain is recognized and the solution is identified, but the cost of action is higher than the cost of inaction. Friction is the dark matter of consumer behavior.
The Current View

The product-management, UX, and customer-success disciplines have largely renamed problem recognition as pain points, and the rename does useful work: it forces specificity about whose pain, which kind, and what friction is blocking action. The most striking shift in the last two decades has been the rise of friction reduction as a category of marketing investment, which has blurred what used to be a clean line between marketing and operations. BNPL alone moved roughly $560 billion globally in 2025; one-click checkout reshaped e-commerce; mobile wallets and digital identity are now removing friction at a pace that makes the canonical model — with its discrete, deliberate stages — look like a slow-motion version of what actually happens at modern purchase. A textbook still describes the shape of the journey accurately. The journey just runs faster, and the marketer's leverage has shifted toward removing the obstacles to action as much as toward creating the desire for it. The bridge into §3 is that which pain a consumer feels — and which they will pay to resolve — depends on who that consumer is. That is the persona move, and it is the centerpiece of the next section.

03
Motivation

What Gives the Gap Its Energy

A graduating MBA needs a watch. Two of them, actually — two students from the same program, same age, same starting salary, both with exactly the gap §2 just described. One spends an hour comparing the Apple Watch's health features against a Garmin's training metrics and buys whichever has better battery life. The other spends six months saving for a Tudor Black Bay because, for them, a good watch is supposed to mean something. Identical gap, identical demographic, entirely different journey to a purchase — and the variable that explains the difference is what marketers call motivation: the question of why this consumer wants what they want, which is what determines which category of product gets bought, which arguments will persuade them, and what "a good watch" even is.

Motivation is the engine the rest of this module sits on top of. Search behavior (§4), evaluation rules (§5), brand loyalty (§6), post-purchase satisfaction (§7) — all of these run differently depending on which motive is in play. Naming the motive correctly is the diagnostic move that organizes everything downstream.

3.1 Maslow, Briefly

The canonical vocabulary for naming motives comes from psychologist Abraham Maslow's hierarchy of needs — five levels of human want, arranged from physiological (food, shelter, sleep) through safety (security, stability), social (belonging, love, acceptance), esteem (recognition, status, self-respect), and finally self-actualization (the realization of one's potential) (Belch & Belch, 2023). The model has been challenged on the empirical strictness of the hierarchy — people often pursue several levels at once, and the order varies by culture — but the vocabulary is durable, and it gives marketers a useful map. In developed economies, where the lower-level needs are mostly met for most consumers, marketing operates mostly in the upper three columns: belonging, esteem, and self-actualization. A consumer buying a Tudor is not buying time-telling. They are usually buying somewhere in the esteem-and-actualization range — the watch as a marker of having arrived, or as a commitment to a discerning version of self.

A second older strand of motivation theory, the psychoanalytic approach that probed unconscious motives via focus groups and projective techniques, was once influential and is now mostly absorbed into qualitative consumer research.

3.2 Same Brand, Different Motivations: The Persona Move

Maslow gives you the vocabulary, but he does not help you with the practical problem, which is that any given purchase serves different motives for different consumers, and that the same brand has to know which consumers it is speaking to in which moment. The modern operating tool for this is the persona — a synthesized portrait of a target segment, built thick enough that you can write a single sentence about what they want from your brand. Personas are how segmentation gets used. They translate demographic and behavioral abstractions into characters whose motivations a creative team can actually write for. The discipline that makes personas worth doing — rather than the cliché version that gets mocked in industry blogs — is naming a specific motivation that drives a specific journey to the same product.

The Nike example is the cleanest version of this move, because it has been running long enough to study. Consider four very different Nike customers, all of whom might walk into the same store and pick up the same shoe for entirely different reasons.

Across the four personas, what changes is the athletes and the motives; what stays is the promise. That is the practical definition of "on brand" from the segmentation-and-positioning chair: one core meaning, multiple motivational on-ramps.

Maslow gives you the vocabulary of motives. Personas give you specific consumers to write to. The brand sits in the middle, making one promise that lands as different gifts to different people.

3.3 The Live Example: Nike Reframes Its Own Tagline

Nike's September 2025 reframe of its iconic Just Do It tagline as Why Do It? is this exact tension playing out in public. The traditional Just Do It audience — the Competitor and the Identity Builder in the grid above — already has the answer to why. Nike's worry, per the campaign's own framing, is that the Newcomer segment (particularly Gen Z) does not share the assumption that the question has an obvious answer; for that consumer, motivation in a climate of "sociopolitical contention and economic pressure" is something to be reasoned into rather than commanded into (Marketing Dive, 2025). The new campaign meets that segment with the question first, then walks them to the original tagline as the answer. The brand promise is unchanged; the motivational on-ramp is calibrated for a different consumer.

Some branding observers argued the move was risky — that messing with one of the most iconic taglines in advertising history was too high a price for reaching a single segment (Lake & Lichtenberg, 2025). The argument cuts the other way too: a brand that cannot speak to where its next generation of consumers actually lives, motivationally, hardens over time into a brand for the consumers it already had. Whether the call was right is an empirical question with a five-year time horizon. The conceptual move — one promise, multiple motivational on-ramps — is the move, and it is what STP looks like in practice.

Brands as Identity Carriers

Optional enrichment · the self-concept lens on the persona move

The persona move treats motivation as the variable: same brand, different motives, calibrated speech. The harder question, especially for premium and lifestyle categories, is why does a consumer choose a brand whose functional benefit is essentially identical to its competitors'? The answer is often identity. Some brands are bought not for what they do but for what they say about the consumer who bought them. The product is a vehicle for an idealized self the consumer wants to wear in front of others, and in front of themselves.

Three brands make the point in different idioms. Patagonia sells outdoor clothing, but a substantial fraction of its sales are to consumers who do not climb anything more vertical than a parking deck; what they are buying is a wearable claim about environmental values, made unambiguous by Yvon Chouinard's 2022 transfer of the company to the Holdfast Collective, a trust dedicated to fighting climate change. Yeti built its first decade selling premium coolers to professional outdoorsmen; its second decade is pivoting toward younger urban buyers who use a $400 cooler at a tailgate, where the cooler is functionally overkill and the point is exactly that. Liquid Death, founded in 2017, reached an estimated $1.4 billion valuation by 2024 selling water — water — in skull-adorned aluminum cans under the tagline "Murder Your Thirst," which is identity work so naked that it is essentially the entire product (House of Marketers, 2025).

All three brands compete in categories where the functional difference between their product and the alternative is essentially nil. Their advantage lives almost entirely on the social and emotional pain columns from §2 — the brand is a piece of self-presentation, and the price premium pays for the identity rather than the goods. The thread back to Unit 4A is direct: 4A argued that brand meaning is half-constructed by the consumer out of their own identity. Identity-driven brands are simply the ones where that consumer half is doing the larger share of the work.

When the product is functionally identical to the alternative, the brand is selling identity, not goods.
The Enduring Idea
  • Motivation is the energy behind the gap. Two consumers with the same problem recognition will take entirely different journeys depending on why they want what they want.
  • Maslow's hierarchy — physiological, safety, social, esteem, self-actualization — gives the vocabulary. In developed economies most marketing operates in the upper three columns, because the lower needs are largely met before the journey begins.
  • The practical tool for working with motivation is the persona: a synthesized portrait of a target segment, thick enough that a creative team can write a sentence about what that consumer wants from the brand. Personas are how segmentation gets used.
  • The on-brand move when serving multiple motivations is one core promise, multiple motivational on-ramps. The Competitor, the Identity Builder, the Practical Parent, and the Newcomer can all be Nike customers without the brand becoming incoherent.
The Current View

Two shifts have made motivation work harder than it used to be. First, the rise of identity-driven consumption has pushed an increasing share of category competition out of the functional column and into the social one, where the consumer's idealized self is doing more of the buying than the product's specifications are. Patagonia, Yeti, and Liquid Death are not edge cases; they are signals that this is where premium-category competition increasingly lives. Second, brands long built on a single motivational on-ramp are now finding that the next generation of consumers needs a different one. Nike's September 2025 launch of "Why Do It?" — a deliberate inversion of its 37-year-old tagline aimed at a Gen Z that wants to be reasoned into motivation rather than commanded into it — is the live example, and it will not be the last. The persona work that §3 just walked through is not optional any more; it is how a brand stays speakable to the consumers who do not yet share its assumptions. §4 picks up the journey: once the motivation is in play, what does the consumer do to find out what to buy?

04
Search & Perception

Taking It In

The consumer is in motion. The gap is felt (§2), the motivation is in play (§3), and the journey to a purchase is now actively underway. What happens next is the part the consumer experiences as "figuring out what to buy." What is actually happening underneath is two intertwined processes: the consumer is searching for information, and their perceptual machinery is filtering that information as it comes in. Standard treatments handle these as two separate topics — search as a behavior, perception as a psychological process — but they are the same act seen from two angles; search is what the consumer is doing, while perception is what is happening to the information while they do it.

4.1 Internal First, Then External

When a gap surfaces, the first thing a consumer does is check what they already know. The standard treatments call this internal search — a scan of memory for relevant brands, past experiences, opinions formed long ago (Belch & Belch, 2023). For routine purchases, the internal search is usually enough. If the consumer has a brand they are loyal to and the situation is familiar, the journey can collapse from five stages to one or two in the time it takes to grab the package off the shelf. This is the territory of habit, which §6 covers in detail.

When the internal search comes up short — new category, high stakes, dissatisfaction with the usual choice — the consumer moves to external search, which is everything outside their head: friends, retailers, reviews, ads, websites, social posts, AI assistants. The standard treatments list these as five separate categories of source; the more useful taxonomy in modern practice is to sort them onto the PESO framework you have already met in this course: paid, owned, earned, and shared touchpoints. Same sources, sorted by who controls them — and that distinction is what determines how much trust the consumer places in what each source says.

External Search, Mapped to PESO

Owned — the brand's website, app, branded social channels, email lists, packaging. Trust is medium — the consumer expects the brand to say good things about itself but also expects the brand to be accurate about what it sells. Shared — social posts and conversations, user-generated content, community discussions, creator and influencer content. Trust is high (peer voice); reach is algorithm-mediated, which means the brand has limited control over which audiences see it.

The diagnostic insight: the modern consumer's real external search weights earned and shared sources more heavily than paid and owned. Brand claims are discounted; peer claims are not. A campaign that ignores earned and shared is, increasingly, a campaign that misses the most decision-weighted touchpoints in the journey.

Consumers don't trust brands speaking about themselves. They trust other consumers speaking about brands.

The diagnostic question for any external search is where the consumer started, because the starting channel shapes everything downstream — the alternatives that get considered, the criteria that get applied, the price the consumer expects to pay. The picture has shifted sharply in the last decade.

4.2 Perception: The Four Filters

Where the consumer searches is half the picture. What happens to the information when they get there is the other half, and it is the territory of perception. The standard treatments define perception as the process by which an individual receives, selects, organizes, and interprets information to create a meaningful picture of the world. The shorter version is the one worth holding onto: perception is selective and constructive at every stage. The consumer does not absorb the marketing world; they filter most of it out and reconstruct what is left.

Sensation is the entry point — the immediate, direct response of the senses to a stimulus (vision, hearing, touch, taste, smell). From there, four sequential filters narrow what makes it into the consumer's working knowledge.

Selective exposure is the first cut: the consumer chooses whether to be in the message's path at all. Changing the channel during commercials, scrolling past an ad, closing a pop-up, walking past an aisle — these are exposure decisions, and they happen before any "persuasion" question is on the table. Selective attention is the second cut, and the most brutal. Among the messages a consumer is exposed to, only a tiny fraction get focused on. Estimates of daily ad exposure range from 5,000 to 10,000 per consumer in a digital-saturated environment; estimates of how many actually get attended to range between 150 and 300 (Andrews & Shimp, 2018). The consumer never sees most of the message, attends to a fraction of what they see, and constructs a meaning from what is left out of materials the brand never controlled. Marketing has to plan against that math, not deny it.

4.3 Comprehension as Construction

Selective comprehension is the third filter, and it is the one that makes marketing communication so much harder than transmission. Even when a message gets exposure and earns attention, the consumer does not passively absorb it. They interpret it. The standard treatments describe this in two steps: feature analysis, where the consumer's perceptual system parses the raw signal — shape, color, words, brand mark — and active synthesis, where the consumer combines those features with context, expectations, prior knowledge, and current mood to construct a meaning (Andrews & Shimp, 2018). The same Patagonia jacket photographed against an alpine summit reads as authenticity; the same jacket photographed at a corporate gala reads as costume. The features are identical, but the synthesis is not.

Unit 4A documented the rate at which this construction goes sideways: studies of televised messages find roughly 30% miscomprehension, and even controlled ad pretests run 10–15% (Andrews & Shimp, 2018). A quarter to a third of any audience takes away something other than what was sent. This is not a fixable bug, and pretending it is leads to the worst kind of marketing self-deception. Miscomprehension is the default condition of perception working as designed — the consumer constructs meaning out of materials they bring as much as out of materials the brand provides.

Selective retention is the last filter. Of the messages that survive exposure, attention, and comprehension, most fade from memory within hours. Mnemonics, repetition, distinctive brand assets (the swoosh, the bull's-eye, the jingle), and recognizable visual systems are the marketer's principal tools for fighting retention loss. This is where the brand-building work of repetition stops being annoying and starts being economically necessary — not because consumers like repetition, but because memory leaks faster than any single message can fill it. Repetition is, in a sense, the friction-reduction play (§2) applied to the memory column: it lowers the cost of recall when the consumer needs to retrieve the brand later.

One brief note before the dual close, because the question always comes up: the subliminal-advertising debate. After more than fifty years of research, the evidence that subliminal messages influence consumer behavior at any meaningful scale is essentially nil; the popular fear is overblown, and the modern consumer-perception model has nothing to gain from it.

The Enduring Idea
  • Consumers run an internal search (memory) before an external search (the world). External sources sort onto the PESO framework — paid, owned, earned, shared — and the consumer weights earned and shared more heavily because the brand did not pay for them.
  • Perception is selective and constructive at every stage. Four sequential filters narrow what reaches working memory: selective exposure (am I in the message's path?), selective attention (am I focusing on it?), selective comprehension (what does it mean to me?), and selective retention (will I remember it?).
  • Of an estimated 5,000–10,000 daily ad exposures, roughly 150–300 actually get attended to. The attention filter is brutal; the brand's job is to be in the 3%, not the 97%.
  • Comprehension is construction, not transmission. The consumer brings half the meaning; the brand provides the other half. Miscomprehension at 25–33% of any audience is the default, not the exception.
The Current View

The shape of search has flipped twice in a decade. Amazon now leads Google as the starting point for US product searches, which has shifted the center of gravity in search engine optimization from "rank on Google" to "rank on Amazon." The next shift is already in motion: AI assistants are absorbing discovery share fast enough that the traditional survey instruments haven't caught up — Figure 4B.5's chart category for "AI search" is missing not because the channel is small but because the survey was designed before the channel mattered. Two consequences for the perceptual model. First, the selective-exposure filter now compounds with an algorithmic filter that runs before the consumer's brain ever gets the signal; the feed shows the consumer a tiny slice of what could reach them, curated for engagement rather than relevance. Second, comprehension has a new failure mode: AI-generated imagery and synthetic content mean the consumer's active-synthesis step is increasingly working from features whose underlying referent may not exist. When the audience cannot trust that an image points to anything real, the meaning they construct rests on weaker ground than it used to. §5 picks up the journey at the next stage: now that the consumer has filtered and constructed some set of meaningful options, how do they actually choose among them?

05
Deciding

Weighing & Deciding: Two Minds

By way of search and perception (§4), the consumer has built — somehow, partially, with whatever attention and bias was on hand — a set of options that count as candidates for the purchase. Now they have to choose among them. This is the stage a textbook calls alternative evaluation, and it is where the canonical model diverges most sharply from how real consumers actually decide.

The divergence has a shape. Standard treatments depict alternative evaluation as a careful, attribute-by-attribute weighing of options — the consumer as deliberative calculator, working through criteria in something like a spreadsheet. That picture is occasionally accurate, for the genuinely consequential decisions that justify the cognitive effort: cars, houses, mortgages, enterprise software, jobs. For most of the purchases people make most days, the deliberative model is closer to a flattering caricature. Real consumers reach for shortcuts; they go with what feels right, or what they bought last time, or what the algorithm pushed to the top, or the brand with the most reviews. They are not lazy or irrational, just busy — and the cognitive economics of attribute-by-attribute comparison are unforgiving when applied to forty purchase decisions a week.

This section walks both modes. The deliberative model is real, and 4C will hand back attitude-change tools that operate inside it. The shortcut-driven model is much more common, and the decision rules that govern it are the practical work of this section. The framework that holds both modes together is the CPM ↔ HEM continuum — and the diagnostic question for any campaign is not "which model applies?" but "where on the continuum does this particular purchase sit?"

5.1 The Consideration Set

Before a comparison can happen, the consumer has to assemble a set of options to compare, and that set is much smaller than the universe of available brands. The standard treatments call this the evoked set (or consideration set) — the brands the consumer actively considers when the gap surfaces (Belch & Belch, 2023). For most categories the evoked set runs three to seven brands. Anything beyond seven exceeds working memory; anything below three usually means the consumer is sliding into habit (§6) and skipping comparison altogether.

Two related sets matter. The inept set is brands the consumer is aware of and actively rejects; once rejected, those brands tend to stay rejected, because a brand has to overcome an existing negative attitude rather than build a new one from scratch. The inert set is brands the consumer is aware of but indifferent to — not rejected, just not considered. From the marketer's chair the inert set is the most frustrating, because invisibility is hard to diagnose; the consumer is not actively saying "no," they are simply not saying anything at all.

The single most important consequence of all this is that getting into the evoked set is what awareness marketing is for. Once you're in, you're in the comparison; once you're out, you don't exist for that purchase, no matter how good your product is. The smaller and quicker the decision, the more it favors whatever brand is automatically retrieved — which is why top-of-mind awareness, the brand that comes up first when a consumer thinks of the category, is worth so much in habitual categories. The consumer's evoked set for "I need a soda" usually has one brand in it.

5.2 Evaluative Criteria

Inside the consideration set, the consumer compares options on evaluative criteria — the attributes that matter to this consumer in this purchase. The criteria differ from consumer to consumer (the four Nike personas from §3 are a clean illustration: the Competitor weights performance specs; the Identity Builder weights cultural cool; the Practical Parent weights durability per dollar; the Newcomer weights low-stakes encouragement). They also differ by purchase (price weighs more when the budget is tight) and by category (latency matters for a database; not for a candle).

One under-appreciated lever lives here. Marketers can sometimes shift which criteria the consumer uses to compare. Apple spent two decades convincing consumers that ecosystem integration was a criterion in laptop choice — before the iPhone era, nobody bought laptops on ecosystem. Tesla performed the same trick with acceleration, shifting it from a sports-car criterion to a daily-driver one. Patagonia did the move with supply-chain transparency in outdoor apparel. Changing the criteria of comparison is, in a sense, more powerful than winning on existing criteria, because it reframes the comparison itself in your favor. The competitor whose entire positioning is built on an attribute that the consumer no longer thinks is the right attribute is in serious trouble before they realize anything has changed.

5.3 Decision Rules

Once the criteria are in play, the consumer needs a rule for combining them into an actual decision. Five rules cover most of consumer behavior. The first — affect referral — is technically not a comparison at all; the rest split into compensatory (tradeoffs allowed) and noncompensatory (tradeoffs not allowed). Real consumers rarely use just one rule; the most common pattern is a noncompensatory rule first (eliminate options that fail a hard minimum or rank poorly on the top criterion), then a compensatory rule or affect referral among the survivors.

5.4 Two Minds: The CPM ↔ HEM Continuum

Five rules is more than enough to describe how any one decision plays out. The deeper question is which mode of decision-making is operating in the first place. Consumer-behavior research distinguishes two modes, which map loosely onto Kahneman's well-known System 2 / System 1 distinction.

The Consumer Processing Model (CPM) treats the consumer as deliberative: gathering information, comparing options, evaluating attributes, and choosing the optimum. CPM-leaning decisions look like the deliberative spreadsheet — slow, effortful, and most common in genuinely consequential purchases. Cars, software contracts, mortgages, insurance, the company you take a job at.

The Hedonic Experiential Model (HEM) treats the consumer as feeling-driven: experiencing brands rather than evaluating them, choosing based on resonance, mood, identity fit, and the emotional quality of the brand contact. HEM-leaning decisions look like the affect-referral rule. The consumer does not compare; they recognize. HEM dominates purchases where the experience is the point — perfume, art, vacations, fashion, the restaurant you pick for an anniversary.

The most important thing to say about CPM and HEM is that they are not two separate camps, even though most introductory treatments present them that way, but the ends of a continuum — and almost every purchase sits somewhere between them, leaning one way.

5.5 The 60/40 Mix: What the Continuum Means for the Budget

The CPM ↔ HEM continuum has a practical payoff that explains a great deal of seemingly irrational marketing spend. In 2013, Les Binet and Peter Field published The Long and the Short of It, a landmark analysis of the IPA Databank of 996 advertising-effectiveness case studies covering 700 brands across 83 sectors and more than thirty years of measurement (Binet & Field, 2013). Their central finding has become marketing's most replicated piece of empirical wisdom: across categories, the most effective marketing programs split investment roughly 60% to brand-building and 40% to sales activation. The optimum varies somewhat by category (online retail closer to 74/26; direct-response-heavy categories closer to 46/54), but the center of gravity holds.

Brand-building runs on HEM principles — emotional, broad-reach, long-term — and creates the affect-referral conditions that win future decisions. Sales activation runs on CPM principles — rational, targeted, short-term — and captures demand that already exists. The 60/40 finding is, in effect, the data answer to a question marketing organizations argue about constantly: how much money should go into work that doesn't show up on this quarter's dashboard? About 60% of it, give or take.

The mix is the explanation for two persistent disputes in marketing departments. Why does brand-building feel like waste to the CFO? Because it operates in the HEM column, on the affect-referral side, on a long time horizon. Its returns show up in decisions that wouldn't have happened otherwise, six months to several years later. Performance dashboards don't measure that, which is why brand-building is consistently underfunded relative to its actual ROI. Why do brand marketers complain about ROAS metrics? Because Return on Ad Spend measures only the 40% — the activation side. It captures conversion events the activation campaign produced and ignores the brand investment that built the affect-referral that made the conversion easy. A brand with strong affect referral converts cheaply on activation; the activation campaign gets credit for the brand's work.

The diagnostic insight for any marketing organization: if you can't tell the CFO what percentage of your spend is brand-building versus activation, you don't yet know what kind of marketing you are doing.

5.6 Attitude Structure

The last conceptual piece for this section is the structure of consumer attitudes — how attitudes are organized in the consumer's head, before any question of how to change them (which belongs to Module 3). The standard treatment is the tripartite model (Belch & Belch, 2023):

  • Cognitive — the beliefs the consumer holds about the brand. "This brand makes durable products. This brand is expensive." Beliefs are factual claims, true or false, that the consumer treats as known.
  • Affective — the feelings the consumer has toward the brand. "I like this brand. I trust it. It makes me feel competent." Affect is what's left when you strip beliefs of their truth value and keep the valence.
  • Conative — the consumer's intent or behavioral tendency. "I plan to buy it next time. I would recommend it. I would defend it in an argument." Conation is the action-readiness dimension.

The three components are usually consistent with each other but not always. A consumer can believe a brand is high-quality (cognitive positive), dislike its marketing (affective negative), and still buy it (conative positive). The interesting marketing problems live exactly where the three components don't align.

The second thing to know about attitudes is that the order in which the three components form depends on involvement — how much the consumer cares about the decision.

High-Involvement (CPM-leaning)
Think → Feel → Do

The low-involvement hierarchy is the one most marketers underweight. Trial generates belief; belief does not always precede trial. This is why sampling, free trials, and "first month free" subscriptions work — they short-circuit the high-involvement hierarchy and let the consumer skip directly to use, generating the affect and cognition retroactively.

Two pieces of attitude theory are deliberately deferred to Module 3 and deserve naming so you know they're coming. The attitude equation — the Fishbein multi-attribute model that calculates attitudes from belief-by-evaluation pairs — and the attitude-change strategies — the levers a marketer pulls to shift consumer attitudes by adding beliefs, changing weights, or reframing the comparison — are persuasion topics, and they belong with the Elaboration Likelihood Model that 4C will unpack. Behavioral Economics as Choice Architecture

Optional enrichment · the M3 bridge, named here so you can see it coming

The Kahneman / Tversky / Thaler tradition has, over the last fifty years, accumulated a rich vocabulary for the systematic ways consumers depart from the deliberative-calculator model. The vocabulary has become so common in modern marketing — and so essential to the M3 persuasion module — that it deserves naming here, even though we will not unpack the persuasion side until next module. Six durable biases show up most often.

Loss aversion. Losses feel roughly twice as bad as equivalent gains feel good. Framing a decision around what the consumer stands to lose (rather than gain) usually moves more behavior.

Defaults and status-quo bias. Whatever option is preselected wins, by a wide margin. The single most powerful design lever in any signup flow is the default; changing organ-donor consent from opt-in to opt-out can move national donation rates by tens of percentage points.

Anchoring. The first number a consumer encounters distorts everything that follows. Listing a product at $200 strikethrough next to $150 makes the $150 feel like a bargain even when $150 is the standard price.

Framing. The same facts presented differently produce different choices. "90% survival rate" and "10% mortality rate" are arithmetically identical and behaviorally not.

Social proof. What the consumer believes other consumers are doing disproportionately shapes what they do. Reviews, ratings, "bestseller" tags, and "10,000 customers chose this" badges all work on social proof.

Mental accounting. Money in different "buckets" is treated as different money. A $50 gift card spends easier than $50 in checking. A $5 daily impulse purchase feels manageable; the same $1,800 annual total, felt directly, would not.

One honest caveat: behavioral economics is mostly durable, but the literature has gotten more skeptical of some specific findings post-replication-crisis. Loss aversion, defaults, anchoring, framing, social proof, and mental accounting are well-supported. The decoy effect and some priming-adjacent biases are more contested. When you reach for these tools, lean on the well-replicated ones.

The bridge into Module 3 is that each of these biases is a peripheral-route persuasion lever wearing different clothes. The Elaboration Likelihood Model, which 4C will unpack in detail, distinguishes two routes by which persuasion can succeed — the central route (the consumer thinks carefully about strong arguments) and the peripheral route (the consumer responds to cues that do not require thinking). The biases catalogued above are the peripheral route's tool kit. By the time you reach 4C, you will already know the levers; what M3 adds is the framework for choosing among them honestly.

These are not tricks. They are how consumers actually decide, which means they are how marketing actually works.
The Enduring Idea
  • Consumers compare options inside an evoked set of three to seven brands. Getting into the evoked set is what awareness marketing is for; once you are out, you do not exist for that purchase.
  • Inside the evoked set, comparison runs on evaluative criteria that vary by persona, situation, and category. Sometimes the criteria themselves are the lever — changing what counts as a criterion is more powerful than winning on the existing ones.
  • Five decision rules cover most consumer behavior: affect referral, compensatory, conjunctive, disjunctive, lexicographic. Real consumers usually combine them — noncompensatory rules first to narrow, then compensatory or affect referral to choose.
  • The mode of decision-making lives on a CPM ↔ HEM continuum. Most purchases sit somewhere between, leaning one way; the diagnostic question is always which way this purchase leans.
  • The Binet & Field 60/40 rule is the practical payoff of the continuum: roughly 60% of marketing budget should build the brand (HEM/long-term), 40% should activate sales (CPM/short-term).
  • Attitudes have three components — cognitive, affective, conative — that form in different orders depending on involvement. High-involvement: think → feel → do. Low-involvement: do → feel → think.
The Current View

Two practical reframes are driving most of the modern conversation about how consumers decide. First, the 60/40 brand/activation mix has crystallized into a working orthodoxy across categories, and the marketing organizations that consistently outperform are the ones who fund the 60% even when the dashboard rewards only the 40%. Second, behavioral economics has reframed many decision rules as choice architecture — design choices made by the marketer (or the platform, or the government, or the algorithm) that shape consumer behavior without changing the consumer's underlying preferences. The biases catalogued in the sidebar are not psychological quirks to exploit; they are the actual machinery of decision-making at scale, and Module 3 will unpack how a marketer chooses among them ethically. There is also a third shift that does not yet have a name: AI summarization is starting to replace compensatory reasoning for a meaningful share of consumers. When ChatGPT or a retail-site AI assistant tells the consumer "the best option for your situation is X, because Y," the consumer is outsourcing the multi-attribute weighing that the canonical model assumes they will do themselves. The decision rule is still compensatory; it is just running on rented cognition. We will not know for several years what that does to consumer behavior at scale. §6 picks up the journey at the next stage: now that we have seen how the consumer decides, how do those decisions get learned and remembered, so that future decisions get cheaper to make?

Learning

Learning, Memory & Habit

The consumer has made a decision — they bought the thing, or they didn't. The journey from gap to purchase, walked over §§2–5, ends in an experience — the actual or imagined use of the product. What happens after that experience is the territory of this section, and it is where the long-term economics of marketing actually live.

Most decisions a consumer makes are not standalone events; they are inputs to future decisions. The experience gets learned, the learning gets remembered, the memory gets retrieved at the next problem-recognition, and the journey skips half its stages because the consumer already knows the answer. This is what brand loyalty is, in mechanical terms. It is also why the cost of acquiring an existing customer's next purchase is so much lower than the cost of acquiring a new customer's first purchase — most of the work was done by memory, not marketing. §6 covers the machinery underneath: how consumers learn, how they remember, and how learning plus memory plus repetition produce the habit loops that the modern subscription economy is built on.

6.1 Two Ways Consumers Learn

Consumer learning takes two broadly different routes, and the marketing relevant to each is genuinely different. Behavioral learning is learning by association — connections built between stimuli and responses through repeated pairing or reinforcement. The mechanism is older than language; humans, dogs, octopuses, and infants all learn this way. The classical work is Pavlov's (dog hears bell, gets food, eventually salivates at the bell alone) and Skinner's (rat presses lever, gets pellet, eventually presses the lever a lot). Neither psychologist had marketing in mind, though modern marketing applies their findings constantly.

Cognitive learning is learning by understanding — building, refining, and reorganizing networks of conceptual associations (called knowledge structures) in memory. The mechanism is later, more language-mediated, and characteristically human. A consumer who learns that "Patagonia makes durable jackets, sources responsibly, and was transferred to a climate trust by its founder" is doing cognitive learning — while the same consumer reaching for Patagonia without thinking, after a decade of that learning has consolidated, is doing the behavioral version.

Real consumer learning blends both. The distinction matters because the marketing levers are different. Behavioral learning is moved by pairing (associating the brand with positive emotion through music, athletes, scenery) and reinforcement (rewarding the purchase with a good experience). Cognitive learning is moved by argument, demonstration, and information design (which is mostly what §4 was about).

6.2 Behavioral Learning: Three Mechanisms

Classical conditioning is the original Pavlovian mechanism, adapted for brand work: pair an unconditioned stimulus (something that already evokes a positive response — emotional music, an attractive setting, a beloved athlete) with the conditioned stimulus (the brand). Repeat the pairing long enough, and the brand alone evokes the response. Most affective brand-building works through some version of this. The reason a Hallmark ad gets paired with images of weddings, family reunions, and reunions of estranged siblings is not that Hallmark sells those events but that the brand wants those emotions retrieved when the consumer thinks "Hallmark." (Andrews & Shimp, 2018) treat this mechanism in detail as the cornerstone of emotional brand-building.

A note on what is not in this section

Classical conditioning is the place to flag where this mechanism goes next in the course. The Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM) that 4C unpacks distinguishes two routes by which persuasion succeeds — the central route (consumer engages carefully with strong arguments) and the peripheral route (consumer responds to cues without effortful thinking). Classical conditioning is the peripheral route by another name. We have taught the mechanism here in 4B; 4C will pick it up and put it inside the larger persuasion framework, alongside the behavioral-economics biases from §5.

Operant (instrumental) conditioning is what B.F. Skinner studied with rats and pigeons: behavior followed by reward strengthens the behavior; behavior followed by punishment (or no reward) weakens it. The consumer version is the most consequential mechanism in this section. A purchase followed by a good product experience reinforces the brand choice; a purchase followed by a disappointing experience extinguishes it. Reinforcement schedules matter — the most stubborn behaviors are produced by variable-ratio reinforcement, where the reward shows up unpredictably. This is the same schedule that makes slot machines and infinite-scroll social feeds work, and we will come back to it in the modern sidebar below.

Vicarious learning is Albert Bandura's contribution to the field: humans don't have to be directly reinforced to learn from reinforcement. We can watch other people get rewarded and update our own behavior accordingly. This is the mechanism underneath influencer marketing, user-generated content, testimonials, and most word-of-mouth. The consumer doesn't have to try the product to know what it's like to enjoy it; they watch a creator enjoy it on TikTok, see the creator's audience respond, and have learned vicariously what would happen if they tried.

6.3 Cognitive Learning and Knowledge Structures

Cognitive learning is what builds the consumer's mental model of a brand. The conceptual unit researchers use here is the knowledge structure — a network of nodes (each representing a piece of information the consumer holds about the brand) connected by associative links (each representing a learned relationship between two pieces of information). The structure is what gets retrieved when the consumer thinks of the brand.

A standard textbook example is the Volkswagen Beetle — a brand whose long cultural history has built unusually rich associations in many consumers' memories (Andrews & Shimp, 2018).

Schemas are a related concept: pattern-templates the consumer has for whole categories of thing, not just individual brands. The consumer has a schema for "luxury car," "fast-food restaurant," "trustworthy financial advisor." A new brand entering a category has to either fit the existing schema (and benefit from the consumer's expectations) or actively reshape the schema (which is harder and slower, but produces larger long-term effects when it works). Tesla did the second move in automobiles; Liquid Death (§3) is doing the second move in bottled water.

6.4 Memory and the Habit Loop

Memory is the bridge between learning and behavior. A knowledge structure that cannot be retrieved when the gap surfaces is, for marketing purposes, equivalent to a structure that doesn't exist. The standard model has three stages: sensory memory (a fraction of a second of raw signal, relevant to perception in §4), working memory (the few seconds of active attention), and long-term memory (everything else, organized in the knowledge structures we just walked through).

Two retrieval tasks matter to the marketer. Recognition is when, given the brand, the consumer remembers having heard of it. This is what package design, in-store displays, and most retail-shelf competition rely on; the consumer doesn't need to recall the brand from scratch, just confirm it when they see it. Recall is harder — given only the category, the consumer retrieves the brand without prompting. Recall is what top-of-mind awareness is, and it is what wins the habitual purchase. The consumer who thinks "I want a soda" and retrieves one brand has already made the decision; everyone else is competing for the runner-up slot.

The economic payoff of recall lives in the habit loop. The contemporary popular framing — best known from Charles Duhigg's The Power of Habit (2012) and James Clear's Atomic Habits (2018), but rooted in older operant-conditioning research — describes habit as a three-part cycle.

Brand loyalty, in mechanical terms, is a well-installed habit loop: the cue (gap surfaces — the consumer is thirsty, say) retrieves the routine (buy Coke) without competition from other options, because the reward (refreshment plus the familiar brand experience) reliably followed in the past. This is why brand-building work that looks irrational from the short-term-ROAS chair is actually building the cue-to-routine link that wins all the future habitual purchases. It is also why disrupting an established brand habit is so hard. The loop is automated, the routine is the path of least cognitive resistance, and the new brand has to either present a moment of conscious deliberation (which the consumer would rather avoid) or insert itself into the cue position somehow — which is what placement, distribution, retail prominence, and aggressive trial offers are designed to do.

Brand loyalty is not a feeling. It is a well-installed habit loop, where the cue retrieves the routine without competition, because the reward reliably followed in the past.

Subscription & App Habit Loops: The Modern Frontier

Where operant conditioning meets product design — and where the ethical edge lives

The most aggressive applications of the habit loop in the last fifteen years live in software products, not consumer-goods brands. The subscription economy and the mobile-app economy have both internalized the cue-routine-reward framework so thoroughly that "engagement design" has become its own discipline. Three patterns dominate.

Engineered cues. Push notifications, email digests, and timed in-app prompts are designed cues — situational triggers placed in the consumer's day to start the routine. Duolingo's evening reminder ("Don't break your streak!") is a cue engineered to fire when the consumer is most likely to have a few minutes and least likely to feel busy. Snapchat's streak-warning notifications work the same way among teens. Headspace's morning reminder, Calm's bedtime nudge, and a thousand fitness-app cues all run the same play.

Variable-ratio rewards. The TikTok feed, the Instagram explore page, the slot-machine-like pull-to-refresh on Twitter — these are all variable-ratio reinforcement schedules in modern UI clothes. The user does not know which scroll will produce the rewarding video, only that some scroll will. The unpredictability is what makes the loop addictive; it is the same mechanism Skinner identified in pigeons, applied to a billion smartphones.

Streaks and social proof as the reinforcer. When the reward is not the experience itself but the count of having done it, the loop becomes about preserving the streak rather than enjoying the underlying activity. Duolingo's green owl, Snapchat's flame, and the workout-app "consecutive days" counter all use this. The consumer becomes loss-averse around the streak (callback to §5's loss-aversion bias) rather than gain-motivated by the activity itself. The brand harvests the loyalty either way.

The cross-functional payoff for the non-marketers in this room is right here. The same loop powers the language-learning app, the fitness tracker, the project-management tool, the team-chat platform, and the operational dashboard. Operations and HR people who learn to read the cue-routine-reward structure in their own internal systems can diagnose adoption failures the same way: What's the cue, and is it actually firing for the user we're trying to reach? What's the routine cost, and is the reward worth it? Is there a streak or social-proof loop reinforcing continued use, or are we depending on the user's intrinsic motivation — which is the weakest reinforcement schedule there is?

There is also an ethical question here that this module has danced around, and that 4C will name directly. The line between a humane habit loop (one that helps the consumer do something they actually wanted to do) and a compulsive one (one that exploits the same machinery against the consumer's interest) is real, and contested. Tristan Harris and the Center for Humane Technology have argued for over a decade that much of mobile-app design has crossed it; others argue that consumers know what they are doing and the design is just helping them do it. The marketing model does not tell you where the line is; it tells you how the loop works, and asks you to make your own decision about which side of it the product you are building actually sits on.

When you design the cue, the routine, and the reward, you have designed the habit. The ethical question is whose interest the habit serves.
The Enduring Idea
  • Consumer learning takes two routes. Behavioral learning runs on pairing (classical), reinforcement (operant), and observation of others (vicarious). Cognitive learning runs on understanding — building and reshaping knowledge structures of associated nodes.
  • A knowledge structure is the network of associations the consumer has learned for a brand. Marketing's primary cognitive work is to strengthen specific links (so that the right association comes up first), add new nodes where useful, and weaken outdated or harmful ones.
  • Memory has two retrieval tasks of marketing significance. Recognition is confirming a known brand when prompted (what package design and shelf competition rely on). Recall is retrieving the brand from a category cue without prompting (what top-of-mind awareness is, and what wins habitual purchases).
  • The habit loop — cue → routine → reward — is the mechanical structure of brand loyalty. A well-installed loop collapses the decision journey from five stages to one. This is the long-term economic prize of all marketing.
The Current View

The habit loop has become the dominant design pattern in the software products consumers spend most of their time inside, and the explicit engineering of cues, routines, and rewards has changed the relationship between brands and the consumers they reach. Streaks, push notifications, variable-reward feeds, and gamified counters are deliberate applications of operant conditioning at a scale and precision that previous generations of marketers could not have imagined. The subscription economy is, in part, the financial wrapper around an engineered habit loop — the brand charges monthly because the routine has become automatic, and the consumer pays for what they would have done anyway. The opportunity and the risk are the same thing: a brand that can build a humane, useful habit loop owns the next decade of that consumer's category spend; a brand that builds a compulsion loop wins the next quarter and earns a regulatory or reputational reckoning in the quarters after. The ethical and the strategic are not separable here; they are aspects of the same design choice. §7 picks up the journey at the final stage: what happens after the purchase, in the post-purchase evaluation and the external influences that shape the next gap recognition.

After & Around

After & Around the Decision

Almost everything in this module so far has worked from the inside out — the consumer's gap, the consumer's motivation, the consumer's perception, the consumer's choice, the consumer's learning. The journey looked like a private event happening inside one person's head, but it is not, and never was. Two things have been on the periphery of every section: what happens after the consumer acts, and the social and situational world the consumer was always inside while they were acting. §7 brings both into view, because both are where the next gap recognition gets written.

The structure is straightforward. Post-purchase evaluation is the closing stage of the five-stage decision process, and it is the single most consequential stage for everything that comes after — brand loyalty, word-of-mouth, repurchase, the inept set. External influences are the contextual forces that shaped every stage from problem recognition onward: culture, subculture, social class, reference groups, situation. A textbook treats them as a separate topic. They are better understood as the segmentation bases (STP) that have been running underneath the whole module, finally surfaced.

7.1 Postpurchase Evaluation: Satisfaction and Dissatisfaction

The consumer bought the thing. Now they use it, or experience it, or live with it, and they compare the actual experience to what they expected. A textbook frames this through expectancy-disconfirmation theory (Belch & Belch, 2023): satisfaction is not driven by absolute product quality so much as by the gap between expectation and experience. A mediocre product that overdelivers against low expectations produces satisfaction; an excellent product that underdelivers against inflated expectations produces dissatisfaction. The framing has two practical consequences that most marketing organizations underweight.

First, expectations are themselves a marketing output. The advertising the consumer saw before purchase set the expectation that the experience is now being measured against. Overpromising in pre-purchase communication is the most common single cause of post-purchase dissatisfaction, and it usually happens because the people writing the ads do not bear the cost of the disappointment those ads produce. Second, the satisfaction window is the source of all the most valuable consumer behavior the marketer wants — repeat purchase, advocacy, recommendation, reviews, ongoing loyalty — and most of it gets shaped in the first hours and days after the purchase, while the consumer is still in the mode of deciding whether they were right to buy it.

7.2 Cognitive Dissonance: When the Consumer Argues With Themselves

That decision is rarely cleanly settled by the experience. The richer mechanism is cognitive dissonance, named by Leon Festinger in 1957 and applied to consumer behavior ever since — the psychological discomfort that arises when two beliefs (or a belief and a behavior) conflict. The consumer bought the product, which represents an action; they hold beliefs about the alternatives they didn't choose, about money, about their judgment as a buyer. Whenever the action and the beliefs are even slightly out of alignment, the consumer feels the discomfort, and the discomfort motivates resolution. Post-purchase dissonance (popularly: buyer's remorse) is this mechanism running on a fresh purchase.

Cognitive dissonance is strongest under the conditions a textbook calls the three Ps: Price (the purchase was expensive enough to feel committing), Permanence (the purchase is hard to reverse), and Personal Importance (the purchase reflects on the buyer's identity or judgment). A new car triggers all three; a pack of gum triggers none. This is why dissonance research has historically focused on big-ticket categories — cars, appliances, houses, technology — even though the mechanism runs everywhere at some intensity.

The consumer has four broad routes to resolve the discomfort.

Confirm the choice
Re-read positive reviews, seek out brand content, talk to other buyers, focus on what the product does well.
Dismiss the alternatives
Discount the brands that weren't chosen ("they had bad warranty," "the reviewer was biased"), reduce comparison anxiety retroactively.
Change the belief
Reduce the weight previously placed on attributes the chosen product doesn't excel at; promote attributes it does excel at.
Return or replace
If resolution fails, undo the decision. Roughly 40% of consumer returns are driven by post-purchase regret, not product defect.

For the marketer, the discomfort window is also the intervention window. The most underused tool in most marketing organizations is the deliberate use of post-purchase communications to reduce dissonance: thank-you emails that congratulate the consumer on their decision; onboarding content that surfaces features they will love; user-community access that shows them other satisfied buyers; product-tip emails that increase competent use. The discipline calls this post-purchase reassurance, and the research is unambiguous about its return — consumers reassured immediately after purchase complete more repeat purchases, leave more positive reviews, refer more friends, and return fewer items (Golden Seller, 2025). Reviews from those satisfied buyers are then the input to the next consumer's pre-purchase confidence: around 70% of consumers consult reviews before unfamiliar purchases, and conversion rates rise sharply with the first five reviews (Capital One Shopping, 2026). The loop is direct: reassure the current buyer; the current buyer makes the next buyer's decision easier.

7.3 External Influences: The World the Consumer Was Always Inside

This entire module has been written as if the consumer were thinking alone. They never were. Five categories of external influence sat under every stage, from problem recognition through post-purchase, and shaped what was even possible to consider at each step. A standard textbook treatment arranges them as an inverted pyramid, broadest at the top, narrowing toward the individual purchase — the wider the layer, the more diffuse and slow-moving its influence, but the deeper its reach (Belch & Belch, 2023).

Each layer is a different segmentation lever. Culture-level segmentation is what global brands do when they decide whether to localize for the Japanese market. Subcultural segmentation is what made Latino-targeted campaigns a $3B+ category in US advertising. The STP payoff is exactly here: this pyramid is the menu of segmentation bases the entire course returns to. Demographic segmentation lives mostly in the subculture and social-class tiers. Psychographic and behavioral segmentation pull from reference groups and situation. Cultural segmentation is what most international expansion has to solve. There is no neutral consumer; every consumer sits at a specific intersection of these layers, and the marketer's first strategic question is always which intersections to address.

A textbook treats post-purchase and external influences as separate topics. They are better understood as the two sides of the same insight: no decision is ever made alone, and no decision is ever fully over.

7.4 Reference Groups and the Modern Peer Voice

Reference groups deserve a second pass, because they are doing more work in the modern decision journey than a textbook treatment alone suggests. The canonical model defines a reference group as any group of people whose values, norms, or behaviors influence the consumer's own — primary (family, close friends) and secondary (professional associations, online communities), aspirational (groups the consumer wants to join) and dissociative (groups the consumer wants to be seen as not belonging to). The 2025 Edelman Trust Barometer found that 80% of consumers trust the brands they personally use — a higher trust level than they extend to business, media, government, or NGOs — and that what they trust most when forming opinions about brands they haven't used yet is their own peers and trusted creators, not advertising (Edelman, 2025).

This is what makes earned and shared touchpoints (the right side of PESO from §4) dominate the modern external-search step. The reference group has expanded; what used to be three to five friends is now a long-tail network of trusted creators, online communities, review platforms, and AI-summarized peer sentiment. The brand's job has not changed conceptually — get the right reference group talking favorably — but the tools and the speed have. A single TikTok creator with audience-trust can move a category in a week.

Reviews and UGC as the Two-Way Mirror

Where post-purchase and external influence collapse into one mechanism

The cleanest current illustration of how post-purchase evaluation and external influences interact is the review, and its larger family of user-generated content (UGC). A review is post-purchase behavior — the consumer is processing their own satisfaction, often resolving residual dissonance by articulating why the purchase was the right one. A review is also, simultaneously, an external influence on the next consumer — an earned-touchpoint peer voice that bypasses brand control and weighs heavily in the new consumer's pre-purchase search.

The numbers are stark. Between 90% and 97% of consumers read reviews before purchasing, depending on the survey; conversion rates climb sharply with the first five reviews displayed, and luxury-category conversion can rise as much as 380% on heavily-reviewed pages (Capital One Shopping, 2026). Brands with strong review programs are running two systems at once: a dissonance-reduction system for current buyers (which produces the reviews) and a pre-purchase reassurance system for next buyers (which uses them). The same artifact does both jobs.

UGC generalizes the move. A creator's video unboxing a product, a customer's Instagram photo wearing it, a Reddit thread discussing it — all of these are post-purchase behavior from one consumer that becomes external influence on another. The fastest-growing brands of the last five years — Liquid Death, Stanley, Owala, Glossier — have built their pre-purchase machine almost entirely on the post-purchase behavior of earlier buyers. The brand orchestrates the conditions; the consumers do most of the marketing.

One more bridge to flag for next module. Edelman's 2025 report names a shift in this space: search engine optimization (SEO) is increasingly less important than what they call generative engine optimization (GEO) — influencing what AI summaries say about a brand when consumers ask ChatGPT or another assistant. 91% of generative-AI users now use these tools for shopping in some way (Edelman, 2025, p. 10). The AI is summarizing the reviews and the UGC and the press, and the consumer is reading the summary. The reference-group voice has not gone away; it has been algorithmically pre-digested before it reaches the consumer.

A review does two jobs. It resolves the writer's dissonance and it shapes the next consumer's decision. The brand that designs for both wins both.
The Enduring Idea
  • Postpurchase evaluation runs on expectancy-disconfirmation: satisfaction comes from the gap between expectation and experience, not from absolute quality. Overpromising in advertising is the most common single cause of postpurchase dissatisfaction, because pre-purchase expectations are themselves a marketing output.
  • Cognitive dissonance — the psychological discomfort of misaligned beliefs and actions — runs strongest where the purchase scores high on the three Ps: price, permanence, personal importance. Consumers resolve it by confirming the choice, dismissing alternatives, changing belief weights, or returning the product. The most underused marketing tool is deliberate postpurchase reassurance.
  • The consumer's external context is layered as an inverted pyramid: culture → subculture → social class → reference groups → situation. Broader, slower-moving, and more diffuse at the top; more specific, faster, and more behaviorally direct at the bottom.
  • The same pyramid is the master inventory of segmentation bases. There is no neutral consumer. Every consumer sits at a specific intersection of these layers, and the first strategic question in marketing is always which intersections to address.
  • Reference groups are doing more work in the modern decision journey than ever before. Peers and trusted creators have replaced advertising as the dominant source of pre-purchase confidence, which is why earned and shared touchpoints dominate.
The Current View

Three shifts are reshaping the after-and-around territory of the journey. First, the review economy has formalized post-purchase behavior into an addressable touchpoint — between 90% and 97% of consumers consult reviews before buying, and the first five reviews on a product page move conversion more than any other single design element (Capital One Shopping, 2026). Second, the peer voice has eclipsed brand voice: the 2025 Edelman Trust Barometer found that 80% of consumers trust the brands they use, but the trust they extend to brands they don't yet use is mediated almost entirely by peers, creators, and earned third-party signals rather than advertising. The strategic implication is that brand-marketing budgets that don't fund the conditions under which peers will speak well are funding only half the necessary work. Third, and most consequential, generative AI is becoming the aggregator of all of this: 91% of AI users now use these tools for shopping, and the AI's summary of what peers, reviewers, and reporters have said is what the consumer actually reads (Edelman, 2025). Edelman calls the new discipline generative engine optimization (GEO) — influencing what the AI says when the consumer asks. The shift from SEO to GEO is the same external-influence machinery from 1980s-era textbooks, run through a new layer of mediation. The reference group has not gone away; it has been compiled into a paragraph the consumer reads in three seconds, and the brand that doesn't show up well in that paragraph doesn't get considered.

The Spine

Five stages: problem recognition → search → evaluation → purchase → postpurchase. A map, not a script. Same diagram as the marketing funnel from the other chair.

§2
The Gap

Every journey starts with a felt difference between actual and ideal. Pain points sort to functional, emotional, social, with friction as the structurally separate barrier between recognition and action.

§3
The Energy

Motivation gives the gap force. Maslow names the vocabulary; personas make it operational. The on-brand move: one promise, multiple motivational on-ramps.

§4
The Intake

Internal search before external. External search mapped to PESO. Perception filters in four sequential cuts — exposure, attention, comprehension, retention — and comprehension is constructed, not received.

§5
The Choice

Evoked set → criteria → decision rules. CPM ↔ HEM as the continuum. 60/40 as the budget payoff. Attitude structure: cognitive, affective, conative; high-involvement think-feel-do vs low-involvement do-feel-think.

§6
The Memory

Learning is behavioral (pairing, reinforcement, observation) and cognitive (knowledge structures). Memory enables retrieval; retrieval enables habit; the cue–routine–reward loop is brand loyalty's mechanics.

§7
The After & Around

Expectancy-disconfirmation drives satisfaction. Dissonance resolves through reassurance or return. External influences — culture, subculture, social class, reference groups, situation — are the master inventory of segmentation bases.

Together: the consumer recognizes a gap, finds energy to close it, takes in information, weighs options, decides, learns from the result, and lives inside a social and situational world that has been shaping every stage. Marketing is the discipline of being useful to that journey at the right stage with the right argument.

The seven sections are one model. The vocabulary of this module is a diagnostic kit: when a purchase isn't happening, point at the stage where the journey broke and at the process running underneath it that needs work.

The synthesis worth carrying into 4C is this: 4A taught how a message becomes meaning. 4B taught what consumers then do with the meaning. 4C is going to teach how to change what the meaning makes them do. The diagnostic from 4A — where in the communication is this breaking down? — was a question about transmission. The diagnostic from 4B — where in the journey is this consumer, and what process underneath needs work? — is a question about behavior. The diagnostic in 4C will be about influence: by what route, central or peripheral, are we trying to change this consumer's attitude, and what does that route demand of the argument we make? Three modules, three diagnostics, one accumulating toolkit.

What You Should Be Able to Do Now
  • Walk the five-stage decision process for a real purchase, naming what is happening at each stage and what could break the journey at that point.
  • Sort a pain point into functional, emotional, social, or friction, and explain which kind of marketing or product intervention is appropriate to that column — including the move where the marketer plants the gap rather than the consumer finding it.
  • Build a persona for a target segment that names a specific motivation, maps to a Maslow level, and supports a single sentence about what that consumer wants from the brand — and explain why multiple personas under one umbrella promise is the on-brand pattern, not brand drift.
  • Map a brand's external-search touchpoints onto PESO and identify the trust-and-reach tradeoffs at each, plus diagnose where in the four selective-perception filters (exposure, attention, comprehension, retention) a campaign is most likely to leak.
  • Diagnose which decision rule a consumer is likely using in a given category (affect referral, compensatory, conjunctive, disjunctive, lexicographic) — and where on the CPM ↔ HEM continuum the purchase sits, with an implication for the brand-building / sales-activation budget mix.
  • Explain why brand-building is consistently underfunded by reference to the 60/40 rule, the activation-side bias of ROAS, and the affect-referral dynamic that brand-building is actually paying for.
  • Sketch a knowledge structure for a familiar brand — central node, primary associations, secondaries — and identify which links a current campaign is trying to strengthen, weaken, or add.
  • Identify the cue, routine, and reward in an existing brand habit loop (consumer-goods, subscription, or app), and name the ethical question the loop's design raises.
  • Predict where post-purchase dissonance is most likely based on the three Ps (price, permanence, personal importance) and recommend the post-purchase reassurance moves that would reduce returns and increase advocacy.
  • Identify which layer of the external-influences pyramid — culture, subculture, social class, reference groups, situation — a particular segmentation strategy is operating in, and what the strengths and limits of each layer are as a segmentation basis.
  • Apply this diagnostic in non-marketing contexts: internal-system adoption, change-management, policy uptake, hiring, and any other decision behavior where someone has to recognize a gap, weigh alternatives, and live with the choice.

Key Terms from Unit 4B

Quick reference for the rest of the unit — 4A's vocabulary stays live alongside this

The Decision Process

Consumer decision process — the five-stage spine: problem recognition → information search → alternative evaluation → purchase decision → postpurchase evaluation. A map, not a script.

Internal psychological processes — the five processes running underneath the stages: motivation, perception, attitude formation, integration, learning. Stages are what; processes are how.

Customer journey map — the modern face of the five-stage model: same stages, non-linear loop across fragmented touchpoints, drawn per persona.

Gap, Pain, and Friction

Problem recognition — the felt gap between actual and ideal state that opens every journey.

Pain point — the specific, articulable version of the gap in the consumer's own voice. Sorts to functional, emotional, or social.

Friction & barriers — the cost of action that sits between a recognized pain and resolution. The dark matter of consumer behavior; the modern frontier of marketing investment.

Marketer-induced gap — problem recognition planted by advertising rather than arising from the consumer's world. The legitimate version introduces a category; the illegitimate version manufactures a remedy for a problem that didn't exist.

Motivation and Persona

Maslow's hierarchy — physiological, safety, social, esteem, self-actualization. Vocabulary for motives; durable as a map even where the strict hierarchy doesn't hold.

Persona — a synthesized portrait of a target segment, thick enough that a creative team can write a single sentence about what that consumer wants from the brand. How segmentation gets used.

Multi-motivation positioning — one core brand promise, multiple motivational on-ramps. The on-brand pattern when a brand serves several segments.

Search and Perception

Internal vs. external search — memory scan first; only goes external when internal comes up short. The choice point for whether the journey collapses into habit.

PESO framework — paid, owned, earned, shared touchpoints. The modern reorganization of external search sources, sorted by who controls them and how much trust each carries.

Perception — the process of receiving, selecting, organizing, and interpreting information. Selective and constructive at every stage.

Four perceptual filters — selective exposure, attention, comprehension, retention. Each removes a substantial fraction of what could have reached the consumer.

Feature analysis → active synthesis — the two steps inside comprehension: parse the signal, then combine with context, expectations, prior knowledge, and mood to construct meaning.

Miscomprehension — the 25–33% of any audience that takes away something other than what was sent. Not a fixable bug; the default condition of perception working as designed.

Deciding

Evoked / consideration set — the three-to-seven brands the consumer actively considers. The inept set is brands rejected; the inert set is brands they're aware of but indifferent to.

Evaluative criteria — the attributes that matter to this consumer in this purchase. Sometimes the strongest lever is changing which criteria count.

Decision rules — affect referral (no comparison; emotion or habit), compensatory (weighted average; tradeoffs allowed), and three noncompensatory: conjunctive (clear every minimum), disjunctive (excellence on one carries the day), lexicographic (rank attributes; win on the top).

CPM ↔ HEM continuum — Consumer Processing Model (rational, deliberative, attribute-comparing) at one end; Hedonic Experiential Model (emotional, recognition-driven, identity-fit) at the other. Most purchases sit somewhere between.

60/40 mix — Binet & Field's IPA finding: roughly 60% of marketing investment to brand-building (long-term, HEM-side), 40% to sales activation (short-term, CPM-side).

Attitude structure — three components: cognitive (beliefs), affective (feelings), conative (intent). Order of formation depends on involvement.

Involvement and the hierarchy of effects — high-involvement: think → feel → do; low-involvement: do → feel → think. Trial generates belief in the low-involvement hierarchy.

Behavioral economics biases — durable systematic departures from the deliberative model: loss aversion, defaults / status-quo bias, anchoring, framing, social proof, mental accounting. The peripheral-route persuasion toolkit M3 will unpack.

Learning, Memory, and Habit

Behavioral learning — learning by association: classical conditioning (Pavlov; pairing), operant conditioning (Skinner; reinforcement), vicarious learning (Bandura; observation of others' reinforcement). The mechanism underneath influencer marketing and UGC.

Cognitive learning — learning by understanding; building and reshaping knowledge structures in memory.

Knowledge structure — a network of nodes (brand attributes) and links (associations) the consumer has built around a brand. Individual, graded by strength, editable.

Schema — the category-level pattern-template the consumer holds for a class of things (luxury car, fast-food restaurant, trustworthy advisor). New brands either fit existing schemas or actively reshape them.

Recognition vs. recall — recognition confirms a known brand when prompted; recall retrieves it from a category cue without prompting. Recall is what top-of-mind awareness is, and what wins habitual purchases.

Habit loop — cue → routine → reward. A consistent cue retrieves a routine, the reward reinforces the cue-routine link, and over time the loop collapses the decision journey into automatic behavior. This is brand loyalty in mechanical terms.

Variable-ratio reinforcement — the reinforcement schedule that produces the most stubborn behaviors. The mechanism Skinner identified in pigeons; the mechanism that makes TikTok feeds and slot machines work.

After and Around

Expectancy-disconfirmation — satisfaction is driven by the gap between expectation and experience, not by absolute quality. Overpromising in ads is the most common single cause of dissatisfaction, because pre-purchase expectations are themselves a marketing output.

Cognitive dissonance — the psychological discomfort of misaligned beliefs and actions; post-purchase dissonance (buyer's remorse) is this mechanism running on a fresh purchase. Strongest under the three Ps: price, permanence, personal importance.

Post-purchase reassurance — the deliberate use of thank-you messaging, onboarding content, and community access to reduce dissonance in current buyers — which produces the reviews and UGC that reduce search anxiety in future buyers.

External influences — the inverted pyramid: culture, subculture, social class, reference groups, situation. The master inventory of segmentation bases.

Reference group — any group whose values or behaviors influence the consumer's own. Primary, secondary, aspirational, dissociative. The strongest predictor of purchase in most identity-driven categories.

UGC / peer voice — user-generated content and the broader phenomenon of consumers speaking about brands to other consumers. The earned/shared touchpoints that now dominate pre-purchase confidence.

GEO — Generative Engine Optimization — the emerging practice of influencing how AI assistants summarize a brand to consumers asking for recommendations. The successor concern to SEO; the new mediation layer between the peer voice and the consumer.

Carry these into Unit 4C alongside 4A's communication vocabulary; the toolkit is cumulative.

Where Unit 4C Picks Up

Unit 4C picks up at the move 4B kept naming and deferring: changing the attitude, not just understanding it. The structural piece — cognitive / affective / conative components, the high- and low-involvement hierarchies of effects — was the 4B contribution. 4C unpacks the equation (the Fishbein multi-attribute model that calculates an overall attitude from belief-by-evaluation pairs) and the change strategies (the levers a marketer can pull to shift a consumer's attitude: adding beliefs, changing weights, reframing the comparison). The deeper framework is the Elaboration Likelihood Model, which distinguishes two routes by which persuasion can succeed: the central route (the consumer engages carefully with strong arguments) and the peripheral route (the consumer responds to cues that do not require thinking). Classical conditioning from §6 and behavioral-economics biases from §5 are both peripheral-route mechanisms by other names; 4C names them as such and puts them inside the larger framework. If 4A answered how does meaning land, and 4B answered what does the consumer do with the meaning once it has, 4C answers the third question: how do we change what the consumer does next?
