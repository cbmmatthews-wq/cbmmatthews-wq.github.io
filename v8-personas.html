Step 04 — The honest argument

Are AI personas good enough?

The case for using them as starting points — and where they break down.

"Many people online swear 'just use AI. Don't waste time making personas the traditional way. Who cares if they are a little more generic or stereotypical.' What would you say to that?"

The "just use AI" crowd has a point — but they're solving the wrong problem.

AI-generated personas are pattern-matching exercises. They synthesize what tends to be true about a demographic based on training data. That's genuinely useful for getting something on the board fast, for alignment conversations, or for gut-checking whether your team is even thinking about the same kind of person. The speed argument is real.

But the criticism lands hard in one specific place: the insight that drives a good creative or strategic decision almost never comes from what's typical. It comes from the tension, the contradiction, the thing that's slightly off. Why does someone who reads labels still buy a product with "energy" in the name? What's the specific moment of weakness or permission that makes them reach for it? Those details don't live in training data — they live in interviews, in ethnography, in watching someone pause in a grocery aisle.

AI personas are also self-confirming. The persona is coherent because it was built to be coherent. A real research process would surface people who don't fit the pattern, and those outliers are often where the most interesting strategy lives.

The honest version of the advice: use AI to get to a first draft in ten minutes, then pressure-test it against real humans before you act on it.

Step 06 — The brief I needed

What would have made these actionable

For personas to drive content strategy and be measurable, the inputs matter as much as the outputs.

"What information should I have provided to create more accurate personas — ones we could use for creative/content and test against real analytics?"

What you could bring directly to the AI

Data you already own

Existing customer data

Purchase frequency, basket composition, channel source. Even aggregated data replaces demographic guesswork with behavioral characterization.

Voice of customer

Reviews, verbatims, comments

The language real people use to describe why they buy is irreplaceable. Raw text — social comments, survey responses, support tickets — can be processed for themes and tensions.

Strategic context

Competitive frame

Are you conquesting Red Bull drinkers? Retaining people who might trade up to Celsius? The strategic frame changes who the relevant personas even are.

Marketing goal

The specific brief

"Create content," "defend shelf space," and "launch in a new region" are three different briefs — and they'd produce three different personas even from identical underlying data.

What's worth gathering before acting on any persona

Primary research

5–6 qualitative interviews

Gives you the actual language, the actual moment of purchase, the actual internal monologue. The source of real tension and nuance.

Quantitative if budget allows

Segmentation data

A cluster analysis or conjoint study tells you whether there are actually three meaningfully different buyer types — or two, or five — rather than an invented number.

Channel behavior

Retail & purchase context

A gas station impulse buyer is behaviorally different from someone who adds V8+Energy to a weekly grocery order, even if they look identical demographically.

The most skipped step

What you can actually do differently

For each segment: a distinct channel, a distinct message hypothesis, and a distinct success metric. Without these three, you have descriptions. With them, you have a testable experiment.

The three things each persona needs to close the analytics loop

Reach

A distinct channel or placement where you can actually reach this segment separately — paid social, in-store, email, etc.

Message hypothesis

A specific creative or copy angle you're testing against this persona — not a generic brand message, but a testable bet.

Success metric

A distinct KPI that would tell you it worked — click-through, conversion, recall, repeat purchase. Different for each persona.
